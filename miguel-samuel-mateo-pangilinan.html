4. With illustrations from Caribbean texts below, discuss the writers use of Creole in : a) In the Castle of My Skin by George Lamming b) Homestretch by Velma Pollard c) Miguel Street by V.S Naipaul d) A Brighter Sun by Samuel Selvon. ​


Answer:

a) In the Castle of My Skin by George Lamming

In the Castle of My Skin is a novel that explores the experiences of a young boy growing up in colonial Barbados. The writer, George Lamming, uses Creole throughout the novel to give voice to the characters and to create a sense of authenticity and intimacy.

One example of this is in the opening lines of the novel, where Lamming writes, "I was born without a name". This use of Creole immediately establishes a sense of place and culture, and highlights the importance of naming in Caribbean society. Lamming continues to use Creole throughout the novel, particularly in the dialogue between the characters.

For example, in this passage, the character Mr Slime speaks to the protagonist, G., in Creole:

" 'G., you goin' to school?' Mr Slime asked.

'Yes, Sir,' I said.

'You better be careful. They teachin' you to read and write so you could sign your name to a cross.' "

This use of Creole not only gives the character a distinct voice, but also highlights the power dynamics between the white colonial master and the black Caribbean subject.

b) Homestretch by Velma Pollard

Homestretch is a collection of short stories by Velma Pollard that explores the lives of Jamaican women. Pollard uses Creole in her writing to capture the rhythms and cadences of Jamaican speech, and to give voice to her characters.

One example of this is in the story "The Last Day of School", where the character Miss Lally speaks to her students in Creole:

" 'Unuh listen to me now, unuh hear? Today is the last day of school, and unuh better behave unuhself. No noise, no running up and down, and no fighting. Unuh hear?'

'Yes, Miss Lally,' the children chorused."

This use of Creole not only creates a sense of authenticity and intimacy, but also highlights the cultural and linguistic diversity of the Caribbean.

c) Miguel Street by V.S Naipaul

Miguel Street is a collection of short stories by V.S Naipaul that explores life in Port of Spain, Trinidad. Naipaul uses Creole in his writing to capture the rhythms and cadences of Trinidadian speech, and to give voice to his characters.

One example of this is in the story "Bogart", where the character Hat speaks to his friends in Creole:

" 'All you fellas know that I is a man who like to live in style. And I have to tell you that I have been living in style for the past week.'

'What style?' we asked.

'Well, boy, I have been eating butter for breakfast.' "

This use of Creole not only creates a sense of authenticity and intimacy, but also highlights the humor and wit of Trinidadian culture.

d) A Brighter Sun by Samuel Selvon

A Brighter Sun is a novel by Samuel Selvon that explores the experiences of East Indian immigrants in Trinidad. Selvon uses Creole in his writing to capture the rhythms and cadences of Trinidadian speech, and to give voice to his characters.

One example of this is in the opening lines of the novel, where Selvon writes, "The sun was bright, brighter than any day before it." This use of Creole not only creates a sense of place and culture, but also establishes a poetic and lyrical tone.

Selvon continues to use Creole throughout the novel, particularly in the dialogue between the characters. For example, in this passage, the character Tiger speaks to his wife in Creole:

" 'You know, Rosie, I was thinking. It's about time we get some furniture in this house. We can't go on living like this.'

'What you want me to do, Tiger? You know we ain't got no money.'

'I know, but we could save a little bit each week. Maybe we could get a table and some chairs.' "

This use of Creole not only gives the character a distinct voice, but also highlights the economic struggles of East Indian immigrants in Trinidad.
