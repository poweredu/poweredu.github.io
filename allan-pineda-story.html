6. what is the structure of the story of tell tale heart by edgar allan poe


Tell-Tale Heart was a short story written by Edgar Allan Poe. It tells the story of the narrator who just murdered his old man because of the fact that the old man possesses the ''vulture eye''.

The story follows a traditional structure.

It starts with an exposition, the narrator informs the readers that he and his old man live together inside an apartment. He also mentions that he is quite annoyed with the eye of the old man and even calls it ''evil''. He also explicitly mentions on how he wants to murder the old man so he could ''free'' himself. The narrator also insist that he is not insane.

Next, is the rising action, which was him planning to do the murder but failed to do so, it was when he attempted to murder the old man in his room for the span of seven nights, in which he failed because he believed that the old man was watching him.

His act of committing the murder was the climax of the story, it was at its peak. He fulfilled his purpose and goal, which was to get rid of the old man and to free himself from the eye. At this point of the story, he felt victorious, triumphant and he refers himself as to wise

After the climax comes the falling action of the story and the falling action of the story was when he deliberately hides the evidence by chopping up the body parts of the old man and hiding them under the floorboard. Then, policemen came to check on his house, because of his neighbor's complaint regarding the noise. The narrator invited the cops inside his house with utmost hospitality to avoid suspicion. He offered them food, drinks, and a chair to relax. He got a chair for himself and purposely placed it directly above the floorboard the hid the body parts in. It was then that he started overthinking and guilt was eating up on him, till the point that he mentally broke down and confessed to the police about his crime.

The story ended with him becoming completely insane.

8. what is the body of tell-tale heart short story by edgar allan poe?​


Answer:

"The Tell-Tale Heart" is a short story by Edgar Allan Poe, first published in 1843. The story is narrated in the first-person point of view by an unnamed narrator who endeavors to convince the reader of his sanity, while describing a murder he committed. The plot of the story revolves around the narrator's obsession with an old man's "vulture eye" and his subsequent murder of the old man. The narrator dismembers the body and hides it under the floorboards, but he is eventually driven to confess his crime due to his overwhelming guilt and paranoia.

The body of "The Tell-Tale Heart" explores themes such as guilt, madness, and the consequences of one's actions. The narrator's descent into madness and his inability to control his actions highlight the destructive power of guilt and obsession. Additionally, the story raises questions about the reliability of the narrator's account and the nature of truth.

Overall, "The Tell-Tale Heart" is a chilling tale that delves into the darkest corners of the human psyche, leaving readers questioning their own perceptions of reality.

21. using narrative pattern make an essay about the story of the tale tell hearts by edar allan poe​


Answer:

Once upon a time, in a dark and gloomy house, there lived an old man with a pale blue eye. The narrator of this story, whose name is never revealed, had become obsessed with the old man's eye. The eye had a filmy, vulture-like appearance that made the narrator feel uneasy and disturbed.

The narrator's obsession grew to the point where he could no longer tolerate the sight of the old man's eye. He became convinced that the eye was watching him, even when it was closed. The narrator's irrational fear of the eye consumed him, and he decided to take drastic action.

One night, the narrator crept into the old man's room and stood there in the darkness, listening to the old man's heartbeat. The sound of the heartbeat became louder and louder, until the narrator could no longer bear it. In a fit of madness, he lunged at the old man and smothered him with a pillow.

The narrator then dismembered the old man's body and hid it under the floorboards of his room. Despite his meticulous planning, the narrator soon began to hear the sound of the old man's heartbeat echoing in his mind, haunting him day and night.

The narrator's guilt and paranoia consumed him, and he eventually confessed to the murder to the police. As he led them to the room where he had hidden the old man's body, the sound of the heartbeat grew louder and louder until the narrator could no longer bear it. He confessed to the crime and was arrested by the police.

The tale of the "Tell-Tale Heart" is a chilling example of the effects of guilt and obsession. The narrator's fixation on the old man's eye led him down a dark path of madness and murder, ultimately leading to his own downfall. The story is a haunting reminder of the power of the human mind and the danger of unchecked obsession.

27. read Edgar Allan Poe's the Fall of the house of usher and examine how the writer established the setting used in the story​


Answer:

In Edgar Allan Poe's "The Fall of the House of Usher," the setting is established as a gloomy and eerie mansion, located in a remote and desolate area. The house is described as "anciently imposing," with "gables of a singular character" and "long, dark windows." The atmosphere of the house is described as "an air of stern, deep, and irredeemable gloom" that "hung over and pervaded all." The house is also described as "a melancholy house upon a dreary plain," which emphasizes the eerie and desolate nature of the setting.

The writer also uses descriptive language to create a sense of foreboding and unease. The house is described as being "decay," with "shattered roof" and "dilapidated walls." The house's architecture is also described as being "gothic" and "fantastic," which adds to the eerie and unsettling atmosphere. The setting is also described as being "in the midst of a singularly dreary tract of country," which emphasizes the isolation of the house and adds to the sense of unease.

The writer also uses imagery and symbolism to establish the setting. The house is described as being "a human eye, with a film over it," which symbolizes the decay and corruption of the house and its inhabitants. The house is also described as "a giant toad" which symbolizes the evil and corrupt nature of the house. The house is also described as being "a giant skull" which symbolizes the death and destruction that will soon befall the house.

Explanation:

In summary, Edgar Allan Poe establishes the setting of "The Fall of the House of Usher" as a gloomy and eerie mansion located in a remote and desolate area. He uses descriptive language, imagery, and symbolism to create a sense of foreboding and unease, which adds to the eerie and unsettling atmosphere of the story.
