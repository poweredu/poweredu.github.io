2. Make a critical analysis is Martines by Anna Felicia Sanchez using Affectivism Literary Theory.


Literary Analysis on Martinés: Affective Literary Theory

It is requisite to be educated with a line of argument or literary analysis style that enables readers to criticize the underlying principles of literary works in order to assess the literature (MasterClass 1). Thus, this is associated with literary theory, a form of systematically based and not easily refutable insights provided in a logical sequence that we can utilize to critically interpret a literary text (Alok Mishra 3).

To this degree, the literature subjected for analysis is the story entitled Martines by Anna Felicia Sanchez using Affectivism Literary Theory which engages emotional aspects from the readers towards the text in the form of words. The emphasis on the socio-cultural context particular to people in the province is reflected in Martinés that tend to fulfill the atmosphere of a short horror story— intended to deliver fright, scare, and disgust to the readers, but hold a lesson that is relevant even to today’s generation at the same time.  

The reader sensed chills to the underlying nature of the plot since the commencement of the story when Richard’s father had told him about the secret to immortality (Dolino). To provide evidence from another reader, as stated by Roro But Lucha “Jodel”, “The short horror story started with an anecdote of Richard Servacio's father telling a myth of a secret to immortality”. in accordance with  “It was said that the oldest among them held the secret to immortality, and that it was this secret that kept the land from drying, from crumbling under the weather of a world that had lost its way along its own axis. With this knowledge, the rest of the land could be saved from drowning in the color of blood, and there would be no need for the shadows of things long gone.” (Sanchez 1). It’s not only in the first paragraph that made the story horror for the reader, the sequence has led its end to a twist of violence and supernatural events (Dolino). The moment where Richard has been chained and Totoy had already abandoned him be for birds to prey on (Sanchez 10) proved terrifying (Medina 1).  

Furthermore, the story Martinés emotionally connects with the reader by means of sympathy. Conforming to the fourth paragraph of the literary piece, Richard negatively responded to the misfortune of his career (Sanchez 1). He became a depressed alcoholic, linking to the sentimental unease of the reader (Dolino). Also, beyond the story flow, it is a grim fact that, as the years progress, the virtues of our culture are gradually becoming overlooked and forgotten by the newer population (Borlasa). The reader has swum into the depth the scope of the lesson behind Martinés could reach for and relates with the sad reality that generally concludes how proceeding generations handle culture. The existence of the event that triggered sacrifices and offerings for the Martinés should have been avoided and the town would not experience promoting this belief if the secret to immortality— as stated in the story (Sanchez 1)— was treasured and never once forgotten in the first place. This leads to the irony of the main character in the folklore; despite the fact that "Martines" was his homeland, he had no recollection of his origins (Borlasa 6).

WHAT IS CAUSE AND EFFECT?

Cause and effect is the relationship between two things when one thing makes something else happen. For example, if we eat too much food and do not exercise, we gain weight. Eating food without exercising is the “cause;” weight gain is the “effect.” There may be multiple causes and multiple effects.In essence, cause is the thing that makes other things happen. Effect refers to what results. It is the what happened next in the text that results from a preceding cause. To put it concisely, cause is the why something happened and effect is the what happened.

As we have already heard, Heidegger's analysis of technology in The Question Concerning Technology consists of three main 'claims': (1) technology is "not an instrument," it is a way of understanding the world; (2) technology is "not a human activity," but it develops beyond human control; and (3) technology is "the greatest danger," risking us only seeing the world through technological thinking.
