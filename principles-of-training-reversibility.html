1. Which of the following states that adaptations that take place as a result of training are reversible? a. Principle of Progression b. Principle of Reversibility c. Principle of Individuality d. Principle of Overload


Principle of Reversibility  

The correct answer is B. Principle of Reversibility states that an athlete loses the effect of training as they cease to perform relevant training activities. In simple terms, when somebody regularly works out and ceases for a period of time, the body will revert to its state before the training began. As such, the Principle of Reversibility is also known as detraining. However, when a person goes back to working out, the effects of detraining can be reversed. https://brainly.ph/question/2327978

Reversibility happens in a short time after training stops. Training loss may occur differently to each person as every 'body' is unique. Usually, it affects muscular endurance more than the strength of the athlete undergoing this process.  

Why does a person go through 'detraining'?

There are several reasons why a person undergoes detraining or stop working out:

Injury Lack of motivation Illness Failure Costs Environment Shift in career Aging What is the importance of Detraining?  

As unpleasant as it sounds, the principle of reversibility is used by many successful athletes to get better in their shape and skills. For example, a weightlifter might have already reached his maximum potential and would like to go further. He will then rest for a while and let his body be inactive. After a short while, he then goes back to training and surpasses his past level.

5. Directions. Provide an example and conclusion for each principle of exercise trainingPRINCIPLEEXAMPLECONCLUSIONIndividualitySpecificityOverloadProgressiveReversibility​yan Po answer


PRINCIPLE: Individuality

EXAMPLE: Two people have different physical fitness levels and goals. Person A wants to increase their endurance, while Person B wants to build muscle. They both need different exercise programs based on their individual needs.

CONCLUSION: Exercise programs should be tailored to an individual's physical fitness level, goals, and limitations to achieve the desired results.

PRINCIPLE: Specificity

EXAMPLE: A basketball player focuses on improving their jumping ability by doing plyometric exercises and practicing jumping techniques.

CONCLUSION: To improve a specific physical skill, the exercise program should be designed to target the specific muscle groups and movements required for that skill.

PRINCIPLE: Overload

EXAMPLE: A weightlifter progressively increases the amount of weight they lift in their training sessions to challenge their muscles and continue to make gains.

CONCLUSION: To make progress and improve physical fitness, the body needs to be challenged beyond its current capacity, which can be achieved by increasing the intensity, duration, or frequency of exercise.

PRINCIPLE: Progressive

EXAMPLE: A runner increases the distance they run each week to build their endurance gradually.

CONCLUSION: Exercise programs should be designed to gradually increase the intensity, duration, or frequency of exercise to avoid injury and improve fitness over time.

PRINCIPLE: Reversibility

EXAMPLE: A person who stops exercising for a long period will lose muscle mass and cardiovascular fitness.

CONCLUSION: The benefits of exercise are not permanent, and physical fitness can be lost if exercise is not maintained regularly.
