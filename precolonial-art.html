20. literary tradition of precolonial​


Answer:

The literary tradition of precolonial societies refers to the rich cultural and artistic heritage of various societies around the world before the onset of colonization. These societies had well-established systems of oral literature, which encompassed myths, legends, proverbs, riddles, folktales, and songs. These oral traditions were passed down from generation to generation through storytelling and performances, and they played a vital role in preserving the cultural identity and history of these societies.

One example of precolonial oral literature can be found in the indigenous cultures of the Americas, such as the Inca, Maya, and Aztec civilizations, which had their own distinct literary traditions. For instance, the Inca civilization had a complex system of quipus, or knotted cords, which were used to record information and stories.

In Africa, precolonial literature included a wide range of oral traditions, including epics, praise poems, and religious and spiritual writings. These traditions were particularly well developed in societies such as the Yoruba of Nigeria, the Zulu of South Africa, and the Hausa of West Africa.

In Asia, precolonial literature encompassed a wide range of traditions, including Hindu epics such as the Ramayana and the Mahabharata in India, and Chinese classics such as the Book of Songs and the Analects of Confucius.

Overall, precolonial literature was a vital and dynamic part of the cultural heritage of many societies around the world, and it continues to be an important source of inspiration and creativity in contemporary times.

25. woman and man in precolonial times?​


Explanation:

In pre-colonial times, among many ethnic groups, custom law gave women equal rights with men. They could own and inherit property, engage in trade and industry, and succeed to chieftainship in the absence of a male heir. They had exclusive right to educate and to name their children. They were also the money keepers. During the Spanish times a woman continued the use of her maiden name after marriage, or else merely appended her husband's surname to her own, and the children assumed the hyphenated surname. Her husband may have built their house, the symbol of their conjugal state, but she was the maybahay, literally the owner of the house.

29. Is it important to preserve the precolonial hagabi?​


Answer:

Hagabi are traditionally large ceremonial benches carved for wealthy Ifugao families as a symbol of wealth, power and prestige. The bench serves as a symbol of a prestigious "hagabi feast" in which the carving of the bench marks the beginning of the celebration.
