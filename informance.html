26. what is the definition of primary information, secondary information, tertiary information​


Answer:

primary information - Primary information comes straight from the original source, in other words it is made up of original documents and raw data. It can be in any format, including written, visual, electronic or oral. It remains unchanged, for example autobiographies and diaries.

secondary information- Secondary sources describe, summarize, or discuss information or details originally presented in another source;

tertiary information- A tertiary source is an index or textual consolidation of primary and secondary sources. Some tertiary sources can be used as an aid to find other sources. The exact definition of tertiary varies by academic field. Academic research standards generally do not accept tertiary sources as citations.

30. What are the differences of printed information, online information and broadcasted information​


Answer:

Printed information refers to information that is physically printed on a medium such as paper, such as in a book or newspaper. Online information refers to information that is available on the internet and accessed through a device such as a computer or smartphone. Broadcasted information refers to information that is transmitted through various mediums such as radio, television, or podcasts.

One major difference between these types of information is their accessibility. Printed information is often more difficult to access, as it requires physically obtaining the printed material. Online information is more readily accessible, as it can be accessed from any device with an internet connection. Broadcasted information is also relatively easy to access, as it can be received through a variety of devices such as radios or televisions.

Another difference between these types of information is their permanence. Printed information is often more permanent, as it is physically printed and can be stored for long periods of time. Online information can also be stored and accessed for long periods of time, but it is also subject to changes and updates. Broadcasted information is typically ephemeral, as it is transmitted in real time and is not typically stored for future access.

Overall, printed, online, and broadcasted information all have unique characteristics and offer different benefits and drawbacks. It's important to consider the strengths and limitations of each type of information when seeking and evaluating sources of information.
