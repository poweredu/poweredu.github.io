Answer:

In William Shakespeare's play "The Merchant of Venice," the characters Bassanio, Morocco, and Aragon are all suitors vying for the hand of Portia. Each suitor must choose one of three caskets - one made of gold, one of silver, and one of lead - to determine whether they will be allowed to marry her.

Bassanio's logic in choosing the lead casket is different from that of Morocco and Aragon. While Morocco and Aragon both choose based on external appearances, Bassanio chooses based on a deeper understanding of Portia's character and virtues.

Morocco is drawn to the gold casket because of its outward beauty and the inscription on it that reads, "Who chooseth me shall gain what many men desire." He sees this as a reflection of Portia's beauty and assumes that the casket contains her hand in marriage. Aragon, on the other hand, chooses the silver casket, which is adorned with various symbols and inscribed with the words, "Who chooseth me shall get as much as he deserves." He believes that he deserves Portia due to his own nobility and sees the casket as a reflection of his own worthiness.

Bassanio, however, recognizes that the true value of a person lies within, and not in their external appearance or wealth. He chooses the lead casket, which has the least outwardly attractive appearance and the inscription, "Who chooseth me shall gain what many men desire." Rather than being swayed by appearances or symbols, Bassanio understands that Portia's true worth lies in her character and virtues, and he trusts that his choice will reveal her true nature.

Overall, while Morocco and Aragon base their choices on superficial qualities, Bassanio's logic reflects a deeper understanding and appreciation of Portia's true worth.
