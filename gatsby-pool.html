9. what is the cause and effect of the novel great gatsby?​


"The Great Gatsby" is a novel written by F. Scott Fitzgerald that was first published in 1925. The novel explores themes such as the American Dream, wealth, love, and the corrupting influence of power.

One of the main causes in the novel is the pursuit of the American Dream, which drives many of the characters' actions. For example, Jay Gatsby becomes wealthy through illegal means in order to win back his lost love, Daisy Buchanan, and live the luxurious life he believes she desires. Additionally, the rampant consumerism and materialism of the 1920s create a desire for wealth and status that motivates characters like Tom and Daisy Buchanan to engage in extramarital affairs and other immoral actions.

The effects of these actions are complex and varied. One major effect is the corruption of the characters, as they become increasingly willing to betray others and engage in morally dubious behavior in order to achieve their goals. For example, Gatsby's pursuit of Daisy ultimately leads to his downfall, as his illegal activities and involvement with criminal elements catch up with him. Similarly, the Buchanans' affairs and recklessness lead to tragedy and heartbreak for themselves and others.

Overall, "The Great Gatsby" portrays the dangers of unchecked ambition and the moral decay that can result from the pursuit of wealth and power.

18. in the great gatsby, how does the text depict struggles between the classes?​


Answer:

The Great Gatsby depicts struggles between the classes through the contrast between the wealthy, elite class and the less affluent middle class. The characters' social status is implied by what they wear, where they live, and how they behave. The novel suggests that social mobility is difficult, if not impossible, and that people are largely defined by their socioeconomic status. 24. In the novel Great Gatsby, how the social classes affect the conflict?​


Answer:

In "The Great Gatsby" by F. Scott Fitzgerald, social class plays a significant role in the conflicts that arise between the characters. The novel portrays the social hierarchy of the 1920s in America, where the wealthy and powerful hold most of the privileges and opportunities, while those in lower classes struggle to make ends meet.

Jay Gatsby, the protagonist, is an example of someone who is not born into the upper class but strives to become part of it. He accumulates his wealth through illegal means, such as bootlegging, in order to impress Daisy Buchanan, his love interest, who belongs to the wealthy and elite class. The conflict arises when Gatsby's desire to win Daisy's love and acceptance is thwarted by the societal norms and expectations that come with her social status.

Tom Buchanan, Daisy's husband, represents the old money and social elite, and his arrogance and sense of entitlement create a contrast with Gatsby's humble origins and aspirations. The tension between Gatsby and Tom, who represent different social classes, escalates into a conflict over Daisy's affection and loyalty.

The novel also highlights the emptiness and corruption that often accompany wealth and power. The wealthy characters in the novel, such as Tom and Daisy, are portrayed as shallow, selfish, and morally bankrupt, while the poorer characters, such as George Wilson and Myrtle Wilson, are depicted as being more genuine and sympathetic.

In summary, the social classes in "The Great Gatsby" play a major role in the conflicts between the characters, highlighting the tension and disparity that existed during the 1920s in America.
