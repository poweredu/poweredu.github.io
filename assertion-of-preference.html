1. explain what is assertion of fact, assertion of convention, assertion of opinion,and assertion of preference ​


Answer:

Assertion of Fact: An assertion of fact is a statement that can be proven to be true or false. It is based on evidence and can be verified. Examples include: The sky is blue, Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius.

Assertion of Convention: An assertion of convention is a statement that reflects the accepted behavior or practice in a particular society or culture. Examples include: It is polite to say please and thank you, In the United States, people drive on the right side of the road.

Assertion of Opinion: An assertion of opinion is a statement that expresses an individual's beliefs or feelings about something. It cannot be proven to be true or false and may vary from person to person. Examples include: Chocolate ice cream is the best flavor, I think rap music is underrated.

Assertion of Preference: An assertion of preference is a statement that expresses an individual's preference for something over another option. It cannot be proven to be true or false and may vary from person to person. Examples include: I prefer coffee over tea, I like apples more than oranges.

13. what are the differences between assertion of fact,convention,preference and opinion​


The differences between assertion of fact, convention, preference, and opinion lie in their objectivity, acceptance, personal preference, and subjectivity, respectively.

Explanation:

Assertion of fact: An assertion of fact is a claim that, in light of available information or evidence, is judged to be either objectively true or incorrect. For instance, the statement "Paris is the capital of France." This claim can be supported by factual evidence found in reference materials like books, maps, and official government websites. Convention: Within a given civilization or culture, a convention is a custom or habit that is commonly accepted and practiced. Conventions can change between countries and eras and are frequently based on custom or social norms. For instance, it is customary in many Western countries to shake hands as a greeting.Preference: A preference is a choice or personal preference that is not always supported by factual information or proof. Individual differences in preferences might result from things like past experiences, culture, and upbringing. For instance, a person may prefer vanilla ice cream over chocolate ice cream, yet this preference is neither objectively true nor incorrect.Opinion: An opinion is a viewpoint or assessment on a certain subject or problem. Opinions can be influenced by subjective elements like personal values, emotions, and biases and aren't always backed up by hard facts or data. For instance, a person may believe that a particular political policy is good or bad, but this belief is not based on fact.

In conclusion, the fundamental distinctions between a assertion of fact, a convention, a preference, and an opinion are found in the objectivity of the statement or belief and the extent to which it is supported by factual data or personal choice.
