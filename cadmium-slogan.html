14. A 98.0°C piece of cadmium (c=.850J/g°C) is placed in 150.0g of 37.0°C water. After sitting for a few minutes, both have a temperature of 38.6°C. What was the mass of the cadmium sample?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the heat gained by water.

[tex]\begin{aligned} q_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} & = m_{\text{H}_2\text{O}}c(T_{f} - T_{i}) \\ & = (\text{150.0 g})(\text{4.184 J/g}\cdot ^{\circ}\text{C})(38.6^{\circ}\text{C} - 37.0^{\circ}\text{C}) \\ & = \text{1,004.16 J} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Determine the heat lost by cadmium.

[tex]\begin{aligned} q_{\text{Cd}} & = -q_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \\ & = -\text{1,004.16 J} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the mass of cadmium.

[tex]\begin{aligned} m_{\text{Cd}} & = \frac{q_{\text{Cd}}}{c(T_{f} - T_{i})} \\ & = \frac{-\text{1,004.16 J}}{(\text{0.850 J/g}\cdot ^{\circ}\text{C})(38.6^{\circ}\text{C} - 98.0^{\circ}\text{C})} \\ & = \boxed{\text{19.89 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the mass of cadmium sample is 19.89 g.

The general form of the Nernst equation is:

Ecell = Eo cell - (RT/nF) * ln Q

Where Ecell is the cell potential at non-standard conditions, Eo cell is the standard cell potential, R is the gas constant, T is the temperature in kelvins, n is the number of moles of electrons involved in the reaction, F is the Faraday constant, and Q is the reaction quotient.

To solve for the concentration of NiCl2 that you should use, you can rearrange the equation to solve for the concentration of NiCl2, which would be represented by the symbol "c" in the equation. You would then plug in the known values for all of the other variables, including the cell potential that you want to achieve (0.17 V), the standard cell potential for the nickel-cadmium system, the temperature, the number of moles of electrons involved in the reaction, and the reaction quotient.

It's important to note that the Nernst equation is only accurate at non-standard conditions, so you may need to make some adjustments to the equation if you are working under standard conditions. Additionally, the standard cell potential for the nickel-cadmium system will depend on the specific reaction that you are trying to carry out.
