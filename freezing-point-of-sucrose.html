3. find freezing point (If) and boiling point (Tb) of a solution containinh 4.0gm sucrose (C12H22O11) dissolved in 200 gm water.​


the freezing point of the solution is -0.1085 °C and the boiling point of the solution is 100.0299 °C.

Answer:

To find the freezing point and boiling point of a solution, we need to use the following equations:

ΔTf = Kf x m x i

ΔTb = Kb x m x i

Where:

ΔTf = the change in freezing point

ΔTb = the change in boiling point

Kf = the molal freezing point depression constant for water (1.86 °C/m)

Kb = the molal boiling point elevation constant for water (0.512 °C/m)

m = molality of the solution (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent)

i = van't Hoff factor (the number of ions or particles that the solute dissociates into)

First, we need to find the molality of the solution:

m = (moles of solute) / (mass of solvent in kg)

The molar mass of sucrose (C12H22O11) is 342 g/mol, so 4.0 g of sucrose is equal to:

(4.0 g) / (342 g/mol) = 0.0117 mol

The mass of water in the solution is 200 g or 0.2 kg.

So, the molality of the solution is:

m = (0.0117 mol) / (0.2 kg) = 0.0585 mol/kg

Next, we need to find the van't Hoff factor (i) for sucrose. Sucrose does not dissociate into ions in water, so i = 1.

Using the equations above, we can calculate the freezing point depression and boiling point elevation:

ΔTf = Kf x m x i = 1.86 °C/m x 0.0585 mol/kg x 1 = 0.1089 °C

ΔTb = Kb x m x i = 0.512 °C/m x 0.0585 mol/kg x 1 = 0.0300 °C

Finally, we can find the freezing point and boiling point of the solution:

If = 0°C - ΔTf = 0°C - 0.1089 °C = -0.1089 °C

Tb = 100°C + ΔTb = 100°C + 0.0300 °C = 100.0300 °C

Therefore, the freezing point of the solution is -0.1089 °C, and the boiling point of the solution is 100.0300 °C.

Solution properties are the properties of a solution that don't depend on the type of solute but only on the concentration of the solute.

Solution properties of electrolyte solutions differ from non-electrolyte solutions because electrolyte solutions contain a greater number of particles because electrolytes break down into ions. So the Solution properties of electrolytes are greater than non-electrolytes.

The term is used in the Solution properties

1. molal

that is, the number of moles of solute in 1 kg of solvent

[tex]\large {\boxed {\bold {m = mole. \dfrac {1000} {mass \: of \: solvent (in \: grams)}}}[/tex]

2. freezing point

Solutions from volatile substances have a higher boiling point and lower freezing points than the solvent

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = T_fsolvent-T_fsolution[/tex]

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = K_f \times m[/tex]

Kf = molal freezing point constant

m = molal solution

From the solutions below

a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl

b. 200 mL of 0.10 m CaCl₂

c. 500 mL of 0.10 m sucrose

d. 100 mL of 0.20 m sucrose

which includes electrolyte solutions are NaCl and CaCl₂, while non-electrolytes are sucrose

For electrolyte solutions there is a van't Hoff factor = i

i = 1 + (n-1) α

n = number of ions from the electrolyte

so the freezing point formula becomes:

[tex]\rm \Delta T_f = K_f \times m \times i[/tex]

Because of the value of i (a Van't Hoff factor), this electrolyte solution produces a freezing point that is greater than the non-electrolyte solution.

So the choice of answers leads to the higher ΔTf which is the electrolyte solution, here is NaCl and CaCl₂

From the freezing point formula, it can be seen that  ΔTf depends on the values ​​of Kf and m (molal)

So that which has the highest ΔTf (=the lowest freezing point) is a. 100 mL of 0.5 m NaCl, because it has a molal higher than option B

12. Problem Solving: 1. A solution is made of 2.00 g of sucrose, C12H22O11, in water, H2O. The molar mass (MM) of sucrose is 342 g/mol. Calculate the (a) boiling point and (b) freezing point of the solution. Kf of water is 1.862. A certain nonvolatile hydrocarbon that weighed 1.00 g was dissolved in 50.00 g of benzene. The resulting solution was boiled at 0.285 °C higher than the boiling point of pure benzene which boils at 80.1°C. Find the molar mass of the hydrocarbon. Kb is 0.52​


Answer:

1. (a) To calculate the boiling point of the solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = Kb x molality

where ΔTb is the change in boiling point, Kb is the boiling point elevation constant for water (0.512 °C/m), and molality is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

First, we need to calculate the number of moles of sucrose in the solution:

n = mass / MM

n = 2.00 g / 342 g/mol

n = 0.00585 mol

Next, we need to calculate the mass of water in the solution:

mass of water = total mass - mass of sucrose

mass of water = 2.00 g + (1000 g/kg) - 2.00 g

mass of water = 998 g

Now, we can calculate the molality:

molality = n / mass of solvent (in kg)

molality = 0.00585 mol / 0.998 kg

molality = 0.00585 mol/kg

Finally, we can calculate the change in boiling point:

ΔTb = Kb x molality

ΔTb = 0.512 °C/m x 0.00585 mol/kg

ΔTb = 0.00299 °C

The boiling point of pure water is 100.0 °C, so the boiling point of the solution is:

boiling point = 100.0 °C + ΔTb

boiling point = 100.0 °C + 0.00299 °C

boiling point = 100.003 °C

(b) To calculate the freezing point of the solution, we can use the equation:

ΔTf = Kf x molality

where ΔTf is the change in freezing point, Kf is the freezing point depression constant for water (1.86 °C/m), and molality is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

Using the same values for n, mass of water, and molality as in part (a), we can calculate the change in freezing point:

ΔTf = Kf x molality

ΔTf = 1.86 °C/m x 0.00585 mol/kg

ΔTf = 0.0109 °C

The freezing point of pure water is 0.0 °C, so the freezing point of the solution is:

freezing point = 0.0 °C - ΔTf

freezing point = 0.0 °C - 0.0109 °C

freezing point = -0.0109 °C

Therefore, the boiling point of the solution is 100.003 °C and the freezing point of the solution is -0.0109 °C.

Explanation:

2. To calculate the molar mass of the hydrocarbon, we can use the equation:

ΔTb = Kb x molality

where ΔTb is the change in boiling point, Kb is the boiling point elevation constant for benzene (2.53 °C/m), and molality is the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

First, we need to calculate the molality of the solution:

molality = n / mass of solvent (in kg)

where n is the number of moles of solute (which is equal to the number of moles of the hydrocarbon) and the mass of solvent is 50.00 g of benzene, which is the same as 0.05000 kg.

molality = n / mass of solvent

molality = n / 0.05000 kg

Next, we need to calculate the change in boiling point:

ΔTb = Kb x molality

ΔTb = 2.53 °C/m x (n / 0.05000 kg)

We know that the solution boiled at 0.285 °C higher than the boiling point of pure benzene, so we can set up the following equation:

0.285 °C = 2.53 °C/m x (n / 0.05000 kg)

Solving for n:

n = (0.285 °C x 0.05000 kg) / (2.53 °C/m)

n = 0.00568 mol

Finally, we can calculate the molar mass of the hydrocarbon:

molar mass = mass / n

where the mass is 1.00 g and n is 0.00568 mol.

molar mass = 1.00 g / 0.00568 mol

molar mass = 176 g/mol

Therefore, the molar mass of the hydrocarbon is 176 g/mol.

15. A solution is made of 2.00 g of sucrose, C12H22O11 in 100.00 g of water, H2O. The molar mass (MM) of sucrose is 342 g/mol. Calculate (a) boiling point and (b) freezing point of the solution.


Answer:

HELLO OPPAIMON

This question is based on the colligative properties of a solution where the solute will cause an increase in the boiling point and a decrease in the freezing point of the pure solvent. For these calculations, we need to use a concentration unit called molality, which is a ratio of the moles of solute per kg of solvent.

In the above question, sucrose is your solute while water is the solvent.

First we need to calculate the # of moles of sucrose using its molar mass.

2.00 g sucrose x 1 mol sucrose / 342 g sucrose = 0.00585 mol sucrose

Now we can calculate the molality (m) of the solution.

m = moles of solute / kg of solvent

Since water is the solvent, and its mass is 100.00 g, use the conversion factor, 1 kg = 1000 g to obtain the mass of water in kg, which = 0.10000 kg (to the correct # of significant figures)

m = 0.00585 mol sucrose / 0.100000 kg H2O = 0.0585 m

To calculate the boiling point of the solution, we need to use the equation,

ΔTb = Kb x m

Kb is the boiling point constant for H2O, and its value is 0.512 ºC / m.

ΔTb = 0.512 ºC / m x 0.0585 m = 0.0300 ºC

Since ΔTb = Tb - Tbº, where Tb is the boiling point of the solution and Tbº is the boiling point of the solvent (H2O), which = 100.00 ºC, rearrange to solve for Tb.

Tb = ΔTb + Tbº = 0.0300 + 100.00 = 100.03°C

Similarly, to calculate the freezing point of the solution, you would use the equation:

ΔTf = Kf x m

Kf is the freezing point constant for H2O, and its value is 1.86 ºC / m.

ΔTf = Kf x m = 1.86 ºC / m x 0.0585 m = 0.109 ºC

Since ΔTf = Tfº - Tf, where Tf is the freezing point of the solution and Tfº is the freezing point of H2O, which = 0.00 ºC, rearrange to solve for Tf.

Tf = Tfº - ΔTf = 0.00 - 0.109 = - 0.109°C

30. What is the freezing point of distilled water? seawater? Whyvdo they differ in freezing point?​


0 degrees C.

Because distilled water is just water without sediment, it freezes at the same temperature: 0 degrees C. The amount of time it takes for the water to freeze depends on the temperature of the freezer, temperature of the water and amount of water in the container.

Why do they differ in freezing point?

Water freezes at 32ºF and in order for water to become ice, molecules need to organize themselves around impurities, or nuclei, in the water. In distilled water, these impurities are filtered out. As a result, even though the distilled water has reached its freezing point, it cannot change states yet
