13. According to the speaker, how should an individual deal with death in the poet crossing the bar?


Answer&Explanation

"Crossing the Bar" is a poem by the British Victorian poet Alfred, Lord Tennyson. The poem, written in 1889, is a metaphorical meditation on death, which sees the speaker comparing dying—or a certain way of dying—to gently crossing the sandbar between a coastal area and the wider sea/ocean. In essence, it is a poem that argues that death is in fact a kind of comfort, a point of view based on the speaker's religious faith in the afterlife. Accordingly, the speaker wants to die quietly and gently, without fear, reassured by the knowledge that what comes next is a meeting with God. "Crossing the Bar" was written shortly before Tennyson's own death, and is the poem that Tennyson wanted placed at the end of all future collections and editions of his poetry.

15. According to the Nicaraguan poet Daisy Zamora, poetry is:


For the Nicaraguan poet Daisy Zamora, poetry is a platform for activism. Her poems reflect life and its struggles especially the fight for women's rights. Her work is known for her strong stance on human rights, politics, feminism, etc. She was also an active part of the resistance against the Somoza dictatorship in 1973. She was even exiled to Hondurias, Panama and Costa Rica. Her literary work is a household in the Latin American literature as it resonates the struggles of the society. She authored 5 widely-read books in Spanish. Through poetry, she has given voice to those who are powerless and abused.

19. What is music according to the poet​


Answer: Rhythm of a poem.

Explanation: Rhythm in poetry can be thought of as the beat or the flow of a poem. It is made up of beat and repetition, so it usually refers to features of sound. It is created by stressed and unstressed syllables in a line or a verse. ... It might be simple or more complex depending on the poet and the poem.

Answer:

How a poem sounds is its music, and the easiest place to hear it is in the rhythm of a poem.

Explanation:

Rhythm (the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables) in a poem is like the melody in music; though words are essential to meaning, the poet's syntax (word choice) is also part of a poem's music.
