10. Compare and Contrast the two poems "The Lamb" and "The Tiger". Write a paragraph explaining how these similarities and differences are related to the theme of good and evil.


Answer:

"The Lamb" and "The Tiger" are two poems by William Blake that explore the theme of good and evil. Both poems present the animals as symbols of these opposing forces, with the lamb representing innocence and goodness, and the tiger representing ferocity and violence.

One of the main similarities between these poems is that they both use the animals as symbols to represent larger ideas. In "The Lamb," the lamb is a symbol of innocence and goodness, and its simplicity and vulnerability are used to evoke feelings of compassion and tenderness in the reader. In "The Tiger," the tiger is a symbol of ferocity and violence, and its power and majesty are used to evoke feelings of awe and fear in the reader.

One of the main differences between these poems is the way in which the animals are described. In "The Lamb," the lamb is described in gentle, soothing terms, as a "meek and mild" creature that is "like a child" and "innocent and sweet." In contrast, "The Tiger" is described in more ominous and fearful terms, as a "fearful symmetry" with "fierce" eyes and a "deadly" grasp. This contrast between the gentle, nurturing language of "The Lamb" and the fierce, menacing language of "The Tiger" reflects the theme of good and evil in the poems.

Overall, the similarities and differences between "The Lamb" and "The Tiger" are related to the theme of good and evil in that they both use the animals as symbols to represent these opposing forces, and they describe the animals in ways that reflect their symbolic roles.

13. Identify how the following themes are represented in Iliad, Beowulf, and the Song of Roland. AENEID BEOWULF THE SONG OF ROLAND Characteristics of a Hero Good and Evil Fate and Destiny Loyalty


Answer:

ILYAD BEOWULF THE SONG OF ROLAND

Characteristics of a Hero: The heroes in Iliad are depicted as strong and courageous warriors, who exhibit their prowess on the battlefield. Achilles, the main hero, is also shown to possess qualities of pride and anger. Similarly, Beowulf, the protagonist of the poem Beowulf, is portrayed as a brave warrior, who is also intelligent and honorable. The Song of Roland also depicts the main character Roland as a brave soldier, who is willing to sacrifice his life for his country and his lord.

Good and Evil: In Iliad, the conflict between the Greeks and Trojans is portrayed as a battle between good and evil. The Greeks are shown as righteous and honorable, while the Trojans are depicted as treacherous and deceitful. Similarly, in Beowulf, the hero is shown to be fighting against evil creatures such as Grendel, Grendel's mother, and the dragon. The Song of Roland, on the other hand, depicts the battle between the Christians and the Muslims, with the Christians being portrayed as the forces of good, and the Muslims as the forces of evil.

Fate and Destiny: In Iliad, the main hero Achilles is aware of his fate, that he will die young, but he still chooses to fight and achieve glory. Similarly, both Beowulf and the Song of Roland also portray the notion of fate and destiny. Beowulf's fate is sealed when he chooses to fight the dragon, while in the Song of Roland, the main character Roland knows that he will die in battle, but he continues to fight nonetheless.

Loyalty: Loyalty is a central theme in all three works. In Iliad, the loyalty of the soldiers towards their leaders is depicted as essential. Beowulf also depicts loyalty towards one's lord and country as a fundamental virtue. The Song of Roland, too, places a great emphasis on loyalty towards one's God, country, and leader.

Answer:

One key difference between the two poems is the tone used to describe each animal. "The Lamb" takes a gentle, nurturing tone, with the speaker asking the lamb questions and describing it as a peaceful and innocent creature. "The Tiger," on the other hand, takes a more fearful and awe-inspiring tone, with the speaker describing the tiger's strength and majesty as well as the destructive power of its flames.

Despite these differences in tone, both poems ultimately present the animals as symbols for good and evil. The lamb represents the good qualities of innocence, purity, and gentleness, while the tiger represents the evil qualities of power, ferocity, and destruction.
