17. Intellectual Revolution that define society under Asian


Revolution has been central to the formation of the modern world. The word itself refers to radical, transformative change and has many generic uses describing phenomena from the “industrial revolution" to the “sexual revolution." As a historical process, “revolution" refers to a movement, often violent, to overthrow an old regime and effect complete change in the fundamental institutions of society. After the French Revolution of the 18th century which deposed the monarchy and attempted to refashion society from top to bottom, revolution became synonymous with the radical overcoming of the past. Modernity, many came to believe, could only be achieved through such violent and total transformation.

The inspiration for many 20th century revolutions was the Russian Revolution of 1917 led by Vladimir Lenin and inspired by the ideas of Marxist Communism. Marx believed that revolution was necessary to move societies from one historical stage to the next, and his formulation strengthened the perception of revolution as a universal and inevitable process in world history. For over a half century, the Russian Revolution provided would-be revolutionaries throughout the world with a model for political revolution and socio-economic transformation. The Soviet Union’s example was especially inspirational to anti-colonial and nationalist revolutionaries, from China’s Sun Yat-sen to Vietnam’s Ho Chi Minh, who saw in the experience of the USSR solutions to the dilemmas of their own countries.

The Iranian Revolution of the late 20th century provides yet another model of revolution. The Islamist revolution of 1979 sought the radical transformation of a state and society perceived by many as overly secular and tainted by Western values and culture. The Iranian Revolution placed nationalist, Islamic values at the center of government and society and became yet another example of modern, revolutionary change. (See also The Middle East and Asia: Revolutions in Comparative Perspective.)

30. Identify the different intellectual revolutions that may have defined society. make a timeline by highlighting the major discoveries and developments in science and technology


INTELLECTUAL REVOLUTION

The 16th and 17th centuries are regarded as the time of the "Scientific Revolution," during which a number of novel theories and scientific advancements that helped to establish a "New Society" were made. These discoveries led to the "Industrial Revolution" in the 18th century, which the world received as a gift. Science took the place of religion. I believe that the following intellectual revolutions helped to change society:

1. Calvinism:

In the 16th century, John Calvin presented the "Doctrine of Predestination," which later became known as Calvinism. According to Calvin, God's fondness of Man is entirely a result of God's free will. Nothing about it is dependent on what God thinks of man. The degree of monetary success a person has in the world can reveal whether God is favoring that person or not.

2. The heliocentric model of the solar system:

Prior to the sixteenth century, it was thought that the sun revolved around the Earth and that the Earth was stationary. In contrast, Copernicus argued that the sun is at the center of the solar system and that the other planets circle around it in his book, which was published in 1543. Copernicus was assisted by Kepler's discoveries in the 17th century.

3. Newtonian Science:

Following the findings of Copernicus and Kepler, individuals started questioning the established religious doctrines and turned to science for justification. The majority of the problems that were posed in the 17th century were answered by Newton's scientific rules and discoveries (1687). Today, scientists use Newtonian physics to comprehend and explain a wide range of events.

4. Theory of Evolution:

Charles Darwin's theory of evolution, which was first put forth in the 18th century, greatly shocked the globe and altered how people viewed their own race. This hypothesis provided an explanation for the development, adaptability, and survival of organisms.

5. Industrial Revolution:

James Watt's invention of the "Steam Engine" in 1776 served as the Industrial Revolution's seedling. The industrial revolution started to engulf all of Europe by the end of the 18th century, and during the following two centuries, it engulfed the entire world. The society's orientation was altered.

6. Paradigm Shift:

Thomas Kuhn popularized this idea in his book "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions" in the 20th century, which provided a fresh perspective on how to view science.

7. Sexual Revolution:

A wave of feminism that demanded women's rights swept the globe in the 1960s and 1970s. Women gained a firm foothold in society and started to hold influential positions in the Global World that were previously held exclusively by men.
