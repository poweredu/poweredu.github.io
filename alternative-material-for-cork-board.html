Conductors are materials that allow heat or electricity to pass through them easily. Insulators, on the other hand, do not conduct heat or electricity well and are used to prevent the transfer of these forms of energy.

Wood, plastic, cork, glass, air, and wool are all poor conductors of heat and electricity, so they are considered to be good thermal insulators. Iron, copper, and aluminum, on the other hand, are good conductors of heat and electricity, so they are not good thermal insulators.

INSULATORS

Classifying materials as insulators or electrical conductors, we have the following:

Insulating materials: glass, plastic, wood and wool;Conductive materials: iron, gold, copper and aluminum.

Types of materials and their classification:

Materials that are insulators are those that do not allow strongly charged electrons from an energy source to pass through our body. That's why they don't conduct electricity.

Conductive materials are those that allow electrical charges with a high amount of charge to be transported and taken quickly to other surfaces.

17. PROBLEM SET (Quiz #3) 1. A small cork with an excess charge of +6.0 μC is placed 0.12 m from another cork, which carries a charge of -4.3 μC. a. What is the magnitude of the electric force between the corks? b. Is this force attractive or repulsive? c. How many excess electrons are on the negative cork? d. How many electrons has the positive cork lost?​


Answer:

a. The magnitude of the electric force between the corks can be calculated using Coulomb's law:

F = k * (q1 * q2) / r^2

where F is the electric force, k is Coulomb's constant (8.99 x 10^9 Nm^2/C^2), q1 and q2 are the charges on the corks, and r is the distance between them. Substituting the values given:

F = (8.99 x 10^9 Nm^2/C^2) * [(+6.0 x 10^-6 C) * (-4.3 x 10^-6 C)] / (0.12 m)^2

F = -1.75 x 10^-2 N

Therefore, the magnitude of the electric force between the corks is 1.75 x 10^-2 N.

b. The force is attractive because the charges on the corks have opposite signs.

c. The number of excess electrons on the negative cork can be calculated using the formula:

q = n * e

where q is the charge on the cork, n is the number of excess electrons, and e is the elementary charge (1.6 x 10^-19 C). Substituting the values given:

-4.3 x 10^-6 C = n * (1.6 x 10^-19 C)

n = -2.69 x 10^13 electrons

Therefore, the negative cork has 2.69 x 10^13 excess electrons.

d. The number of electrons lost by the positive cork is equal to the number of excess electrons on the negative cork. Therefore:

n = +6.0 x 10^-6 C / (1.6 x 10^-19 C)

n = 3.75 x 10^13 electrons

Therefore, the positive cork has lost 3.75 x 10^13 electrons.

30. who was the person who used his microscope to observe a thin slice of cork,and conclude that the cork was made up of cells?​


ROBERT HOOKE

Explanation:

The cell was first discovered and named by Robert Hooke in 1665. He remarked that it looked strangely similar to cellula or small rooms which monks inhabited, thus deriving the name. However what Hooke actually saw was the dead cell walls of plant cells (cork) as it appeared under the microscope.

Answer:

Robert Hooke

The 17th-century English physicist Robert Hooke was curious about the remarkable properties of cork--its ability to float, its springy quality, its usefulness in sealing bottles. Hooke investigated the structure of cork with a new scientific instrument he was very enthusiastic about called a microscope.

Robert Hooke FRS was an English scientist and architect, a polymath, recently called "England's Leonardo", who, using a microscope, was the first to visualize a micro-organism.
