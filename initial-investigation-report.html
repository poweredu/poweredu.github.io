8. It was reported in a daily newspaper that the Filipino men in Metro manila aged 50 to 60 years, exercise an average of 14 hours a week. A sports’ columnist initial reaction, upon reading the article, is that the average figure is a bit too high. He decides to investigate by interviewing a small sample of 25 men in Metro Manila aged 50 to 60 years. He finds that the average number of exercise hour per week is 11 hours with a standard deviation of 2 hours. At 5% level, was the sports writer correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high?


To determine whether the sports writer was correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high, we can conduct a hypothesis test using the sample data.

Let's define our null and alternative hypotheses:

Null hypothesis (H0): The true population mean exercise hours per week (μ) is equal to or less than 14 hours (μ ≤ 14).
Alternative hypothesis (Ha): The true population mean exercise hours per week (μ) is greater than 14 hours (μ > 14).
We will use a one-sample t-test to test these hypotheses, with a significance level of 0.05 (or 5%).

The test statistic can be calculated as:

t = (x̄ - μ) / (s / √n)

where x̄ is the sample mean, μ is the hypothesized population mean, s is the sample standard deviation, and n is the sample size.

Substituting the values we have:

t = (11 - 14) / (2 / √25) = -7.5

The critical t-value for a one-tailed test with 24 degrees of freedom (n-1) and a significance level of 0.05 is 1.711.

Since our calculated t-value of -7.5 is less than the critical t-value of 1.711, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the true population mean exercise hours per week is significantly less than 14 hours at a 5% level of significance. Therefore, the sports writer was correct in thinking that the figure stated in the newspaper was high.
