The materials around us are at room temperature. When we describe a material as being a solid, liquid or gas, we are usually describing them at room temperature. We can change a material into its other states by heating it up or cooling it down.

For example, water is a liquid at room temperature.

If we cool it down, it changes into a solid – ice.

If we heat it up, it changes into a gas – steam.

Melting Point and Freezing Point

A particular material changes from being a solid to a liquid at a fixed temperature. This is its melting point.

Ice melts to form water at 0 °C. So its melting point is 0 °C.

We can easily reverse this process. If we cool water below 0 °C, it freezes to form ice. So we also call this temperature the freezing point of water.

28. how is oxygen affect the changes in materials?​


Answer:

Simply put: oxygen (O₂) is a very reactive guy and, sooner or later, jumps on everybody.

Perhaps at room temperature you can still slow it down, but an oxygen-driven Redox reaction is one of the commonest chemical processes you can encounter wherever you go. In a nutshell, oxygen is very fond of picking up electrons from many other chemical species and thus convert itself into an oxide anion (O²¯), which is for example found in many Oxide compounds. This, along with more specific reaction conditions, is the important part of many chemical pathways O₂ is involved in:

O₂ + 4e¯ → 2O²¯

The two electrons come from the material/molecule/compound being oxidised (4e¯ stands for “4 electrons” picked up by one oxygen molecule). By doing so, O₂ is said to become reduced to, indeed, an oxide anion.

Think about, for example, Rust formation, Cellular respiration, rock formation, etc. They are all oxygen-based processes. Perhaps the equation above is an oversimplification of what actually happens, but is fairly accurate on a descriptive level nonetheless.

Materials have been shaping human history since the dawn of civilisation.

This page is dedicated to celebrating some of the materials that have significantly changed the way humans live. Because #materials matter

The first tools produced by humans were made from stone. Around 10000 years ago, humans started making objects from metals, such as gold, silver and copper. However, these metals are soft and they were mostly used for jewellery, decorative items and pots and pans for cooking, rather than tools.

Around 3000 BCE, it was discovered that adding other elements to copper, particularly tin, made it much stronger and harder, and therefore suitable for tools.

This alloy of copper and tin is called bronze. Its discovery allowed much stronger and more durable tools to be produced than had been possible before.

Bronze could also be molded and worked into different shapes more easily than stone, leading to more sophisticated tools and the first swords.
