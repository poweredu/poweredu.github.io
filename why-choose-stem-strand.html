17. Why is DRRM part of the curriculum? How is this subject relevant to STEM Strand?​


Answer:

Explanation:

Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) is an integral part of the educational curriculum. It helps by institutionalizing disaster preparedness in the school curriculum. It is essential to know the concept, importance, elements, and principles of DRRM which are beneficial in one's life. Learning DRRM is not only relevant to the STEM Strand, but it is also essential, practical, and beneficial to other strands as well as to the faculty, staff, family members, relatives, and friends.

It is essential to assess the disaster-related knowledge, preparedness and readiness, adaptation, awareness, and risk perception of students, which can help in  heightening the level of resiliency of students toward natural disasters such as typhoons, flashfloods, landslides, earthquakes, tsunamis, volcanic eruptions, and other calamities.

Through DRRM, students can also make use of the importance of Emergency plan , conduct monitoring and  evaluation , observe early warning  systems , and prepare survival kits and  materials.

In times that require DRRM implementation, these students can now know practical applications of DRRM such as organizing quick response groups, particularly in identified disaster-prone areas, develop a community  emergency  preparedness plan and  community disaster  preparedness plan, do hazard-mapping within school environments, implement drills, and many more to minimize vulnerability  and disaster risk.

22. Conduct a SWOT Analysis of STEM Strand .. and give 10 examples of Strength, 10 weaknesses, 10 opportunities, 10 threats of STEM strand ​


Answer:

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning tool used to identify the Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats of a particular project, business or industry. In this case, we will conduct a SWOT analysis of the STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) Strand.

Strengths:

High demand for STEM professionals in the job market.

Provides students with practical skills and knowledge to solve real-world problems.

Encourages critical thinking and problem-solving skills development.

Provides opportunities for students to conduct research and engage in scientific discovery.

Prepares students for further studies in STEM-related fields.

Fosters creativity and innovation.

Integrates technology and computational thinking into the curriculum.

Enhances students' communication and collaboration skills.

Provides hands-on experience in STEM-related projects and activities.

Aligns with the demands of the Fourth Industrial Revolution.

Weaknesses:

High demand for competent and qualified STEM teachers.

Limited funding and resources for STEM education.

Limited opportunities for hands-on and practical experience due to the lack of equipment and facilities.

Perception that STEM subjects are difficult and intimidating.

Limited awareness of STEM career opportunities.

STEM subjects are often perceived as being more expensive due to the cost of equipment and materials.

Lack of diversity and inclusivity in STEM fields and education.

Insufficient integration of social sciences and humanities in the STEM curriculum.

Difficulty in measuring and assessing student learning and progress in STEM subjects.

Difficulty in creating engaging and relevant STEM curricula that cater to the needs of diverse student populations.

Opportunities:

Increasing demand for STEM professionals in emerging industries such as biotechnology, artificial intelligence, and robotics.

Collaboration opportunities with local businesses and organizations to provide students with real-world experience and practical learning opportunities.

Increased government funding and support for STEM education.

Availability of online resources and platforms for STEM learning.

Increasing demand for STEM teachers.

Greater awareness of the importance of STEM education and the need for diversity and inclusivity.

Opportunities for interdisciplinary and cross-curricular learning.

Opportunities for international collaboration and exchange programs.

Greater emphasis on soft skills development in STEM education.

Availability of professional development and training opportunities for STEM teachers.

Threats:

Rapidly evolving technology leading to changing skills requirements and obsolescence of skills.

Global competition for STEM talent.

Disruptive technologies leading to job displacement and automation of certain roles.

Economic uncertainty leading to reduced funding for STEM education.

Lack of diversity and inclusivity in STEM fields and education.

Negative perception of STEM subjects and careers leading to a lack of interest and engagement.

Insufficient support for students from underrepresented groups in STEM education and careers.

Poor quality of STEM education due to inadequate training and support for teachers.

Lack of regulatory frameworks for emerging technologies and industries.

Gender bias and stereotyping leading to gender disparities in STEM education and careers.

Note: The number of strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats listed is for illustrative purposes only and may vary depending on the context and situation.
