15. Influence of the western classical art tradition to philippine art form.


The use of paint, canvas, and other materials, as well as influences like impressionism, expressionism, surrealism, cubism, pop, and minimalism, all show the impact of the western classical art tradition. The Spanish brought artistic paintings to the Philippines when they arrived there in the sixteenth century. During this time, the Spaniards used paintings as visual aids for their religious propaganda to promote Catholicism. Western art forms have influenced traditional philippine art:

• When the Spaniards arrived in the Philippines the conquerors employed art in 1521 to spread the Catholic religion by creating stunning paintings.. With communication as a problem, the friars used images to explain the concepts behind Catholicism, and to tell the stories of Christ’s life and passion Through carved santos, the Holy Family and other saints were first presented to the Filipino mind, through paintings on church walls, engravings on estampas and estampitas, and the way crucis (Stations of the Cross).

• The Spanish friars and the Chinese, the colony's main trading partner, were gradually importing novel art forms, even if the ethnic art forms such as ceramics, weaving, and metalwork were preserved. The friars' brought along icons were utilized as sculptural models. Chinese brushwork painting techniques were introduced to Filipino artists. There were also introduced engravings.

• Patronage as a notion developed. To carve, engrave, and paint, artisans were hired and compensated. Rituals took the role of the arts that were formerly practiced in a spirit of community and in a setting of community. The new patron of the arts was the church, in particular the friars.

• The friars imposed stringent oversight over their work because the majority of art created during the first two centuries of Spanish dominion was for the church. Art was only used by the church and for religious purposes up until the 19th century.

18. what are the importance of traditional Philippine arts to the recents modern arts?​


Answer:

Traditional Arts (TA) provides a shared experience for the community. Values and belief systems are often embedded in these art forms and passed down through generations; as such, they form a common language through which we can engage the various communities that make up our society. The history of Modern Philippine art is marked by the conflict between the rules and views of the Academy and the innovative methods of the Modernists. The Academic style was established during the Spanish colonial period and followed the rules of the Spanish, Italian and French Academies. While drawing on Western forms, however, the works of Filipino painters, writers, and musicians are imbued with distinctly Philippine themes. By expressing the cultural richness of the archipelago in all its diversity, Filipino artists have helped to shape a sense of national identity.

Explanation:

The traditional arts in the Philippines encompass folk architecture, maritime transport, weaving, carving, folk performing arts, folk (oral) literature, folk graphic and plastic arts, ornament, textile, or fiber art, pottery, and other artistic expressions of traditional culture.[1] There are numerous Filipino specialists or experts on the various fields of traditional arts, with those garnering the highest distinctions declared as Gawad Manlilikha ng Bayan (GAMABA), equal to National Artist.

Folk architecture

Main articles: Architecture of the Philippines, Nipa hut, Torogan, and Daru Jambangan

Folk architecture in the Philippines differ significantly per ethnic group, where the structures can be made of bamboo, wood, rock, coral, rattan, grass, and other materials. These abodes can range from the hut-style bahay kubo which utilizes vernacular mediums in construction, the highland houses called bale that may have four to eight sides, depending on the ethnic association, the coral houses of Batanes which protects the natives from the harsh sandy winds of the area, the royal house torogan which is engraved with intricately-made okir motif, and the palaces of major kingdoms such as the Daru Jambangan or Palace of Flowers, which was the seat of power and residence of the head of Sulu prior to colonization.
