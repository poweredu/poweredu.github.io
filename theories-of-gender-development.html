8. Choose two (2) of the six (6) theories of gender development. Explain each theory in 5-10 sentences. And give atleast two (2) concrete examples.​


Answer:

The social learning theory and the cognitive development theory.

Social Learning Theory:

The social learning theory suggests that gender roles and behaviors are learned through observation, imitation, and reinforcement from the people and environment around us. Children learn from observing others and imitating the behaviors and attitudes they see. They also receive reinforcement or punishment for their behaviors and learn from the consequences of their actions. This theory suggests that gender roles are not innate but are learned through socialization.

For example, a child may learn to exhibit stereotypical gender roles by observing their parents' behaviors. If a child sees their mother performing housework and taking care of the children, while their father works outside the home, the child may learn that these are the appropriate gender roles. Similarly, if a child is praised for exhibiting gender-appropriate behaviors, such as a girl being praised for being nurturing or a boy being praised for being tough, they may continue to exhibit these behaviors.

Cognitive Development Theory:

The cognitive development theory suggests that children actively construct their own understanding of gender through their experiences and interactions with the environment around them. Children develop schemas, or mental representations, of what it means to be male or female based on their observations and experiences. As children develop, they refine these schemas and integrate new information into their understanding of gender.

For example, a child may develop a schema for what it means to be a boy based on their observations of other boys around them. As they interact with more people and gain new experiences, they may refine their schema to include more complex ideas about gender, such as the idea that boys can also have emotions and be nurturing. Another example is a child who may question why they cannot wear dresses if they are a boy, challenging the idea that clothing is gendered.
