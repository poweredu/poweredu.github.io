6. What is the contribution of ampere in the electromagnetic wave theory?


Electromagnetic Wave

The magnetic force between two currents is mathematically described by Ampere's Law, which is also known as the Ampere's Law of Electromagnetism. He also conducted a number of experiments, using the results of which he was able to explain some previously observed electromagnetic wave phenomena.

Memoir on the Mathematical Theory of Electromagnetic Wave Phenomena Ampere’s most important academic work on the subject of electricity and magnetism, was released in 1826. Other 19th-century theories about electricity and magnetism were built on the theoretical groundwork laid out in this paper. It influenced the work of scientists like Faraday, Weber, Thomson, and Maxwell and sparked their investigation and discoveries.

The unit for measuring electric current known as the ampere was named in Ampere's honor. An ampere was once thought to be the force produced between two parallel electrically charged wires, but as science progresses over time, so does our understanding of what an ampere is. The ability of a specific current to deposit a precise amount of a substance on an electrode during electrolysis is described by the modern definition of ampere.

Answer:

Ampère, who was born into a prosperous bourgeois family during the height of the French Enlightenment, personified the scientific culture of his day. His father, Jean-Jacques Ampère, was a successful merchant, and also an admirer of the philosophy of Jean-Jacques Rousseau, whose theories of education, as outlined in his treatise Émile, were the basis of Ampère’s education. Rousseau argued that young boys should avoid formal schooling and pursue instead an “education direct from nature.” Ampère’s father actualized this ideal by allowing his son to educate himself within the walls of his well-stocked library. French Enlightenment masterpieces such as Georges-Louis Leclerc, comte de Buffon’s Histoire naturelle, générale et particulière (begun in 1749) and Denis Diderot and Jean Le Rond d’Alembert’s Encyclopédie (volumes added between 1751 and 1772) thus became Ampère’s schoolmasters. In addition, he used his access to the latest mathematical books to begin teaching himself advanced mathematics at age 12. His mother was a devout woman, so Ampère was also initiated into the Catholic faith along with Enlightenment science. The French Revolution (1787–99) that erupted during his youth was also formative. Ampère’s father was called into public service by the new revolutionary government, becoming a justice of the peace in a small town near Lyon. Yet when the Jacobin faction seized control of the Revolutionary government in 1792, Jean-Jacques Ampère resisted the new political tides, and he was guillotined on November 24, 1793, as part of the Jacobin purges of the period.

29. What did ampere contribution to electromagnetic theory


Ampere's contribution to electromagnetic theory was the formulation of Ampere's law.

Explanation :

Electromagnetic theory states that electromagnetic waves consist of alternating electric and magnetic fields. This means that electric and magnetic fields may exist at different times and in different spaces, but propagate with the same frequency.

In electromagnetic studies, the presence of electric charge flowing in a conductor can produce a magnetic field. To find out the strength of the magnetic field from the presence of an electric current, one way is to use Ampere's Law.

Ampere's law states "The line integral of the tangential component of the magnetic field strength around a closed path is equal to the current enclosed by the path"

Ampere's law can be applied to calculate the magnetic field strength in several systems. This depends on the form of the conductor which affects the electric current in the system.
