Adventures of Huckleberry Finn is one of Mark Twain's best-known and most important novels. The novel tells the story of Huckleberry Finn's escape from his alcoholic and abusive father and Huck's adventurous journey down the Mississippi River together with the runaway slave Jim.

4. Write a critique on the story "the Adventures of Huckleberry finn" using Marxist Approach​


Answer:

The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn is a novel written by Mark Twain, first published in 1884. The story is set in the mid-1800s in the American South and follows the adventures of a young boy named Huck Finn and his friend Jim, a runaway slave. The novel has been analyzed through various literary approaches, including Marxist theory.

Using a Marxist approach, one could argue that The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn reflects the social and economic conditions of the time in which it was written. Marxists view literature as a reflection of the society and the economic system in which it is produced. In this case, the novel can be seen as a critique of the capitalist system, particularly its impact on the lives of the poor and oppressed.

The character of Huck Finn can be seen as a representation of the working-class, who are oppressed and exploited by the capitalist class. Throughout the novel, Huck is forced to navigate a world in which he is constantly at the mercy of the wealthy and powerful. He is also made to confront the contradictions and injustices of the society in which he lives.

The character of Jim, the runaway slave, can also be analyzed through a Marxist lens. Jim is a victim of the capitalist system, which treats him as property rather than as a human being. His struggles against the system highlight the injustices of the slave trade and the dehumanizing effects of the capitalist system.

Furthermore, the novel can be seen as a critique of the ideology of the ruling class. The novel is set in a society that is built on the exploitation of the poor and the oppressed. The wealthy and powerful use their control over the means of production to maintain their position of privilege, while the working class is left to struggle for survival.

In conclusion, The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn can be analyzed through a Marxist approach to literature, which reveals the ways in which the novel reflects the social and economic conditions of the time in which it was written. Through its portrayal of the struggles of Huck and Jim, the novel highlights the contradictions and injustices of the capitalist system and provides a critique of the ideology of the ruling class.

Most likely, morality was the largest difference between Huck and Harry Potter. In the story, Huck frequently lies, steals, and threatens other people.

Harry Potter frequently engaged in wrong behavior, such as lying and stealing, yet he never went out of his way to cause harm to a student. Harry should try to had a zero tolerance for bullying and showed great compassion for everyone, especially his pals, from the start opposed to Huck.

Despite the fact that Huck and Harry had completely different adventures, saving someone or something was always the ultimate objective.

Huckleberry Finn's forty-three chapters are devoted to his adventures with Tom and Jim, whereas all seven of Harry Potter's books are about his adventures with his companions while defending the wizarding world.

Harry and Huck had quite dissimilar endings and futures. The author of Harry's story actually goes on to describe Harry, his friends, and family in the future. While Huckleberry Finn leaves his home and everything behind, Mark Twain left us wondering what will happen to him after that.

"The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn" is a classic American novel that tackles important themes such as racism, morality, and the struggle for freedom. The story follows Huck Finn, a young boy who runs away from home and embarks on a journey down the Mississippi River with an escaped slave named Jim.

The novel provides a commentary on the moral and ethical dilemmas faced by Huck as he grapples with his own beliefs and societal expectations. It also highlights the harsh realities of racism and the struggle for freedom in the post-Civil War South.

While the novel has been criticized for its use of racial slurs and stereotypes, it is important to view it within its historical context and recognize its significance in the literary canon. "The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn" is a powerful reminder of the importance of questioning societal norms and standing up for what is right, even when it goes against the grain.

Overall, "The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn" is a thought-provoking and compelling novel that continues to resonate with readers today.
