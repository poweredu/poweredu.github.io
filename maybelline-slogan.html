7. Maybelline Cousteau's backup oxygen tank reads 900 mmHg while on her boat, where the temperature is 27C. When she dives down to the bottom of an unexplored methane lake on a recently-discovered moon of Neptune, the temperature will drop down to-183'C. What will the pressure in her backup tank be at that


Answer:

270.32 mmHg

Explanation:

For this problem we can first list all the given conditions.

The initial pressure of the backup oxygen tank is 900 mmHg and its initial temperature is 27 C.

Let P1 = initial pressure and T1 = initial temperature

So P1 = 900 mmHg and T1 = 27 C

It is also given that the final temperature of the tank is -183 C. Again let us assign variable for this data.

Let T2 = -183 C

We can observe that the temperature of the tank lowered or became colder than the initial, hence we have a negative value. In order for us to have a positive and correct answer, we can convert this temperature to Kelvin.

To convert degree Celsius into Kelvin we must add 273.15 to -183

273.15 + (-183 C) = 90.15 K

And for consistency of units, we must also convert the initial temperature into Kelvin. 27 C + 273.15 = 300.15 K

Using Gay-Lussac's Law, which has the equation [tex]\frac{P1}{T1}[/tex] = [tex]\frac{P2}{T2}[/tex], we can solve for P2 (which is the final pressure) that we are looking for.

Arranging the working equation we have P2 = [tex]\frac{(P1)(T2)}{(T1)}[/tex]

and substituting the given data, P2 = [tex]\frac{(900 mmHg)(90.15 K)}{(300.15 K)}[/tex]

P2 = 270.32 mmHg

10. Maybelline Cousteau's backup oxygen tank reads 900 mmHg while on her boat, where the temperature is 27 degrees Celsius. When she dives down to the bottom of an unexpected methane lake on a recently discovered moon of Neptune, the temperature will drop down to 183 degrees Celsius. What will the pressure in her backup tank be at that temperature?


Given:

[tex]P_{1} = \text{900 mmHg}[/tex]

[tex]T_{1} = \text{27°C + 273 = 300 K}[/tex]

[tex]T_{2} = -\text{183°C + 273 = 90 K}[/tex]

Unknown:

[tex]P_{2}[/tex]

Solution:

[tex]\frac{P_{1}}{T_{1}} = \frac{P_{2}}{T_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = P_{1} × \frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}[/tex]

[tex]P_{2} = \text{900 mmHg} × \frac{\text{90 K}}{\text{300 K}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{P_{2} = \text{270 mmHg}}[/tex]
