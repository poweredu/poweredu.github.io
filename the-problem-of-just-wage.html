Answer:

Minimum wages have been defined as “the minimum amount of remuneration that an employer is required to pay wage earners for the work performed during a given period, which cannot be reduced by collective agreement or an individual contract”.1

This definition refers to the binding nature of minimum wages, regardless of the method of fixing them. Minimum wages can be set by statute, decision of a competent authority, a wage board, a wage council, or by industrial or labour courts or tribunals. Minimum wages can also be set by giving the force of law to provisions of collective agreements.

The purpose of minimum wages is to protect workers against unduly low pay. They help ensure a just and equitable share of the fruits of progress to all, and a minimum living wage to all who are employed and in need of such protection. Minimum wages can also be one element of a policy to overcome poverty and reduce inequality, including those between men and women, by promoting the right to equal remuneration for work of equal value.

Minimum wage systems should not be seen or used in isolation, but should be designed in a way to supplement and reinforce other social and employment policies. Several types of measures can be used to tackle income and labour market inequality, including pro-employment policies, social transfers, and creating an enabling environment for sustainable enterprises.

The purpose of a minimum wage, which sets a floor, should also be distinguished from collective bargaining, which can be used to set wages above an existing floor. Figure 1 shows a hypothetical wage distribution with a "minimum wage zone" and a "collective bargaining zone" which can be used to establish minimum standards and to set wages above an existing floor.

Figure 2 illustrates that the effectiveness of minimum wages depends on many factors, including the extent to which they afford protection to all workers in an employment relationship, including women, and youth and migrant workers, regardless of their contractual arrangements, as well as all industries and occupations in the economy (coverage); whether they are set and adjusted at an adequate level that covers the needs of workers and their families, while taking into account economic factors (level); and whether employers comply with minimum wage regulations (compliance).

25. What can be a lasting solution to the problems of inadequate wage and unemployment?​


Explanation:

There are a few ways to look at these problems.

First, the government solution:

Let’s think through how a government solution would exist. Government might create a bill which forces wages up (minimum wage statutes). But this is temporary since inflation (which occurs with continued government intervention like the minimum wage statutes just discussed) drives up the cost of living creating the feeling of wage inadequacy once again. So maybe just continuous minimum wage increases for eternity.

Next we’d have to create jobs by forcing employers to hire more. The interesting part of this is the minimum wage hikes will undoubtedly put many more out of a job, since they can’t be afforded by employers. Anyway, back to a supposed government solution. Some might say government could lower taxes on businesses allowing them to hire more. This might work. But let’s be serious, might doesn’t create a lasting solution for the problem of unemployment. The employer has too many choices now we’ve lower his taxes. Could we lower his taxes only if he hires more employees. This deduction wouldn’t work since too high of a deduction couldn’t be given or the government would cease to exist (too little income and the debt to GDP ratio too much making it very unfavorable to lend further causing default) while too little would cause employers to do nothing different because of the high cost of another employee. Neither the government nor employers would be satisfied with a deduction like this. Forcing them to not be so greedy and have a minimum increase each year in hiring based on the growth of the population.

We can imagine how any of these interventions create a quick trip to super-depression level disasters. Companies would quickly go out of business as they are forced to pay more for wages and an entire workforce to maintain government minimums. Government can’t seem to maintain the right balance. Friedrich Hayek taught us the which explains why central planners can’t figure out how to balance the ins and outs of the economy. There’s just no way to gather and act upon the knowledge needed to run the economy. The missing point in Hayek’s article is the specific kind of knowledge needed for healthy economic action. Free market prices allow us to get what we need to know to make decisions which will, as a society, keep bubbles and busts from happening again and again. Private property which allows, among other things, full control over a thing (e.g. a company, etc.) is what allows accurate pricing to exist. Hans-Hermanne Hope outlines this need in .

The only lasting solution I am aware of (there may be some other undiscovered method out there to make life better and easier) to the problems of inadequate wages and unemployment is allow the free market to work to its full potential. It will open up plenty of jobs and allow for everyone to be paid adequately for the job they perform. There is no guarantee there will be “enough” employment with companies looking to employ, but in a free market there is a greater likelyhood of opportunities for side hustles or entrepreneurial actions to make a living where there is much less of this with government. There’s no guarantee of wages which will make everyone wealthy, but they will at least be accurate. Then employees can know how they can maximaize their labor and time to make enough money to live to their desired potential.

30. What can be a lasting solution to the problems of inadequate wage and employment


ECONOMICS: MACROECONOMICS Question:

What can be a lasting solution to the problems of inadequate wage and employment?

Answer:

The problem of inadequate wage and employment is a complex issue that requires multiple solutions at different levels. Here are some possible lasting solutions:

Education and skill development

Providing access to education and training opportunities that equip workers with the skills needed for higher-paying jobs can increase their earning potential and job prospects.

Investment in infrastructure

Government and private sector investment in infrastructure, such as transportation and communication systems, can create new job opportunities and increase economic activity.

Job creation through entrepreneurship

Encouraging and supporting entrepreneurship can create new job opportunities and provide an alternative to traditional employment.

Labor protections

Stronger labor protections, such as minimum wage laws and regulations on working conditions, can help ensure that workers receive fair wages and benefits.

Economic policies

Implementing economic policies that promote inclusive growth, such as progressive taxation and social safety nets, can help reduce income inequality and provide a safety net for workers who are struggling to make ends meet.

It's important to note that each solution may not be applicable in every situation, and a combination of strategies may be necessary to address the complex nature of the problem.
