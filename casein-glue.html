17. Reaction mechanism for Biuret test of Casein.​


Answer:

The Biuret test is a common qualitative test for the presence of peptide bonds in a given sample. Casein is a protein found in milk, and it is composed of multiple amino acids linked by peptide bonds. The Biuret test can detect the presence of protein in the sample, and it involves a reaction between copper ions and the peptide bonds.

The Biuret test involves adding a few drops of dilute copper sulfate (CuSO4) solution to the sample solution, followed by the addition of a small amount of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution. The addition of sodium hydroxide raises the pH of the solution, causing the peptide bonds in the protein to break down and form complexes with the copper ions. These complexes give a characteristic violet color to the solution, indicating the presence of protein in the sample.

The reaction mechanism for the Biuret test can be summarized as follows:

Step 1: Formation of copper ion complexes

Cu2+ + 2OH- ⇌ Cu(OH)2

Step 2: Formation of peptide bond complexes

Cu(OH)2 + 2R-NH-CO-R' ⇌ [Cu(OH)-(R-NH-CO-R')] + H2O

The final product is a violet-colored complex between the copper ion and the peptide bond, which confirms the presence of protein in the sample. The intensity of the color produced is directly proportional to the amount of protein present in the sample.

In the case of Casein, which is a protein, the peptide bonds present in its structure undergo the same reaction, leading to the formation of violet-colored copper-peptide complexes. This reaction helps in the identification and quantification of Casein protein in a given sample.

Gluten is a protein found in wheat, rye, and barley and is known for its elastic properties, which give bread its chewy texture. Myoglobin is a protein found in muscle tissue and is responsible for storing and releasing oxygen to the muscles.

The Biuret Test is a biochemical test used to detect the presence of peptide bonds in a substance. When a substance containing peptide bonds reacts with copper sulfate in an alkaline solution, it forms a complex with a violet color.

Casein is a protein found in milk, and it contains a large number of peptide bonds. When casein is subjected to the Biuret Test, it will give a positive result, and the color of the solution will turn violet.

Soy protein, on the other hand, is a plant-based protein that also contains peptide bonds. When subjected to the Biuret Test, soy protein will also give a positive result, and the color of the solution will turn violet.

The Benedict's test, on the other hand, is used to detect the presence of reducing sugars in a substance. When a reducing sugar reacts with Benedict's reagent (a solution of copper sulfate and sodium carbonate), it forms a colored precipitate.

In the case of the Biuret test with casein and soy protein, adding Benedict's reagent would not be expected to produce any significant change in the solution. The Benedict's test is not designed to detect proteins or peptide bonds, so it would not react with the casein or soy protein.
