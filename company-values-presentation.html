1. A company invested ₱ 400,000 presently and expected a revenue of “x” at the end of years one and two. What value of “x” is expected to have a net present worth of approximately zero, if the company is given the discount rate of 14% compounded annually?


The expected revenue is 5,600,000 if the company is given a 14% discount rate.

      When comparing current earnings to projected earnings, any changes in a company's operations should be taken into account. Pro forma statements can give you this information. Trends can also be used to calculate estimated earnings. Regression analysis can be used to estimate earnings by examining historical trends. Estimated earnings can also be determined via scenario analysis. To avoid drawing general conclusions, scenario analysis examines a variety of potential outcomes, from the worst case to the best case scenario. Estimate computations should take into account the situation of the entire economy. Giving weight to the general economy is one of several elements that can be included in scenario analysis.

Estimated earnings with profit margin formula:

[tex]Estimated Earnings= Projected sales x Projected Net Profit Margin[/tex]

    Since net profit margin equals net income divided by sales, the formula of predicted sales multiplied by projected net profit margin can be utilized. Sales will be cancelled out by multiplying net sales and net profit margin, leaving net income (earnings). Although this is a general idea, it is more difficult to calculate the actual estimates.

[tex]Est.=400,000 x 14%[/tex]Expected revenue is 5,600,000.

Answer:

D

Step-by-step explanation:

FV=PV(1+r)^{n}

FV = future value

PV = present value

r = annual interest rate

{n} = number of periods interest held

( You can calculate future value with compound interest using this formula: future value = present value x (1 + interest rate)n. To calculate future value with simple interest, use this formula: future value = present value x [1 + (interest rate x time)]. )

[tex]F _{1} = P(1 + {j})^{n} + \frac{A[(1+j)²-1)}{j} [/tex]
