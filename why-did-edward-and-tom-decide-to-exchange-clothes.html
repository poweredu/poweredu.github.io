Answer:

The theme that most stuck out to me in Uncle Tom’s Cabin was the role of women in the story. In previous texts, we have talked about the “damsel in distress” narrative where women have little impact on the story. In this novel, however, the women are empowered, painted as the “good guys”, and make a real impact on the freedom of slaves. In both the cases of Mrs. Shelby and Mrs. Bird, the women are the more humane than their male counterparts. Mrs. Shelby first aids Eliza escape with her child by delaying the departure of Haley’s search team, inviting Haley to dine for lunch by the time his horses are ready.  Aunt Chloe then proceeds to take considerable time preparing the meal, delaying Haley even more.

Senator Bird has voted to pass The Fugitive Slave Law of 1850, forbidding anyone from aiding runaway slaves and requiring them to be turned in. Again, the female counterpart is portrayed as the humane one, in this case Mrs. Bird, condemning Mr. Bird for his vote, saying “You ought to be ashamed, John! Poor, homeless, houseless creatures! It’s a shameful, wicked, abominable law, and I’ll break it, for one thing, the first time I get a chance; and hope I shall have a chance, I do!” (68) Mrs. Bird reprimands her husband, which eventually leads to Senator Bird assisting Eliza and her son to a safehouse for the night.

In both cases, the women play a role in the escape of Eliza and her child, against the initial intentions of their husbands. Both Mr. Shelby and Mr. Bird are playing the hand that they are dealt, to a certain respect, simply following the law and utilizing the system of how society is set up. However, the female characters are morally superior, and play into the larger theme of female virtue that is prominent throughout the novel. Although they still lack the power of men, each female character still makes an impact within the family, through the husband.
