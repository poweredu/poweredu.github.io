2. what is opera? give example of philippine opera?


Answer:

Opera, is a staged drama set to music in its entirety, made up of vocal pieces with instrumental accompaniment and usually with orchestral overtures and interludes.

Explanation:

Philippine Opera - art and music form in which singers and musicians perform a dramatic work combining text (called a libretto) and a musical score, usually in an elaborate theatrical setting (opera house), dialogue is entirely sung and not spoken.

Started in the Philippines during the 19th Century when Instrumental virtuosos, opera singers, and Spanish zarzuela performers came. The element of tragedy, emanating from the popular themes of romance, deceit, murder, vendetta, and other elements of human frailty, became a favorite story pattern of the Filipino opera.

Examples of Philippine Opera:

Sandugong Panaginip - First Filipino opera by Pedro Paterno first performed in the Zorilla Theater on August 2, 1902. later translated by the Englishman M.W. Loving with the title “ The Dreamed Alliance” Lakangbini - opera by Patricio Mariano staged at the Metropolitan Theater on December 19, 1933.Laloba Negra - is a three-act Filipino opera. Acts I and II are based on history. Act III is based on a legend attributed to Fr. Jose Burgos. The music was composed by National Artist Francisco Feliciano, with libretto by soprano Fides Cuyugan Asensio. The premiere was held in 1984 followed by another presentation in 1985.Noli me Tangere - A three-act opera composed by Felipe De Leon with libretto by Guillermo Tolentino. The title was said to have originated from the Latin version of the words from the Bible verse John 20: 17. It was written to expose the ills of the Spanish Catholic friars and the ruling colonial government.El Filibusterismo - also a three-act opera composed by Felipe De Leon in 1970 with libretto in Filipino by Anthony Morli. It is a novel written as a sequel to the former novel. It tells the continuing struggle of the Filipinos to achieve freedom from Spanish rule.

23. What do you think are the differences and similarities of European Opera to Philippine Opera?


Answer:

European Opera and Philippine Opera have both similarities and differences in terms of their musical style, themes, and performance traditions.

One of the major differences is the language used in the opera. European Opera is often performed in Italian, French, German, or English, while Philippine Opera is usually performed in Tagalog or other local languages. This difference in language also reflects the cultural and historical influences on the development of each opera tradition.

Another difference is the musical style. European Opera is known for its grand and elaborate musical arrangements, often with large orchestras and choirs, while Philippine Opera often features more traditional Filipino musical instruments and melodies.

However, both opera traditions share similarities in their themes, such as love, tragedy, and human emotions. They also both feature strong vocal performances, acting, and stagecraft to convey the story and emotions of the characters.

In terms of performance traditions, both European Opera and Philippine Opera have formal and traditional aspects, with conventions for dress, behavior, and audience participation. However, Philippine Opera also often incorporates elements of folk culture and local customs, while European Opera tends to be more formal and stylized.

Overall, while there are significant differences between European Opera and Philippine Opera, both traditions offer unique and powerful experiences of music, drama, and storytelling

Boisterous belting followed by a broken champagne glass—this is just one of the many perceptions people have about opera. And though the high-pitched singing might not always break glass, it is bound to keep listeners fastened to their seats, waiting for the next aria to commence.

But modern interest in operas is at a decline. Unlike its lively introduction to the country as the Spanish zarzuela—where comedy, drama, and music capture audiences—the public tends to perceive that “opera is boring,” as Philippine Opera Company (POC) Artistic Director Karla Gutierrez bluntly puts it.
