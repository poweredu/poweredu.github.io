5. Public Administration vs Private Administration


Public Administration versus Private Administration

The difference between public administration and private administration is that while public administration is a political activity that is run by the government with a service motive, private administration is a business activity that is run by a group of individuals not related to government in any way whatsoever with the sole motive of profit.

Main differences between Public Administration and Private Administration

Public administration is set up by the government. While Private administration is not set up by the government instead it is set up by a group of private individuals.

Public administration follows a bureaucratic approach while private administration follows an egalitarian approach.

Public administration follows a pluralistic approach of decision making while private administration follows a monopolistic approach to decision making.

Public administration has a service and welfare motive. Private administration is profit-oriented i.e the sole motive here is profit maximization.

Public administration is accountable to the general public while private administration is accountable to its employees.

Public Administration earns revenue through taxes, fines, fees, etc. while private administration earns revenue through profit.

Administrators working in a public administration are paid salaries while administrators working in a private administration earn their remuneration through profits.

Public administration is political activity while private administration is a business activity.

11. 2. What are the Problems of administrative law in the system of public administration? ​


Administrative law is a branch of law that deals with the legal principles and procedures governing the actions of administrative agencies, such as government bodies and regulatory agencies. While administrative law is important in ensuring accountability and transparency in the functioning of public administration, it is not without its problems. Here are some of the problems of administrative law in the system of public administration:

Lack of transparency and accountability: One of the key problems of administrative law is the lack of transparency and accountability in the decision-making processes of administrative agencies. This can lead to arbitrary and inconsistent decision-making, as well as a lack of public trust in the fairness and impartiality of the administrative system.

Over-centralization: Administrative law can also be hindered by the over-centralization of decision-making power in administrative agencies. This can limit the ability of lower-level officials to make decisions and can also lead to delays in decision-making and an inefficient bureaucracy.

Limited access to justice: Administrative law can also be problematic in terms of access to justice. Administrative agencies may have limited resources and may not always be able to provide adequate remedies or appeals for those who have been adversely affected by their decisions.

Inflexibility: Administrative law can be inflexible and may not be able to keep pace with changes in society and technology. This can lead to outdated regulations that are not effective in addressing new challenges and issues.

Lack of expertise: Administrative agencies may lack the necessary expertise to make informed decisions, particularly in technical areas such as environmental or health and safety regulations. This can lead to decisions that are based on incomplete or inaccurate information.

Corruption: Administrative law can also be undermined by corruption and other forms of misconduct within administrative agencies. This can lead to decisions that are made for personal gain rather than in the public interest.

Overall, these problems highlight the need for ongoing reforms and improvements in the field of administrative law, to ensure that it remains effective and relevant in the evolving landscape of public administration.

18. what are the roles of public administrationists in the field of public administration ​


Answer:

Public administration is the implementation of government policy and also an academic discipline that studies this implementation and prepares civil employees for working in the public service.[1] As a "field of inquiry with a diverse scope" whose fundamental goal is to "advance management and policies so that government can function".[2] Some of the various definitions which have been offered for the term are: "the management of public programs";[3] the "translation of politics into the reality that citizens see every day";[4] and "the study of government decision making, the analysis of the policies themselves, the various inputs that have produced them, and the inputs necessary to produce alternative policies."[5]

Public administration is both an academic discipline and a field of practice; the latter is depicted in this picture of United States federal public servants at a meeting.

Public administration is "centrally concerned with the organization of government policies and programs as well as the behavior of officials (usually non-elected) formally responsible for their conduct".[6] Many non-elected public employees can be considered to be public administrators, including heads of city, county, regional, state and federal departments such as municipal budget directors, human resources (HR) administrators, city managers, census managers, state mental health directors, and cabinet secretaries.[4] Public administrators are public employees working in public departments and agencies, at all levels of government.

In the United States, civil employees and academics such as Woodrow Wilson promoted civil service reform in the 1880s, moving public administration into academia.[7] However, "until the mid-20th century and the dissemination of the German sociologist Max Weber's theory of bureaucracy" there was not "much interest in a theory of public administration".[8] The field is multidisciplinary in character; one of the various proposals for public administration's sub-fields sets out six pillars, including human resources, organizational theory, policy analysis, statistics, budgeting, and ethics.[9]
