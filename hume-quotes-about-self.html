12. what is self according to David Hume​


Hume is at the peak of empiricism, for him, experience is more than ratio as a source of knowledge, both internal and external experience. According to him, all science is related to human nature. This knowledge is the only solid foundation for other sciences.

According to Hume, human understanding is influenced by certain basic uncertainties about the external world, the future, causes, and certainties - these certainties are natural human instincts, which are neither produced nor prevented by reason or human thought processes. With natural instincts, humans can achieve certainties that make human knowledge possible.

Hume's theory destroys the theory of rationalism which says that the source of knowledge is through reason or reason. According to Hume, knowledge comes from experience received by sensory impressions.

It thus encourages us that to find knowledge requires our experience, so to prove the truth of knowledge requires field research, such as observation or experiment, which in this way is the starting point of human knowledge.

Hume does not accept substance, because what is experienced is only impressions of some characteristics that always exist together. From impressions come ideas. Impressions are the result of direct sensing, whereas ideas are memories of such impressions.

For example, there is an object with the characteristics of white, smooth, light, and thin. On the basis of these characteristics, it can be concluded that the object is paper.

Hume said that our atomic ideas are related to impressions, linked or joined by means of the three laws of association which are subtle forces that compel us to combine one idea with another.

21. the self is the bundle theory of the mind by david hume​


Answer:

Theory advanced by David Hume to the effect that the mind is merely a bundle of perceptions without deeper unity or cohesion, related only by resemblance, succession, and causation. Hume’s well-argued denial of a substantial or unified self precipitated a philosophical crisis from which Immanuel Kant sought to rescue Western philosophy.
