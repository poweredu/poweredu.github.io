7. what are the differences of active fault and inactive fault?


An active fault is a fault that has displayed recent seismic activity, while an
inactive fault has not displayed recent seismic activity. Do not be fooled by
the word "recent," however, as we are talking about "recent" from a geological
perspective, which is much different from a non-geologic perspective. Because of
the fickle nature of plate tectonics, an active fault could have earthquakes as often as once every few years or once every one thousand years.

11. differentiate active faults from inactive faults​


Answer:

﻿⁸

Explanation:

Structures along which we anticipate displacement to occur are known as active faults. All shallow earthquakes take place on active faults by definition since a shallow earthquake is a process that causes displacement across a fault. Meanwhile, inactive faults are structures that we can recognize without experiencing earthquakes.

If there has been movement or evidence of seismic activity within the last 10,000 years, a fault is typically regarded to be active. A geologic risk that is linked to earthquakes as a source is active faulting. Identifiable features that do not produce earthquakes are known as dormant faults.

Earth scientists categorize faults based on the direction of slip along the fault and the dip, or angle of the fault with regard to the surface. Dip-slip faults are those that move in the same direction as the dip plane; depending on how they move, they can be classified as normal or reverse (thrust) faults.

There are three types of faults:

Strike-slip faults

Rocks are moving horizontally past one another and barely moving vertically, according to strike-slip faults. Strike-slip faults are the Anatolian and San Andreas Faults.

Normal faults

Space is created by common flaws. When two crustal blocks separate, the crust forms a valley. Two well-known areas where normal faults are tearing apart the Earth's crust are the Basin and Range Province in North America and the East African Rift Zone.

Reverse faults

One block of crust is pushed on top of another by reverse faults, also known as thrust faults. These faults are frequently seen in areas where tectonic plates collide and mountain ranges like the Himalayas and the Rocky Mountains are pushed upward.

12. Active faults are_________While inactive faults are _________​


Explanation:

ACTIVE FAULTS

An active fault is a fault that is likely to become the source of another earthquake sometime in the future. Geologists commonly consider faults to be active if there has been movement observed or evidence of seismic activity during the last 10,000 years.

INACTIVE FAULTS

Inactive faults are structures that we can identify, but which do no have earthquakes. As you can imagine, because of the complexity of earthquake activity, judging a fault to be inactive can be tricky, but often we can measure the last time substantial offset occurred across a fault.
