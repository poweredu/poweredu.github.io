8. Discuss the characteristics and types of psychological tests.


Answer:

Psychological tests are standardized measures designed to assess an individual's cognitive, emotional, and behavioral functioning. The following are the characteristics and types of psychological tests:

Characteristics:

Standardization: Psychological tests are administered and scored the same way for each individual.

Reliability: Psychological tests yield consistent results across time and administrators.

Validity: Psychological tests measure what they are intended to measure.

Objectivity: Psychological tests are unbiased and free from personal judgment.

Types:

Intelligence Tests: Assess cognitive abilities and intelligence.

Personality Tests: Assess personality traits and characteristics.

Neuropsychological Tests: Assess cognitive function and identify brain dysfunction.

Aptitude Tests: Assess specific abilities or skills, such as verbal or mathematical skills.

Interest Tests: Assess an individual's interests and preferences.

Achievement Tests: Assess knowledge or skills in a particular area, such as reading or math.

Projective Tests: Assess unconscious or hidden aspects of an individual's personality through their interpretation of ambiguous stimuli.


9. Why does psychological experiments utilize animals for testing?​


Answer:

experiments. Here is a selection of common animal procedures:

Forced chemical exposure in toxicity testing, which can include oral force-feeding, forced inhalation, skin or injection into the abdomen, muscle, etc.

Exposure to drugs, chemicals or infectious disease at levels that cause illness, pain and distress, or death

Genetic manipulation, e.g., addition or “knocking out” of one or more genes

Ear-notching and tail-clipping for identification

Short periods of physical restraint for observation or examination

Prolonged periods of physical restraint

Food and water deprivation

Surgical procedures followed by recovery

Infliction of wounds, burns and other injuries to study healing

Infliction of pain to study its physiology and treatment

Behavioural experiments designed to cause distress, e.g., electric shock or forced swimming

Other manipulations to create “animal models” of human diseases ranging from cancer to stroke to depression

Killing by carbon dioxide asphyxiation, neck-breaking, decapitation, or other means

Answer:

There are four main types of validity: Construct validity: Does the test measure the concept that it's intended to measure? Content validity: Is the test fully representative of what it aims to measure? Face validity: Does the content of the test appear to be suitable to its aims?Common methods to assess construct validity include, but are not limited to, factor analysis, correlation tests, and item response theory models (including Rasch model).
