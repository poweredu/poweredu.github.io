Auroras normally appear in areas where the earth’s magnetic field is concentrated and pulls past moving particles radiated by the sun down into the earth’s atmosphere, where they collide with gas molecules and cause them to glow.

This happens near the earth’s magnetic poles in the arctic and antarctic. In some rare conditions (e.g. a strong solar flare) auroras can appear in places like central Europe, but it is virtually impossible over the equator, as the magnetic field guides the particles well above the atmosphere

12. What processes give rise to the Aurora Borealis and Aurora Australis​


Answer:

The auroras, both surrounding the north magnetic pole (aurora borealis) and south magnetic pole (aurora australis) occur when highly charged electrons from the solar wind interact with elements in the earth's atmosphere. Solar winds stream away from the sun at speeds of about 1 million miles per hour.

15. what is the aurora borealis?


The Aurora Borealis is a light show caused by collisions between electrically charged particles released from the sun that enter the earth's atmosphere and collide with gases such as oxygen and nitrogen. 17. Where aurora can find? AND How aurora formed?


The aurora which is called Aurora Borealis in the north and Aurora Australias in the south can be most strongly seen in the poles of the earth. 

Aurora is formed when solar winds which are large streams of charged particles reach the earth's atmosphere and excite the atoms that are in there. The excited atoms will then occupy a higher energy level and once they go back to their normal energy level, they will emit energy in the form of heat or light which you see in auroras.
