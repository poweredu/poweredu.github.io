5. a motorcycle travels 75 km in 25 minutes what is the average speed of the motorcycle​


Answer:

Given that I believe now that you genuinely don't know how to work this out, I'll try to help you understand it (I'm not a teacher though, so please bear with me).

What you have in the question is a distance (75 km) and a time (1 hour).

If you have this information you also have the speed. Speed is distance per time. In your question, this translates to ‘seventy-five kilometres per hour’, or in shorthand 75 km/hr.

You have to assume the speed is constant otherwise you run into difficulties.

Next, work out how far thd bike travels in one minute (because the second part of your question asks how far it has travelled in a number of minutes (40 mins)).

There are 60 minutes in one hour, so you divide both bits of information by 60 to ensure that on the minutes side, you have ‘one’.

60 divided by 60 = 1 minutes

75 divided by 60 = 1.25 km

So in 1 minute, the bike travels 1.25 km

Now multiply both sides by 40 to ensure that on the minutes side you have 40.

1 x 40 = 40 minutes

1.25 x 40 = 50 km

And there's your answer:

In 40 minutes, the bike travels 50 km.

This exact same process will work for any scenario using the same type of units (hours, minutes, km for example).

Now, factorials.

Quite simply, 3! = 3 x 2 x 1

And 9! = 9 x 8 x 7 x 6 x 5 x 4 x 3 x 2 x 1

14. A motorcycle accelerates at 4m/s2 from rest for 10 s. Find the KE ofmotorcycle after 5s. Mass of motorcycle is 300 kg​

1500

Explanation:

Let’s begin with a particle with an acceleration a(t) is a known function of time. Since the time derivative of the velocity function is acceleration,

\[\frac{d}{dt}v(t)=a(t),\]

we can take the indefinite integral of both sides, finding

\[\int \frac{d}{dt}v(t)dt=\int a(t)dt+{C}_{1},\]

where C1 is a constant of integration. Since

\[\int \frac{d}{dt}v(t)dt=v(t)\]

, the velocity is given by

\[v(t)=\int a(t)dt+{C}_{1}.\]
