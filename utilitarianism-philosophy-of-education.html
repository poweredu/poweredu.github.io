6. philosophy of education


Philosophy of education is the branch of applied or practical philosophy concerned with the nature and aims of education and the philosophical problems arising from educational theory and practice. Because that practice is ubiquitous in and across human societies, its social and individual manifestations so varied, and its influence so profound, the subject is wide-ranging, involving issues in ethics and social/political philosophy, epistemology, metaphysics, philosophy of mind and language, and other areas of philosophy. Because it looks both inward to the parent discipline and outward to educational practice and the social, legal, and institutional contexts in which it takes place, philosophy of education concerns itself with both sides of the traditional theory/practice divide. Its subject matter includes both basic philosophical issues (e.g., the nature of the knowledge worth teaching, the character of educational equality and justice, etc.) and problems concerning specific educational policies and practices (e.g., the desirability of standardized curricula and testing, the social, economic, legal and moral dimensions of specific funding arrangements, the justification of curriculum decisions, etc.). In all this the philosopher of education prizes conceptual clarity, argumentative rigor, the fair-minded consideration of the interests of all involved in or affected by educational efforts and arrangements, and informed and well-reasoned valuation of educational aims and interventions.

Philosophy of education has a long and distinguished history in the Western philosophical tradition, from Socrates’ battles with the sophists to the present day. Many of the most distinguished figures in that tradition incorporated educational concerns into their broader philosophical agendas (Curren 2000, 2018; Rorty 1998). While that history is not the focus here, it is worth noting that the ideals of reasoned inquiry championed by Socrates and his descendants have long informed the view that education should foster in all students, to the extent possible, the disposition to seek reasons and the ability to evaluate them cogently, and to be guided by their evaluations in matters of belief, action and judgment. This view, that education centrally involves the fostering of reason or rationality, has with varying articulations and qualifications been embraced by most of those historical figures; it continues to be defended by contemporary philosophers of education as well (Scheffler 1973 [1989]; Siegel 1988, 1997, 2007, 2017). As with any philosophical thesis it is controversial; some dimensions of the controversy are explored below.

This entry is a selective survey of important contemporary work in Anglophone philosophy of education; it does not treat in detail recent scholarship outside that context.

10. why is spencer's educational though described as utilitarian?​


Spencer's educational view though describes as utilitarian.

Because, education of Spencer is closely related to normative ethical educational theory which dictates that kindness is an act that maximizes happiness and well-being for all affected individuals.

FURTHER EXPLANATION:

Utilitarianism is a normative ethical theory that dictates that kindness is an act that maximizes happiness and well-being for all affected individuals.

Although there are forms of utilitarianism with different characterizations, the basic idea of ​​ethical utilitarianism is to maximize utility, or what is often defined by the term welfare. Jeremy Bentham, founder of utilitarianism, defines utility as "a character in any object that produces benefit, gain, pleasure, good, or happiness ... [or] to prevent harm, pain, evil, or unhappiness to those whose interests are considered ."

Utilitarianism is a version of consequentialism, an ethical theory that holds that the consequences of an action are the only standard by which to judge right and wrong. Unlike other forms of consequentialism, such as egoism and altruism, utilitarianism considers the interests of all human beings to be equal. Proponents of utilitarianism differ on several issues, such as whether an action should be chosen based on the likelihood of the outcome (action utilitarianism), or whether an agent should act according to utility-maximizing rules (rule utilitarianism). There is also a difference of opinion as to whether the total utility (total utilitarianism), the average utility or the utility of the poorest people should be maximized.

Spencer became one of the main proponents of modern curriculum theory. He created a stir in England with his curriculum theory as the main focus of education continued to be Latin and Greek and literature. In his work “What Knowledge is Most Valuable?” Spencer states that this question needs to be answered before any curriculum is chosen or any instruction begins. Once this question is answered, it must be ensured that the curriculum aids in advancing survival and progress.

To achieve this progress Spencer believed that five activities were needed in the curriculum. These activities assist self-preservation, job performance, child-rearing, social and political participation, recreation and recreation. Again, the main goal is to teach subjects that will contribute to a successful life.

Education today continues to be influenced by Spencer's Social Darwinist theory. In fact, the curriculum activities based on human needs are still implemented in one form or another.

14. "Education without philosophy is blind and philosophy without education is invalid"​

It is a quote written by Thomas G. W. P in his book introduction to Philosophy, published in 1968. The purpose behind this beautiful phrase is that education and philosophy are two subjects that cannot be separated no matter what. They complement each other, and thus, when there is only one available, then there will be a consequence (blind and invalid).

Education without philosophy is blind, because it does not have a strong foundation. Philosophy in education acts as a fundamental system which provides the direction, purpose, as well as methodology to achieve the purpose of education.

Meanwhile, philosophy without education will fail and considered invalid due to its vast amount of discourse and topics that could go on and on. Education will act as a field where philosophy can apply and practice what it can do, thus reaching its full potential.

In conclusion, the two subjects complete each other, and it is better to use them hand in hand to avoid extremity in each side (education and philosophy).

30. Explain; 1. Utilitarianism 2. The Philosophies of Bentham and Mill 3. Positive and Negative Utilitarianism4. Business Utilitarianism5. Business depends upon the consequence of an action ergo consequentialism6. Business depends on welfarism 7. Business is individualism 8. Business is aggregation ​


Answer:

Utilitarianism is a philosophical approach that focuses on maximizing the overall happiness and minimizing the overall suffering for the greatest number of people.

2. Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill were two influential philosophers in the utilitarian tradition. Bentham believed that pleasure and pain were the most important factors in determining what was moral, while Mill believed in the idea of "higher pleasures," which prioritized intellectual and moral pursuits over physical pleasures.

3. Positive utilitarianism focuses on maximizing pleasure, while negative utilitarianism focuses on minimizing suffering.

4. Business utilitarianism is an application of utilitarianism in business ethics. It involves maximizing profits while also considering the well-being of all stakeholders, including employees, customers, and society as a whole.

5. Consequentialism is a moral theory that evaluates the morality of an action based on its outcomes or consequences. In business, consequentialism can be seen as focusing on the results of an action in terms of financial gain or loss.

6. Welfarism is the belief that the well-being of individuals is the most important factor in determining what is morally right. In business, welfarism can be seen as focusing on the well-being of employees, customers, and society as a whole.

7. Individualism is a philosophy that emphasizes the importance of individual freedom and autonomy. In business, individualism can be seen as prioritizing the interests of the individual business owner or entrepreneur over the interests of other stakeholders.

8. Aggregation is the idea that the interests of all stakeholders should be considered equally when making decisions. In business, aggregation can be seen as an approach that seeks to balance the interests of all stakeholders, rather than prioritizing the interests of one group over another.
