Song to Celia

-Jonson's "Song: To Celia" is a short monologue in which a lover addresses his lady in an effort to encourage her to express her love for him. ... As a result, the poem becomes a lively, expressive song extolling the immortality of love.

Rosalind’s Madrigal

-'Rosalind's Madrigal' by Thomas Lodge is a five stanza poem told from the perspective of the main character of an Elizabethan play. The name, Rosalind, refers to the play, “Rosalind,” in which a woman is struck by the pains of love, while the word “madrigal” is a reference to a form of a song from the Renaissance.

12. which line from the poem the ladys dressing room would best help the reader infer celias social class

Consider the poem The Lady's Dressing Room by Jonathan Swift. There are several lines that could give us a hint or could describe the social status, social class, and further, the social mobilization.

Consider these lines:

1. Five hours, (and who can do it less in?)

By haughty Celia spent in dressing;

2. The goddess from her chamber issues,

Arrayed in lace, brocades and tissues.

3. Now listen while he next produces

The various combs for various uses,

These three lines could prime us that somehow, Celia belongs to a class that can afford several cosmetic items to suffice her beauty regimen needs.

The fact also that there is a room dedicated to house her cosmetic materials is an indication that she lives in a spacious abode.

The language of the poem that was used, e.g. the specificity of the cosmetic material, i.e. various combs, lace, brocades, tissues, etc. signifies the meticulousness of a lady in terms of affording those kinds of wants.

All in all, words and lines in the poem that would signify one's social class can be interpreted and arranged to be able to decode and to infer characterization and a character's social class.

14. What is the thirst that arises from the soul? What is the drink divine that can satisfy that thirst in Song of celia


Answer:

The thirst that from the soul doth rise, Doth ask a drink divine: The speaker explains his desire for a drink that is not a drink. ... Because his "thirst" is from the "soul," it requires something more "divine" than, say, "wine" to satisfy it.

He notes that "as the lady's kiss in the cup satisfied physical thirst better than wine, so her nectar should satisfy the thirst of the soul better than Jove's drink could."

Below The Surface by Celia Berrell

Noise pollution: cars, machines.

It’s not as harmless

as it seems.

The call of every little bird

won’t travel far

or can’t be heard.

Its song for some potential mate

is stifled by

the noise we make.

And so it either moves away

or has no fertile

eggs to lay.

The Emu pondered what to do

so songs of birds

could still get through.

Deciding that to drown our sound

the humans should

live under-ground.
