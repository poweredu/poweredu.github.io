2. Describe how the filipino value system affects the attitudes of business constituents.​


The Filipino Values in Business

The Filipino value system, also known as Filipino values, consists of a collection of values or a value system that a majority of Filipinos have traditionally regarded. Our own peculiar assemblage of consistent beliefs, moral standards, ethical practices, etiquette, and cultural and personal ideals are promoted by our culture in this Philippine belief system. However, as in any culture, an individual's sacred beliefs can vary depending on faith, childhood, and other factors.

 

Here are some of the examples of Filipino values: Utang na loob, or debt of gratitude, means remembering the good things that others have done for you, particularly when you are in need. Many who have done well to others may misuse this debt of gratitude by asking for favors or items that are either unfair or beyond the means of the indebted. Utang na loob can be beneficial or harmful to business, based on a variety of factors, but it does have an impact on how business is done, especially when the businessperson is faced with ethical concerns. Filial piety is the virtue of honoring one's parents, grandparents, and great grandparents. Filipinos place a strong emphasis on their families. Filipinos regard their families as a vital social network that must be protected. They place a premium on one's family's protection and unity. The Filipino family is so close-knit that it is normal for family members to work for the same organization. The suki scheme is also a patronage system in which a consumer buys their goods from the same individual on a daily basis. In the merchandising industry, Filipinos often buy from individual vendors who can provide them low rates, high quality, and credit. This are the standard requirements for being a suki. The formation of an economic trade agreement requires the existence of confidence and the maintenance of friendship between the two parties.
