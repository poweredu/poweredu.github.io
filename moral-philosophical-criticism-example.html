5. Give two real life example in each Philosophical view and one real life example in moral discernment​


Answer:

Philosophical Views:

Utilitarianism:

Example 1: A company decides to switch to eco-friendly packaging for their products, even though it is more expensive, because it will have a positive impact on the environment and the well-being of future generations.

Example 2: A government invests in public transportation to reduce traffic congestion and improve air quality, even though it requires significant financial resources, because it will benefit the majority of the population.

Deontology:

Example 1: A doctor refuses to provide a patient with a drug that has not been approved by regulatory authorities, even though the patient believes it will cure their illness, because it would violate ethical principles and professional obligations.

Example 2: A police officer refuses to use excessive force against a suspect, even though it may expedite an investigation, because it would violate the individual's human rights.

Virtue Ethics:

Example 1: A coach teaches their team to value fair play and good sportsmanship, rather than just winning at any cost, because it promotes character development and fosters a sense of community.

Example 2: A parent teaches their child to be honest and kind, rather than just seeking personal gain or avoiding punishment, because it is the right thing to do and will help them build positive relationships.

Moral Discernment:

Example: A person is faced with the decision of whether or not to report a coworker who is engaging in unethical behavior. After considering the potential consequences and reflecting on their personal values and principles, they decide to report the behavior because they believe it is the right thing to do, even though it may lead to conflict or negative consequences.

30. discuss the philosophical foundation of morality ​


Answer:

Moral philosophy is the branch of philosophy that contemplates what is right and wrong. It explores the nature of morality and examines how people should live their lives in relation to others.

Moral philosophy has three branches.

One branch, meta-ethics, investigates big picture questions such as, “What is morality?” “What is justice?” “Is there truth?” and “How can I justify my beliefs as better than conflicting beliefs held by others?”

Another branch of moral philosophy is normative ethics. It answers the question of what we ought to do. Normative ethics focuses on providing a framework for deciding what is right and wrong. Three common frameworks are deontology, utilitarianism, and virtue ethics.

The last branch is applied ethics. It addresses specific, practical issues of moral importance such as war and capital punishment. Applied ethics also tackles specific moral challenges that people face daily, such as whether they should lie to help a friend or co-worker.

So, whether our moral focus is big picture questions, a practical framework, or applied to specific dilemmas, moral philosophy can provide the tools we need to examine and live an ethical life.
