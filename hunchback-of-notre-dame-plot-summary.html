2. summary of the story "the hunchback of notre dame


In 15th century Paris, Clopin the puppeteer tells the story of Quasimodo, the misshapen but gentle-souled bell ringer of Notre Dame, who was nearly killed as a baby by Claude Frollo, the Minister of Justice. But Frollo was forced by the Archdeacon of Notre Dame to raise Quasimodo as his own. Now a young man, Quasimodo is hidden from the world by Frollo in the belltower of the cathedral. But during the Festival of Fools, Quasimodo, cheered on by his gargoyle friends Victor, Hugo, and Laverne, decides to take part in the festivities, where he meets the lovely gypsy girl Esmeralda and the handsome soldier Phoebus. The three of them find themselves ranged against Frollo's cruelty and his attempts to destroy the home of the gypsies, the Court of Miracles. And Quasimodo must desperately defend both Esmeralda and the very cathedral of Notre Dame.

3. plot of the story The hunchback of Notre Dame​


The plot of The Hunchback of Notre Dame revolves around a young man named Quasimodo, who is physically deformed, with a hunched back and an ugly face. Quasimodo lives in the bell tower of the famous Notre Dame Cathedral in Paris, where he rings the bells.

One day, Quasimodo ventures out into the streets of Paris during the Festival of Fools, where he meets a young gypsy woman named Esmeralda. Quasimodo falls in love with Esmeralda, but so does a handsome soldier named Phoebus, who is engaged to be married. Additionally, the wicked Archdeacon of Notre Dame, Claude Frollo, is also infatuated with Esmeralda, but his love for her is twisted and obsessive.

As the story progresses, events conspire to bring about Quasimodo, Esmeralda, and Frollo together. A plot to frame Esmeralda for a crime results in her being arrested and sentenced to death, and Quasimodo must save her from the gallows. Meanwhile, Frollo's twisted love for Esmeralda leads him down a dark path, and he becomes more and more dangerous.

In the dramatic climax of the story, Quasimodo defends Esmeralda from the wrath of the mob, before soldiers intervene to end the chaos. In the end, Frollo, consumed with his obsession, meets his downfall, and both Phoebus and Quasimodo lose their love to death.

The story of The Hunchback of Notre Dame is a tragic one, exploring themes of love, obsession, betrayal, and the corrupting influence of power. It showcases the dark side of humanity, as well as the beauty and nobility that can be found in the unlikeliest of places.

6. resolution of the hunchback of notre dame


The resolution of The Hunchback of Notre Dame ends in tragedy when Quasimodo, after trying to save La Esmeralda’s life, realizes that he has still lost her. Since he no longer has any reason to live for, he then kills Claude Frollo who is the one responsible for the death of La Esmeralda.


With nothing else to live for, Quasimodo perishes in a vault while clinging unto La Esmeralda’s dead body, since in death, he can no longer distance himself to the one he truly loves.

8. critique the hunchback of notre dame​


Answer:

Explanation:

"The Hunchback of Notre Dame" is a novel written by Victor Hugo that was first published in 1831. The novel tells the story of Quasimodo, a hunchbacked bell-ringer of Notre Dame Cathedral, and his struggles to find acceptance and love in a society that judges him based on his appearance.

Overall, "The Hunchback of Notre Dame" is a compelling and thought-provoking novel that addresses themes such as beauty, love, and acceptance. One of the strengths of the novel is its vivid portrayal of medieval Paris, which provides a rich and immersive setting for the story. Hugo's descriptions of Notre Dame Cathedral and the surrounding city are detailed and evocative, and help transport the reader back to a bygone era.

Another strength of the novel is its complex and multifaceted characters. Quasimodo, the protagonist, is a sympathetic and tragic figure who struggles to find acceptance and love in a society that judges him based on his appearance. Esmeralda, the object of his affection, is a strong and independent woman who defies societal norms and expectations. The other characters in the novel, such as Frollo and Phoebus, are also well-developed and add depth and nuance to the story.

However, there are also some criticisms of "The Hunchback of Notre Dame". One criticism is that the novel can be overly melodramatic and sentimental at times, with some scenes feeling contrived or forced. Another criticism is that the novel can be slow-paced and meandering in some parts, with lengthy descriptions and digressions that can detract from the overall pacing and flow of the story.

Despite these criticisms, "The Hunchback of Notre Dame" remains a classic and enduring work of literature that continues to captivate and inspire readers today. Its themes of acceptance, love, and the struggle for identity and belonging are as relevant today as they were over a century ago, and its vivid portrayal of medieval Paris continues to enchant and fascinate readers around the world.

24. critique of the hunchback of notre dame​


Answer:

"The Hunchback of Notre Dame" is a novel written by Victor Hugo and published in 1831. It tells the story of Quasimodo, a deformed bell-ringer of the Notre Dame Cathedral, and his love for the beautiful gypsy dancer, Esmeralda. The novel has been adapted into numerous films, stage productions, and other media, and has become a classic of French literature.

One critique of the novel is its length and sometimes slow pacing. The story can be difficult to follow at times, with long descriptions and detailed historical information that may not be relevant to the main plot. Additionally, some readers may find the novel's themes of religion, politics, and morality to be heavy-handed or didactic.

Another critique is the portrayal of the character of Esmeralda, who is often objectified and sexualized by male characters in the story. Her character is often defined by her physical appearance and her relationships with men, rather than her own agency and personality. This can be seen as a reflection of the patriarchal and sexist attitudes of the time in which the novel was written.

Furthermore, some readers may find the treatment of Quasimodo to be problematic. Although he is portrayed as sympathetic and heroic, his deformity is often used as a source of pity or even amusement by other characters. This can be seen as perpetuating negative stereotypes and attitudes towards people with disabilities.

Despite these critiques, "The Hunchback of Notre Dame" remains a powerful and thought-provoking work of literature. Its exploration of themes such as social justice, love, and redemption, as well as its vivid depiction of medieval Paris, continue to resonate with readers today.

27. reflection about the hunchback of notre dame​


"The Hunchback of Notre Dame" is a novel written by Victor Hugo in 1831, which has since become a classic of French literature. The story takes place in Paris in the 15th century and centers around the character of Quasimodo, a deformed and physically challenged man who lives in the Notre-Dame cathedral and is tasked with ringing the bells.

One of the central themes of the novel is the conflict between appearance and reality, as well as the idea that society often judges people based on their outward appearance rather than their true character. Quasimodo is mistreated and ridiculed by many because of his physical deformities, yet he possesses a kind heart and a deep sense of compassion.

The novel also touches on themes of power, love, and betrayal, as well as the idea of societal hierarchy and class divides. The character of Esmeralda, a beautiful and kind-hearted dancer, becomes the object of desire for many, including the villainous Archdeacon Claude Frollo, leading to a tragic and heartbreaking ending.

Overall, "The Hunchback of Notre Dame" is a powerful and thought-provoking novel that raises important questions about societal norms and values, and the treatment of those who do not fit within them. It is a timeless tale that continues to resonate with readers today.
