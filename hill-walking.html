7. A girl walked for 30 minutes. She noticed that she traveled farther in the first 15 minutes of her walk than in the second 15 minutes. What can she conclude about her walk? A She walked over many hills. B Her average speed was faster during the first half of her walk. C She walked in two different directions. D She was walking at a constant speed.


Answer:

B. Her average speed was faster during the first half of her walk

Explanation:

According to straight line motion, the speed is directly proportional to the distance (s = d/t). Therefore, if there is an increase in speed, the distance increases also when time is constant.

Since the problem states that the girl noticed that she walked farther on her first 15 minutes. This means that she walked faster than the second 15 minutes of her walk.

To observe more, let us use computations to examine the connection between speed and distance.

Example:

1st scenario:

Speed = 2 m/s

Time = 15 minutes x 60s/ 1 minute = 900 s

d = ?

Solution:

Speed = d/t

2 m/s = d /900 s

d = speed x time

d = (2m/s)(900 s)

d = 1,800 m

2nd scenario:

Speed = 3 m/s

Time = 15 minutes = 900 s

d = ?

Solution:

Speed = d/t

d = speed x time

d = (3 m/s)(900 s)

d = 2,700 m

Therefore, through computation, we can say that an increase in speed means increase in distance.

16. A child pulls a sled up a snow covered hill. the child does 405 j of work on the sled. If the child walks 15m up the hill, how large of a force must the child exert?


Answer:

[tex] \therefore [/tex] The large amount of force must child exert is 27 Newtons.

Explanation:

To find how large of force that the child exert, we are going to use the derived Work Formula below;

[tex] \boxed{\bold{F = \frac{W}{d}}} [/tex]

Given values from the problem:

Work done is 405JDistance of the Child is 15m

Substitute the given values to the formula.

[tex] \rm{F = \frac{W}{d} = \frac{405}{15}=27N} [/tex]

[tex] \therefore [/tex] The large amount of force must child exert is 27 Newtons.
