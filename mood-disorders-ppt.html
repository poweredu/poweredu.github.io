8. introduction about mood disorder of young people during pandemic​


Explanation:

Mood disorders, such as depression and anxiety, are on the rise among young people during the COVID-19 pandemic. This is due to a variety of factors, including fear of the virus, loss of social contact, disruption of routines, economic uncertainty, and the lack of in-person social interaction. Symptoms of mood disorders can include changes in sleep patterns, loss of interest in activities, changes in eating habits, irritability, and difficulty concentrating. It is important for young people to be aware of these changes and seek professional help if needed.

Answer:

The COVID-19 pandemic has had a significant impact on the mental health of people worldwide, including young people. Many young people have experienced disruptions in their daily routines, social isolation, and increased stress and anxiety due to the pandemic's effects on education, employment, and family life. As a result, there has been a rise in mood disorders among young people, including depression and anxiety. These disorders can have a significant impact on young people's lives, affecting their academic performance, social relationships, and overall quality of life. In this context, it is essential to raise awareness of these issues and provide support and resources to young people who may be struggling with their mental health during these challenging times.

24. Research paper of the impact in the pandemic on mood disorders and suicide


Beyond the unprecedented health emergency, the COVID 19 pandemic represents a new big challenge for mental health researchers and clinical practitioners. During the coronavirus COVID-19 pandemic, a large number of people have been severely impacted in terms of development of mood disorders. On top of that, many people have suffered anxiety, depression, sadness, sleep problems and perceived social isolation.

Mood disorders are rising exponentially due to fatalities, economic turndown and isolation. Little attention is also given to health workers and practitioners, who are severely affected and overwhelmed by this emergency. Considering the global number of deaths - about 380,000 - the entire world is affected by these processes in different and faceted ways. Confinement, future instability, and fear not only exacerbated pre-existing issues, such as domestic violence, social phobias and depressive symptoms, but also trigger new psychopathological problems, sometimes escalating even in suicide.

The goal of this Research Topic is to analyze the overall effects of the pandemic on mood disorders and their behavioral consequences, such as cases of self-injurious behaviors and suicide, from different multidisciplinary perspectives and cultures. The aim is to encourage open discussion about current challenges and to provide practical and clinical directions for future intervention to cope with the impact of COVID-19 on the mood disorders of healthcare professionals, victims and their relatives.

27. What is substance induced mood disorders?


Substance induced mood disorders

Substance induced mood disorder is a chronic disorder that requires regular treatment. It is a situation where a person uses illegal drugs or substances, prescribed drugs or drugs that can be purchased at pharmacies in inappropriate doses. In addition, people with substance induced mood disorder also consume excessive doses of alcohol. Patients can stop or change their habits. This is different from addiction. Addiction is a disease that makes it difficult for a person to escape dependence. Substances that are often abused include: cigarettes and other tobacco products, marijuana, cocaine, and alcohol.

A person can be called abusing prescription or over-the-counter drugs if they take drugs prescribed for someone else, take more than the recommended dose of drugs, and take drugs for non-medical reasons. Some of the symptoms experienced are loss of interest in things one used to enjoy, spending more time alone, disrupted sleep cycles, problems with social interactions, rapid emotional changes, and often making excuses or lying to use drugs.
