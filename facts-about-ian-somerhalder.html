2. UNDERSTANDING ABOUT NATURE'S NUMBER by: Ian Stewart ​


Answer:

Why is the universe so mathematical? This question, which may never have occurred to you, turns out to be one of the stickiest philosophical conundrums of the day.

The short answer is that no one knows the answer. No one knows why mathematics--an invention inside our heads--so accurately describes the physical world, the independent world outside

Step-by-step explanation:

In Stewart’s view, mathematics is the search for patterns in nature. Mathematics, he says, “is a more or less systematic way of digging out the rules and structures that lie behind some observed pattern or regularity.” He also tells us that one “function of mathematics is to organize the underlying patterns and regularities in the most satisfying way.”

“The most satisfying way”? That’s an interesting standard, as it seems largely subjective. Mathematical truths are independent of the human mind and human aesthetics. Or are they?

Stewart devotes one delightful chapter to explaining how the mathematics underlying the acoustics of a Stradivarius violin is the same as the mathematics underlying television

22. ian and lioness what are their ecological relationship​


Answer:

In biology, symbiosis is the relationship of two living organisms in an ecosystem.

Types of symbiosis:

Mutualism

Commensalism

Predation

Parasitism

Mutualism

This is the relationship between two organisms who benefits from each other.

Ex. Flowering plants and bees.

Commensalism

This is the relationship between two organisms where one benefits while the other is neither harmed nor benefited.

Ex. Orchids on a tree.

Predation

This is the relationship between two organisms where a prey is hunted by a predator to be a food.

Ex. Fox (predator) and rabbit (prey).

Parasitism

This is the type of relationship between two organisms where one benefits (parasite) while the other (host) is harmed.

Ex. Lice (parasite) in the human (host).

24. introduction to nature's number by ian stewart


Answer:

In “Nature's Numbers,” Ian Stewart presents many more, each charming in its own way. He shows, for example, how the oscillations of fireflies follow the same mathematical rules as the gaits of horses. “Nature's numbers,” he says, are “the deep mathematical regularities that can be detected in natural forms.”

Ian Stewart is a mathematician and prolific author, having written over 40 books on all aspects of maths, as well as publishing several guides to the maths used in Terry Pratchett’s Discworld books, writing half a dozen textbooks for students, and co-authoring a couple of science fiction novels.

Stewart writes in a marvellously clear style but, more importantly, he is interesting: he sees the world in an interesting way, in a mathematical way, and manages to convey the wonder and strangeness and powerful insights which seeing the world in terms of patterns and shapes, numbers and maths, gives you.
