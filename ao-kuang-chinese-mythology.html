3. the founder of music in chinese mythology was​


Answer:

Ling Lun

Explanation:

According to legends, the founder of music in Chinese mythology was Ling Lun who, at the request of the Yellow Emperor to create a system of music, made bamboo pipes tuned to the sounds of birds including the phoenix. A twelve-tone musical system was created based on the pitches of the bamboo pipes, the first of these pipes produced the "yellow bell" (黃鐘) pitch, and a set of tuned bells were then created from the pipes.

Archaeological evidence indicates that music culture developed in China from a very early period. Excavations in Jiahu Village in Wuyang County, Henan found bone flutes dated to 9,000 years ago, and clay music instruments called Xun thought to be 7,000 years old have been found in the Hemudu sites in Zhejiang and Banpo in Xi'an

The Hindu mythology of rasas in the music of India and the Chinese philosophy on music share some similarities and differences.

One major similarity is that both cultures view music as having a powerful effect on the emotions and psyche of individuals. In Hindu mythology, rasas are the different emotional moods or sentiments that are evoked by music, and they are believed to have a transformative effect on the listener's state of mind. Similarly, in Chinese philosophy, music is seen as a tool for cultivating and regulating emotions and for promoting harmony between individuals and society.

Another similarity is the importance of the spiritual and metaphysical dimensions of music. In Hindu mythology, music is believed to be a divine art form that connects the human soul with the divine, and it is often associated with religious rituals and ceremonies. In Chinese philosophy, music is seen as a reflection of the cosmic order and the natural world, and it is believed to have a profound influence on the human spirit and the balance of yin and yang.

However, there are also some differences between the two cultures' views on music. For example, the Hindu mythology of rasas emphasizes the individual's subjective experience of music, while the Chinese philosophy on music focuses more on the objective qualities of sound and rhythm. Additionally, Hindu music has a more improvisational and free-flowing structure, while Chinese music is more structured and formulaic.

Overall, while there are some similarities and differences between the Hindu mythology of rasas in the music of India and the Chinese philosophy on music, both cultures share a deep appreciation for music as a transformative and spiritual art form.
