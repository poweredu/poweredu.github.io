3. Read articles on indigenous science on the internet. Examine what is common among these indigenous science beliefs and practices...


Answer:

Indigenous science is a diverse set of beliefs and practices that are unique to the different cultures and communities of Indigenous peoples around the world. Despite their diversity, there are some commonalities that can be observed across different indigenous science traditions.

One common theme is the interconnectedness of all things. Indigenous peoples view the natural world as a complex web of relationships between humans, animals, plants, and the environment. This holistic approach emphasizes the importance of maintaining balance and harmony between different elements of the ecosystem.

Another common belief is the importance of oral traditions and the transmission of knowledge from one generation to the next. Indigenous peoples have developed sophisticated systems for passing down knowledge, including storytelling, song, dance, and ritual practices.

Indigenous science also places a strong emphasis on observation and experimentation. Indigenous scientists often have a deep understanding of the natural world, based on centuries of careful observation and experimentation. This knowledge is often passed down through apprenticeships and other forms of informal learning.

Finally, indigenous science often includes spiritual and ceremonial practices. These practices are deeply rooted in the culture and worldview of Indigenous peoples and are often used to connect with the natural world and with ancestors.

In summary, common beliefs and practices found in indigenous science include interconnectedness, oral tradition, observation and experimentation, and spiritual and ceremonial practices. However, it is important to note that these beliefs and practices vary widely among different indigenous cultures and communities, and should not be seen as a monolithic or homogenous set of ideas.

5. Reflection of Indigenous science and technology in the Philippines


Indigenous science and technology in the Philippines refers to the traditional knowledge, practices, and technologies of the country's indigenous communities, which have been developed over generations through close observation of the natural environment and the application of local knowledge and resources. These practices and technologies are deeply rooted in the cultural and spiritual beliefs of the indigenous peoples and are often closely linked to their livelihoods, social organization, and community life.

In the Philippines, the indigenous peoples comprise a significant portion of the population and are recognized by the government as distinct cultural communities with their own rights and identities. Many of these communities have developed sophisticated systems of agriculture, medicine, architecture, and crafts that are adapted to their specific ecological and cultural contexts. For example, the Ifugao people of the Cordillera region are known for their terraced rice fields, which are not only aesthetically impressive but also highly efficient in conserving water and soil and maximizing crop yields. The T'boli people of Mindanao are skilled in weaving intricate patterns from abaca fiber, while the B'laan people are known for their knowledge of medicinal plants and their use in treating various ailments.

Despite the richness and diversity of indigenous science and technology in the Philippines, it has often been marginalized and undervalued in the face of the dominant Western scientific paradigm. Indigenous knowledge and practices have been dismissed as "primitive" or "superstitious" and have often been replaced by modern technologies and ways of life that are not always compatible with local ecosystems and cultures. This has led to the erosion of traditional knowledge and practices and has resulted in the loss of biodiversity and cultural diversity.

However, in recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the value of indigenous science and technology in promoting sustainable development and cultural diversity. Many indigenous communities are now actively involved in efforts to preserve and promote their traditional knowledge and practices, and there are also initiatives to incorporate indigenous knowledge into mainstream science and education. For example, the Department of Education in the Philippines has developed a curriculum that integrates indigenous knowledge and practices into science and social studies subjects, and there are also research programs that focus on documenting and studying indigenous science and technology.

15. what are the different indigenous burial practices in the Philippines?​


Answer:

During the Pre-Raphael period the early Filipinos believed in a concept of life after death.[1] This belief, which stemmed from indigenous ancestral veneration and was strengthened by strong family and community relations within tribes, prompted the Filipinos to create burial customs to honor the dead through prayers and rituals. Due to different cultures from various regions of the Philippines, many different burial practices have emerged. For example, the Manobos buried their dead in trees, the Ifugaos seated the corpse on a chari before it was brought to a cave and buried elsewhere.[2] The most common forms of traditional burials are supine pits, earthenware jars, and log coffins, and have been a topic of interest among Philippine archaeologists since the early 20th century.

Present-day Filipinos have retained the belief of life after death from their ancestors but generally practice Western religions such as Christianity. The most prominent contemporary practice of honoring the dead is by holding a wake and a following mourning period.[3] Modern traditions reflect indigenous values as well as influences of the Philippines' Spanish, American, and Chinese inhabitants.
