2. Comparison between ancient word problems to present word problems leading to quadratic equations


Answer:

In ancient times, word problems were often used for practical purposes such as measuring land areas or determining the quantity of goods to be exchanged in a market. These problems typically involved simple algebraic equations, such as linear equations, that could be solved using basic arithmetic.

However, as mathematics developed over time, word problems became more complex and began to involve quadratic equations. In present-day word problems, quadratic equations are commonly used to model real-life situations involving motion, physics, finance, and other areas.

For example, a present-day word problem might ask students to find the maximum height and distance of a ball thrown in the air. This problem can be modeled using a quadratic equation that describes the height of the ball as a function of time. By solving the equation, students can determine the maximum height and distance that the ball will reach.

In contrast, an ancient word problem might ask students to find the area of a field that is rectangular in shape but has one corner missing. This problem can be solved using a simple algebraic equation, such as A = l x w, where A represents the area, l represents the length, and w represents the width of the field.

Overall, the use of word problems has evolved over time as mathematics has become more advanced and complex. While ancient word problems often involved simple equations, present-day word problems frequently require the use of quadratic equations to model real-life situations.

17. 4. Give examples of word problems leading to quadratic equations during ancient times. Compare these with the present problems.


Answer:

During ancient times, word problems leading to quadratic equations were mainly related to geometry and were used to solve practical problems in construction and land surveying. Here are some examples:

Babylonian brick problem: A square courtyard is to be surrounded by bricks of length x. If the courtyard measures 40 by 40 units, and there are 2 rows of bricks on each side, how many bricks are needed? This problem leads to a quadratic equation of the form x^2 + 4x - 1600 = 0.

Archimedes cattle problem: Archimedes was asked by King Hiero to determine the number of cattle in his herd. Archimedes used a system of equations involving both linear and quadratic equations to solve the problem.

In the present time, word problems leading to quadratic equations are used in various fields such as physics, engineering, finance, and computer science. Here are some examples:

Projectile motion problem: A ball is thrown from a height of 5 meters with an initial velocity of 20 meters per second at an angle of 30 degrees with the horizontal. What is the maximum height reached by the ball? This problem leads to a quadratic equation of the form -5t^2 + 10t + 5 = 0, where t is the time taken for the ball to reach maximum height.

Investment problem: If an investment of $5000 earns a total interest of $1200 after 2 years, with $800 earned in the first year, what is the rate of interest? This problem leads to a quadratic equation of the form 6r^2 - 4r - 1 = 0, where r is the rate of interest.

Earliest methods used to solve quadratic equations:

Method used by the Babylonians: The Babylonians used a table of squares to solve quadratic equations. They used the table to find two numbers whose sum and product were equal to the given numbers in the equation. For example, if the equation was x^2 + 6x = 8, they would find two numbers whose sum is 6 and product is 8. They found that the numbers were 2 and 4, and hence x^2 + 6x = (x + 2)(x + 4) = 0.

Thomas Carlyle's geometric solution: Thomas Carlyle was a Scottish mathematician who found a geometric solution to quadratic equations. He used a rectangle whose length was x + a and width was x. He then divided the rectangle into two parts and rearranged them to form a square. This square had an area of x^2 + ax. He then equated the area of the square to a given constant to solve the quadratic equation.

Pythagorean geometric solution: The Pythagoreans used a geometric approach to solve quadratic equations. They drew a square whose side length was x and then divided it into two rectangles. The length and width of the rectangles were (x + a) and (x - a), respectively. The difference of the areas of the rectangles was equal to the given constant in the equation. They then used the Pythagorean theorem to solve for x.

The problem of finding the length and width of a rectangular garden whose area is 120 and whose diagonal is 17: This problem was solved by Thomas Carlyle using his geometric approach. He found that the length and width of the garden were 12 and 5, respectively.
