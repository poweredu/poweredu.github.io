12. Describe briefly the unique/distinct and salient of each communication model.Aristotle's ModelLasswell's ModelShannon-Weaver ModelOsgood-Shcramm ModelThe Wesley and Maclean ModelBarnlunds Transactional ModelDance's Helical Model​


Answer:

1. Aristotle’s Model

A framework for thinking about how to improve your communication abilities, by looking at key aspects underpinning a situation.

2. Lasswell’s Model

A basic framework for analyzing one-way communication by asking five questions: Who, said what, through which channel, to whom, with what effects?

3. Shannon-Weaver Model

The Shannon-Weaver model is the first to highlight the role of ‘noise’ in communication, which can disrupt or alter a message between sender and receiver.

4. Osgood-Schramm Model

The Osgood-Schramm model explores communication that is equal and reciprocal. It does not differentiate between the sender and receiver, but sees each as being in an equal position as message encoders and decoders.

5. The Westley and Maclean Model

The Westley and Maclean model shows that our communication is influenced by environmental, cultural and personal factors.

6. Barnlund’s Transactional Model

Barnlund’s Transactional Model of Communication highlights the role of private and public cues that impact our messages.

7. Dance’s Helical Model

Dance’s Helical Model sees communication as a circular process that gets more and more complex as communication occurs, which can be represented by a helical spiral.

15. Donabedian model is a conceptual model that provides a framework for examining health services


Explanation:

The model is named after the late Stanford sociologist and medical doctor, Donald Donabedian.

Donabedian model is a conceptual model that provides a framework for examining health services. It focuses on three different domains: "technical" or "measurable" quality (which includes structural elements such as adequate staffing levels, supplies, and equipment); "clinical" quality (which includes measures of the appropriateness of care according to best practices); and finally, “caring” quality which captures patients' perspectives about their interactions with providers. The components are measured by various tools including patient satisfaction surveys. The tools are often used by clinicians, researchers, and other stakeholders, but also by patients themselves.

The model has been applied to a wide range of health care settings including inpatient and outpatient hospital departments, primary care practices, nursing homes, community mental health centers and prisons. It has also been applied in other settings outside the medical sciences such as law enforcement agencies.

Donabedian model’s current use is mostly focused on outcomes research that compares the performance of different health care systems in terms of their ability to improve or maintain quality measures relevant to individual patients.


16. how the DNA model was conceptualized​


Answer:

The DNA model was conceptualized through a combination of observations, experiments, and theoretical insights by several scientists over many years.

In the early 1900s, researchers discovered that the genetic information of an organism is stored in its chromosomes, which are composed of both proteins and nucleic acids. In the 1940s, Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty showed that DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the genetic material responsible for heredity, as opposed to proteins.

In the early 1950s, Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins used X-ray crystallography to study the structure of DNA. They produced images that hinted at the molecule's double-helical shape, but they did not fully understand the significance of their findings.

Meanwhile, James Watson and Francis Crick, two young researchers in England, were working to decipher the structure of DNA. They used Franklin's and Wilkins' data, along with their own experimental results and theoretical insights, to construct a model of DNA's double helix. In 1953, they published a paper in the journal Nature describing the structure of DNA and its implications for how genetic information is stored and transmitted.

The DNA model proposed by Watson and Crick was groundbreaking because it provided a clear and elegant explanation for how genetic information is replicated and passed on from generation to generation. It also paved the way for advances in genetics, biotechnology, and medicine, as scientists began to understand more about how DNA works at the molecular level.
