12. CALCULATE THE NUMBER OF GRAMS OF SOLUTE NECESSARY TO PREPARE 100 ML OF A 0.15 N SODIUM SULFATE


Given:

desired volume = 100 mL Na₂SO₄ = 0.1 L Na₂SO₄

desired concentration = 0.15 N Na₂SO₄

mass of Na₂SO₄ = ?

Solution:

normality (N) = molarity (M) x factor

for Na₂SO₄, factor = 2

normality = molarity x 2

0.15 N = molarity × 2

molarity = 0.15 N/2

molarity = 0.075 M

molarity = moles of solute/L of solution

molarity = (mass of solute/molar mass of solute)/L of solution

(molarity)(L of solution) = mass of solute/molar mass of solute

mass of solute = (molarity)(L of solution)(molar mass of solute)

mass of solute = (0.075 M)(0.1 L Na₂SO₄)(142.04 g/mol)

mass of solute = 1.0653 g Na₂SO₄

17. Benzoic acid + sodium sulfate sublimation


Benzoic acid is a common preservative, while sodium chloride is one of humankind's most ancient and popular seasonings. You can separate a mixture of these two compounds by exploiting the difference in solubility. Benzoic acid is poorly soluble in cold water, while sodium chloride dissolves well in water even at cold temperatures. Many high school or college intro labs involve an experiment of this kind to teach students how to separate components of a mixture.


Transfer the sample of benzoic acid and sodium chloride to one of the 250 ml beakers.


Add 75 ml of water.


Stir the mixture to dissolve the salt.


Prepare an ice water bath in the 1-liter beaker. Place the 250 ml beaker in the ice water bath, but without allowing it to tip over or take in water from the ice bath. Continue to stir the mixture.


Place a piece of filter paper in the funnel, and dampen it slightly so it adheres to the funnel. Place the empty 250 ml beaker beneath it, and pour the mixture through the filter paper in the funnel. The benzoic acid, which did not dissolve, will remain in the filter paper, while the sodium chloride solution will pass through.

20. The fertilizer ammonium sulfate, (NH4)2SO4, reacts with sodium hydroxide to produce sodium sulfate, water and ammonia. Determine the mass of sodium hydroxide needed to react with 15.0 g of ammonium sulfate.​


Solution:

Step 1: Write and balance the chemical equation.

(NH₄)₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O + 2NH₃

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of substances needed.

For (NH₄)₂SO₄

molar mass = (14.01 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 8) + (32.07 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 4)

molar mass = 132.15 g/mol

For NaOH

molar mass = (22.99 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 1) + (1.008 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 40.00 g/mol

Step 3: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄ : 2 mol NaOH

Step 4: Calculate the mass of NaOH needed by using the mole ratio.

[tex]\text{mass of NaOH = 15.0 g (NH₄)₂SO₄} × \frac{\text{1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄}}{\text{132.15 g (NH₄)₂SO₄}} × \frac{\text{2 mol NaOH}}{\text{1 mol (NH₄)₂SO₄}} × \frac{\text{40.00 g NaOH}}{\text{1 mol NaOH}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of NaOH = 9.08 g}}[/tex]

25. Write the equations of the reaction to obtain the Schweizer reagent, given copper sulfate solution, sodium hydroxide solution and ammonia solution.​


Answer:

CuSO₄ + 2NaOH → Cu(OH)₂↓ + Na₂SO₄

Cu(OH)₂ + 4NH₃ → [Cu(NH₃)₄](OH)₂

Explanation:

Sodium hydroxide solution is added over the copper (II) sulfate solution. A gelatinous blue precipitate of copper (II) hydroxide is formed.

Ammonia solution is added to the mixture resulting from the first reaction. The copper (II) hydroxide precipitate reacts with ammonia to form tetraaminocopper (II) hydroxide, also called Schweizer's reagent - a complex, water-soluble, deep blue combination that can dissolve cellulose.

27. 2NaOH + H2SO4 → 2H2O + Na2SO4 How many grams of sodium sulfate will be formed, if you start with 1.50 L of a 4.0 M solution of sodium hydroxide?


Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of NaOH.

[tex]\text{moles of NaOH = molarity × volume of solution (L)}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of NaOH = 4.00 M × 1.50 L}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of NaOH = 6.00 mol}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of Na₂SO₄.

molar mass = (22.99 g/mol × 2) + (32.07 g/mol × 1) + (16.00 g/mol × 4)

molar mass = 142.05 g/mol

Step 3: Determine the mole ratio needed.

mole ratio = 2 mol NaOH : 1 mol Na₂SO₄

Step 4: Calculate the mass of Na₂SO₄.

[tex]\text{mass of Na₂SO₄ = 6.00 mol NaOH} × \frac{\text{1 mol Na₂SO₄}}{\text{2 mol NaOH}} × \frac{\text{142.05 g Na₂SO₄}}{\text{1 mol Na₂SO₄}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of Na₂SO₄ = 426 g}}[/tex]

Further explanation

A reaction coefficient is a number in the chemical formula of a substance involved in the reaction equation. The reaction coefficient is useful for equalizing reagents and products.  

The reaction coefficient shows the mole ratio of the compounds involved in the reaction  

Mole can be found from the amount of substance mass and its molar mass

[tex]\tt mole=\dfrac{mass}{molar~mass}[/tex]

Reaction :

[tex]\tt 3Na_2SO_4+2Al(NO_3)_3\rightarrow 6NaNO_3+Al_2(SO_4)_3[/tex]

a. 15 grams of Al(NO₃)₃

molar mass Al(NO₃)₃ : 212.996 g/mol

mole Al(NO₃)₃ :

[tex]\tt mole=\dfrac{15}{212.996}=0.07[/tex]

mole Na₂SO₄ : (mole ratio Na₂SO₄ : Al(NO₃)₃ = 3:2)

[tex]\tt \dfrac{3}{2}\times 0.07=\boxed{\bold{0.105}}[/tex]

b. 2.75 moles of Na₂SO₄

mole Al(NO₃)₃ :

[tex]\tt \dfrac{2}{3}\times 2.75=\boxed{\bold{1.83}}[/tex]

c. 120 grams of Al₂(SO₄)₃

molar mass Al₂(SO₄)₃ : 342.15 g/mol

mole of Al₂(SO₄)₃

[tex]\tt \dfrac{120}{342.15}=0.351[/tex]

mole of Na₂SO₄

[tex]\tt \dfrac{3}{1}\times 0.351=1.053[/tex]

mass of Na₂SO₄ :

[tex]\tt 1.053\times 142.04=\boxed{\bold{149.57~gr}}[/tex]
