3. Who said this line and why? "My only love sprung from my only hate. Too early seen unknown and known too late"​


Answer: Hereee

Explanation:

Here is your answer

This line is spoken by Juliet in William Shakespeare's play, "Romeo and Juliet." She says this line upon discovering that the man she has fallen in love with, Romeo, is a member of the rival family, the Montagues. It expresses her confusion and sorrow at the realization that her love has grown out of hate, one of the many tragic ironies that underscore the play's themes of love, fate, and the consequences of impulsive actions. Juliet realizes that their love may be doomed from the start due to the animosity between their families, the Capulets and the Montagues. This line is notable for its use of oxymorons, in which contradictory terms are used together, such as "only love" and "only hate." Shakespeare often used these kinds of contrasts to convey the nuances of human emotions and themes in his plays.

10. 2. The daughter of Zeus who sprung from his head​


Answer:

Athena.

The story of Athena’s birth is first retold in Hesiod’s Theogony, a poem about the genealogy of the gods. The Iliad alludes to it, but doesn’t give details — probably because his audience was already familiar with the myth.

After defeating his own father Cronos and taking the power, Zeus married Metis (that is, Wisdom) and she got pregnant. Zeus feared that his own child could overthrow him, and asked Heaven and Earth for advice.

Heaven and Earth told him to swallow the pregnant Metis, in order to avoid the child’s birth. Zeus tricked Metis and somehow managed to swallow her, but this could not prevent her from delivering the child in her womb.

After a while, Athena emerged from Zeus’s forehead. In some retellings of the myth, Zeus had either Prometheus or Hephæstus to cut open his head so that the daughter could come out. She was born wearing weapons.
