compare and contrast the following media platforms below based on the following aspects.

Answer:

Here are a few examples of ethnographic research in action:

1. Social Media Analytics

Social media is used by 2.3 billion people and any one Internet user has on average 5.54 social media accounts.

If we look at Twitter alone, there are 500 million tweets sent each day and Twitter has 310 million active users each month. This demonstrates the volume of consumer feedback available to researchers. Social media posts are unprompted — there is no direction from a researcher on content. Posts are shared organically on topics which are important to the consumer at that moment in time. This makes social analytics a great example of digital ethnography.

2. Eye Tracking

What better way to understand a consumer’s natural behaviour than to see what they see. Eye tracking has several applications in market research from understanding shopper behaviour, to measuring marketing effectiveness, to exploring how consumers interact with digital content. All that is required is for participants to wear glasses which track their eyes movements as they shop, browse the Internet, etc.

3. Scrapbooks

Whilst scrapbooks are not as sophisticated as eye tracking, they are just as effective in allowing consumers to show you what surrounds them, what attracts their attention and what they find visually appealing. Participants simply submit photos of items, places, or situations that stand out to them or they feel had a significant influence on their decision. In this way, researchers can once again immerse themselves in consumer’s environment.

4. Discovery Forums

Whilst “a picture is worth a thousand words”, sometimes it’s the words that count. Describing day-to-day routines, behaviour around the house or interactions with particular people (e.g. family members) is sometimes done more easily in words. In addition, the anonymity created by an online environment encourages consumers to open up and write in great depth.

5. Vox Pops

Another format of digital ethnography is Vox Pop videos. This activity uses the high consumer engagement with smartphones and their sophistication to the researcher’s advantage. Participants record short video messages where they ‘think out loud’ and share those thoughts with a researcher almost instantly. This activity is also a very good way for participants to show how they do things: for example, how they interact with a product for the first time. Do they read instruction manual or dive right in and figure it out via trial and error?

6. Online Diaries

Those of you who kept a diary know what a great way they are to record your daily experiences. Online diaries are therefore an excellent way of getting to know your customers. By their nature, online diaries allow researchers to read entries ‘in situ’, giving them an immediate insight into customer lives. For best practices and more benefits of online diaries in market research, visit Annette Smith’s blog: All you need to know about online diary studies.

17. how relevant is observation to ethnography​


How relevant is observation to ethnography Because ethnography may help in the investigation of extremely complex or important design problems. When studying and/or communicating with target groups in their real-life environment, a successful researcher is important.

What is ethnographic research?

Ethnographic research is a qualitative approach in which researchers observe and/or communicate with subjects in a sample in their natural world. Ethnography was popularized by anthropology, but it is still used in a variety of social sciences. Ethnography is used to promote a designer's greater interpretation of the design issue – including the applicable domain, audience(s), procedures, priorities, and context(s) of usage – in the fields of usability, user-centered design, and service design. An ethnographic research conducted as part of a usability experiment aims to get "under the hood" of a design challenge (and all its associated issues). It is hoped that by accomplishing this, a planner would be able to fully comprehend the dilemma and, as a result, design a much superior solution.

Why use participant observation?

Participant observation, among other ethnographic approaches, is based on the classic methods used in early anthropology by Malinowski and others when they observed specific groups, mostly for years at a time, taking detailed notes. Inductive participant observation is typically used as part of an exploratory testing process, with the aim of generating theories from the results. It is often associated with the grounded theory approach, in which researchers return to the study territory with greater and deeper understanding. The power of participant observation to explain depth (thick description) and to better understand human behavior is its strength.

When to use ethnography

In the early stages of a user-centered architecture project, ethnography is most helpful. This is due to the fact that ethnography is concerned with gaining an interpretation of the architecture challenge. As a result, it makes more sense to perform ethnographic research at the start of a project to inform future design decisions (which will happen later in the user-centred design process). Ethnographic approaches (such as participant observation) may be used to test an actual design as well, but their true meaning comes from having an early understanding of the applicable domain, audience(s), procedures, objectives, and context(s) of usage.

22. why is ethnography essential in anthropology​


Ethnography is considered essential in anthropology because it allows anthropologists to study and understand the culture, customs, and everyday lives of a specific group of people in a holistic and emic (insider's) perspective. Through ethnography, anthropologists are able to gain in-depth knowledge of the people they are studying, including their beliefs, values, social norms, and practices. This information is used to make cross-cultural comparisons, contribute to the development of anthropological theories, and can also be applied to help address social issues. Ethnography also allows anthropologists to document and preserve the culture of a group of people, which is important for future generations.

25. similarities of ethnography and case study​


Answer:

The main difference between case study and ethnography lies in their intent and focus; case studies intend to uncover the tacit knowledge of culture participants whereas ethnographic studies intend to describe the nature of phenomena through detailed investigations of individual cases.
