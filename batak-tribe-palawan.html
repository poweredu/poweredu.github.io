18. can we say that bataks of palawan are half-naked? why?​


Answer:

Yes, because Palawan is the largest province in the Philippines and is home to several indigenous ethnolinguistic groups such as the Tagbanua, Palaw’an, Tao’t Bato, Molbog, Batak, Agutaynen, and Cuyonon.These groups of people live in remote villages throughout the province that are found in mountains and coastal areas. According to the province’s history, the ancestors of these indigents have occupied Palawan long before the Malay settlers from the Majapahit Empire of Indonesia arrived in the area during the 12th century.In the year 1962, a team of anthropologists from the Philippine National Museum unearthed various fossils at the Lipuun Point which is now commonly known as the Tabon Cave in the municipality of Quezon in Palawan.The team which was led by Dr. Robert Fox was able to discover the remains of Homo Sapiens that are believed to be 22, 000 to 24, 000 years old during that time. Those remains were then named “Tabon Man” and were, then, used for further study. The discovery of the Tabon Man and the fossils found in the cave gave way for Palawan to earn the title “The Cradle of Philippine Civilization”.

25. give atleast 1 scope of Palawan tribes ​


Answer:

One of the main scopes of Palawan tribes is their rich cultural heritage and traditional practices. Palawan, an island province in the Philippines, is home to several indigenous tribes, such as the Tagbanua, Batak, Palawano, and others, who have preserved their unique customs, rituals, and ways of life for generations.

The scope of Palawan tribes' cultural heritage includes their traditional knowledge of medicinal plants, agriculture, hunting, fishing, and handicrafts. Many of these tribes have deep spiritual beliefs and practices, including animism and reverence for nature, which are reflected in their daily lives and cultural ceremonies.

Palawan tribes also contribute to the biodiversity conservation efforts in the region. They have intricate knowledge of the local ecosystems, which they use in their traditional practices, such as sustainable farming methods, forest management, and marine resource conservation. Their traditional practices often align with modern conservation principles, making them important stakeholders in the protection of Palawan's unique and fragile environment.

Additionally, Palawan tribes provide opportunities for cultural exchange and learning, as they open their communities to visitors who are interested in their traditions and ways of life. This promotes cultural diversity, understanding, and appreciation among different communities, both locally and internationally.

Overall, the scope of Palawan tribes extends beyond their cultural heritage and includes their contributions to biodiversity conservation, sustainable resource management, and cultural exchange, making them significant stakeholders in the preservation of Palawan's cultural and natural heritage.
