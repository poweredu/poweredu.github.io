7. List at least six (6) activities that exemplify the ways on how cultural relativism mitigates ethnocentrism​


Answer:

Cultural relativism is the idea that one's beliefs and practices should be understood based on that person's own culture. Proponents of cultural relativism also tend to argue that the norms and values of one culture should not be evaluated using the norms and values of another.

Ethnocentrism is a perspective that promotes an individual's culture as the most efficient and superior. Hence, the individual who exhibits ethnocentrism feels that his or her culture is the most appropriate as compared to other cultures.

Cultural relativism can mitigate ethnocentrism through:

1. Cultural Acceptance

- Learn to accept that there are countless of cultures in every society, and your culture is just a part of the whole.

2. Religious tolerance

- Although your religion teaches you to worship only your God, it may not be a violation if at least you learn to tolerate other people's faith.

3. Respecting cultural differences

- You have to remind yourself that what is right  and acceptable to you may turn out to be wrong and unacceptable for others. Hence, you have to respect cultural differences.  

4. Avoiding Judgments

- Avoid making judgements just because some people think, speak, and act differently from the way you think, speak, and act.

5. Not thinking highly of your own culture

- Avoid thinking highly of your own culture, because you might end up thinking that your culture is superior while other cultures are inferior.  

6. No room for racism and discrimination

- Just because your white and he's black, you're tall, and his short, you're highly educated while he's not  doesn't give you the right to discriminate or treat him badly. We have to do away with racism and discrimination because it only reflects on what kind of people we really are.

"Cultural relativism" can mean several different things, and much of the debate over its desirability can be traced to this ambiguity. Descriptive cultural relativism is basically undeniable: Cultures do, in fact, differ in their social and moral norms. But normative culture relativism is not as obviously true: It isn't clear how, if at all, our treatment of people in different cultures should vary based on their culture's norms. So the normative sense of cultural relativism that says we ought to judge other people by the standards of their own culture could be right, but isn't necessarily.

21. list activities that exemplify the ways on how cultural relativism mitigates ethnocentrism​


Answer:

1. Be Self-aware.

2. Educate yourself and others.

3. Listen.

4. Speak Up.

5. Review Team Norms.

6. Avoid Giving or Taking Offense.

7. Be Forgiving.

Explanation:

Be Self-aware. Acknowledge the advantages or disadvantages you have. Recognize your ethnocentric attitudes and how strong they are. This awareness opens the way for you to modify actions and attitudes as needed and understanding the need for change.

Educate. Read, attend lectures, presentations, and training sessions designed to help interaction between different ethnic groups. Interact with members of a different ethnic group and ask questions to gain understanding.

Listen. Seek to understand before trying to make yourself understood. If need be restate or reframe questions and comments from the other person's point of view to gain a greater understanding.

Speak Up. Using appropriate methods, identify stereotypes, prejudices and discrimination when they occur.

Review Team Norms. Periodically review your teams norms, goals, and expectations to determine if they are built on inclusion and pluralism or if a subgroup has formed that tends to dominate other members in the group. Make adjustments as needed.

Avoid Giving or Taking Offense. Change will take time and effort. Everyone makes mistakes and may need multiple times to alter their behavior. If you are aware that the comment you are about to make may be offensive, take a moment to determine another way to say it or frame the comment or question in terms of better understanding and intent not to give offense.
