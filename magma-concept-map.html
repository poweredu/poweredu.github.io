Further explanation:

On the surface of the planet, rocks are solid masses that form naturally and are composed of different minerals. They make up a sizable portion of the upper mantle and a smaller portion of the earth's crust. Rocks can be categorized into three primary groups according to how they were formed: igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic. Around 15% of the Earth's current land surface is made up of igneous rocks, 73% of it is covered by sedimentary rocks, and 12% of it is covered by metamorphic rocks.

What are Igneous rocks?

Magma, volcanic ash, and other volcanic materials are used to create igneous rocks. The magma in the earth's mantle erupts during a volcanic eruption as lava on the surface of the planet, where it solidifies to produce extrusive igneous rocks. Another method involves magma seeping into the earth's crustal fractures of pre-existing rocks, where it crystallizes to create intrusive igneous rocks. Depending on their size and shape, intrusions can be classified as batholiths, stocks, laccoliths, sills, dikes, etc. Basalt, pumice, scoria, etc. are examples of extrusive rocks, while granite, gabbros, diorite, etc. are intrusive rocks.

Rocks that are igneous can be recognized by their texture, density, color, and mineral makeup. Its texture is influenced by the rock's size, form, cooling and solidification process, and crystallinity distribution. When heated, molten rock (magma), which is derived from the Greek word for "fire," crystallizes and solidifies, and igneous rocks are created. Magma forms close to active plate boundaries or hot regions deep within the Earth. Lava is the term for magma that rises to the surface.

What are Sedimentary rocks?

Minerals and biological material build up in layers on top of one another over time to form sedimentary rocks. Sandstone, conglomerate, dolomite, flint, iron ore, limestone, shale, and coal are a few examples of sedimentary rocks. On the other hand, metamorphic rocks are created when heat and pressure cause changes in the minerals and geological texture of pre-existing rocks. Anthracite, gneiss, hornfels, marble, phyllite, schist, slate, etc. are some examples of metamorphic rocks.

Viscosity of Magmas

Viscosity depends on primarily on the composition of the magma, and temperature. Higher SiO2 (silica) content magmas have higher viscosity than lower SiO2 content magmas (viscosity increases with increasing SiO2 concentration in the magma).14 Sep 2015

Explanation:

The composition of magma depends on the rock it was formed from (by melting), and the conditions of that melting. Magmas derived from the mantle have higher levels of iron, magnesium, and calcium, but they are still likely to be dominated by oxygen and silicon. All magmas have varying proportions of elements such as hydrogen, carbon, and sulphur, which are converted into gases like water vapour, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen sulphide as the magma cools.
