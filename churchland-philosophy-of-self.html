13. The self is the brain of Churchland your own view​


Paul Churchland is a Canadian philosopher who thinks that people should improve the way they think about and use words to describe themselves. He thinks that the way our brains work is what makes us who we are. Churchland's work is all about questioning the ideas and terms that are used to explain behavior or how a person feels, thinks, and acts in relation to what's going on in their body and how they define themselves because of their emotions. This is one of the ideas behind Folk Psychology, which is also called "common sense psychology."

Eliminative materialism is the main idea that Churchland is based on. Eliminative materialism basically says that people's common understanding of the mind is wrong and that most of the mental states that people believe in do not actually exist. This idea also applies to how behavior and emotions are understood. This led to his idea of neurophilosophy. He thought that to fully understand a person's behavior, one should know how the brain's nerve cells move in response to different emotions, actions, and reactions, and how these brain movements affect the body.

With this in mind, we can get rid of the vagueness of identifying the mind, behavior, and self in general in a way that is subjective and has no basis. By learning about the different neural pathways, how they work, and what those movements mean for people, we will not only have proof that behavior can be measured, but also that the constant movement of the brain can be the basis of who a person is. Churchland and his wife talk about this in their paper "The Brain as the Self."

18. self-knowledge philosophy


Answer:

In philosophy, “self-knowledge” standardly refers to knowledge of one’s own sensations, thoughts, beliefs, and other mental states. At least since Descartes, most philosophers have believed that our knowledge of our own mental states differs markedly from our knowledge of the external world (where this includes our knowledge of others’ thoughts). But there is little agreement about what precisely distinguishes self-knowledge from knowledge in other realms. Partially because of this disagreement, philosophers have endorsed competing accounts of how we acquire self-knowledge. These accounts have important consequences for a broad range of philosophical issues, especially issues in epistemology and the philosophy of mind.

DUALISM

So here's a question. Why do people say 'read my mind' instead of 'read my brain'? Along the same lines, why is it 'brain surgery' instead of 'mind surgery'?

Interestingly, we can find an answer in today's lesson as we explore the works of Paul Churchland, specifically his theories on self and the brain. Since Churchland is a modern-day philosopher who studies the brain, let's first take a look at some older philosophical theories on the subject.

For much of history, many western philosophers have held to the theory of dualism. When it comes to discussing human life, dualism is the idea that the mind and the body are separate.

In other words, we all have a physical brain, but we also have a separate mind. Adding to this distinction, dualists have historically asserted the mind is the seat of our consciousness. On the contrary, the brain is really just an organ similar to the heart or lungs.

Because the mind is the seat of our consciousness, it's what gives us our identity. No, we can't see it, taste it, or touch it, but it does exist. Not only does it exist, but it is what makes self, self.
