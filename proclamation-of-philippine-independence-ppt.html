14. Reading the proclamation of the Philippine independence​


The Proclamation of Philippine Independence was read on June 12, 1898, by General Emilio Aguinaldo, the country's first president. The event marked the end of more than 300 years of Spanish colonial rule in the Philippines.

The proclamation was read from the balcony of Aguinaldo's residence in Kawit, Cavite, in front of a large crowd of Filipinos who had gathered to witness the historic event. The Philippine national anthem was also played for the first time, and the Philippine flag was raised to signify the country's independence.

The proclamation declared the Philippines as a sovereign state and called on all Filipinos to defend their newfound freedom. It also recognized the support of foreign nations, particularly the United States, in the struggle for independence.

However, the Philippine independence was short-lived. In 1899, the Philippine-American War broke out, and the United States took control of the country. It was only on July 4, 1946, that the Philippines was granted full independence by the United States.

Today, the proclamation of Philippine independence is celebrated every June 12 as a national holiday in the Philippines. It is a reminder of the bravery and determination of the Filipino people in their fight for freedom and sovereignty.

With the public reading of the Act of the Proclamation of Independence of the Filipino People (Spanish: Acta de la proclamación de independencia del pueblo Filipino; Filipino: Kasulatan ng Pagpapahayag ng Kasarinlan ng Sambayanang Pilipino), Filipino revolutionary forces under General Emilio Aguinaldo proclaimed the sovereignty and independence of the Philippine Islands from the colonial rule of Spain. Independence was proclaimed on 12 June 1898 between four and five in the afternoon in Cavite at the ancestral home of General Emilio Aguinaldo some 30 kilometres (19 mi) south of Manila. The event saw the unfurling of the Flag of the Philippines, made in Hong Kong by Marcela Agoncillo, Lorenza Agoncillo, and Delfina Herboza, and the performance of the Marcha Filipina Magdalo, as the national anthem, now known as Lupang Hinirang, which was composed by Julián Felipe and played by the San Francisco de Malabon marching band.
