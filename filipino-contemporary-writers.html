3. Short review about a work of a contemporary filipino writer.​


"Insurrecto" by Gina Apostol is a remarkable work of contemporary Filipino literature that challenges the notion of truth and history. The novel follows the story of two women - one a Filipino translator and the other an American filmmaker - who are working on a movie about the Philippine-American War. As they travel to different historical sites in the Philippines, they engage in a series of conversations that reveal the complex and often contested nature of history.

Apostol's writing style is playful and experimental, incorporating footnotes, multiple narrators, and a non-linear narrative structure. Through these literary devices, she explores the themes of identity, memory, and the power of language to shape our understanding of the past.

"Insurrecto" is a unique and thought-provoking work that challenges readers to question their own assumptions about history and the stories we tell ourselves about our lives and the world around us. It is a must-read for anyone interested in contemporary Filipino literature and the ongoing legacy of colonialism in the Philippines.

9. How did the American regime influence the development of Philippine literature, and give one author and his work that emerged during this period that continues to influence and inspire contemporary Filipino writers today?


Answer:

The American regime had a significant impact on the development of Philippine literature. When the Americans colonized the Philippines in 1898, they brought with them their language, culture, and educational system. This led to the spread of English language and literature, which gradually replaced Spanish as the dominant literary medium. The American regime also brought new literary forms and genres such as the short story, the novel, and free verse poetry, which were widely adopted by Filipino writers.

One author and his work that emerged during this period and continue to influence and inspire contemporary Filipino writers today is Jose Garcia Villa and his book of poetry titled "Have Come, Am Here" (1942). Villa is considered as one of the most influential Filipino writers of the 20th century and is known for his innovative use of language and form in poetry.

"Have Come, Am Here" is a collection of Villa's free verse poems that explore themes of love, death, and the human condition. The book is notable for its use of imagistic language, unconventional syntax, and vivid imagery. Villa's poems often contain striking and surprising images that challenge the reader's perceptions of reality.

Villa's work has had a profound influence on contemporary Filipino writers, particularly those writing in English. His innovative use of language and form has inspired many Filipino poets to experiment with their own writing and to push the boundaries of what is considered traditional or conventional in poetry. Villa's legacy has also paved the way for the emergence of contemporary Filipino poets who continue to explore new possibilities in language and form, and to use poetry as a means of expressing their unique experiences and perspectives as Filipinos

Contemporary Composition: "Tatsulok" by Bamboo Mañalac

"Tatsulok" is a song by Filipino rock band Bamboo, composed by the band's lead vocalist, Bamboo Mañalac. It was released in 2005 as part of their self-titled debut album.

The song's lyrics revolve around the themes of social inequality and the struggle for justice. The title "Tatsulok" means "triangle" in English, which is a metaphor for the unequal distribution of power and resources in Philippine society. The lyrics describe how the rich and powerful exploit and oppress the poor and powerless, creating an unjust and unequal society.

The song starts with a haunting guitar riff and gradually builds up to a powerful and energetic chorus, with Bamboo's powerful vocals driving the song forward. The music perfectly complements the lyrics, creating a sense of urgency and intensity that reflects the song's message.

Overall, "Tatsulok" is a powerful and socially conscious song that tackles important issues in Philippine society. Its catchy melody and driving rhythm make it a memorable and effective piece of social commentary.

Traditional Composition: "Pamulinawen" by Felipe Padilla de Leon

"Pamulinawen" is a traditional Ilocano folk song composed by Filipino composer Felipe Padilla de Leon. It is a love song that expresses the singer's deep affection for a woman named Mulinawen.

The song is known for its beautiful melody and poetic lyrics. The opening lines, "Pamulinawen, pusok imdengam man / Toy umas-asug agrayo ita sadiam," describe the singer's yearning for Mulinawen, comparing her to a beautiful flower that he longs to possess.

The song's melody is simple and sweet, with a gentle rhythm that perfectly captures the song's romantic and nostalgic mood. The use of traditional Ilocano instruments, such as the kudyapi and the banduria, further adds to the song's traditional flavor.

Overall, "Pamulinawen" is a timeless and beautiful love song that has been enjoyed by generations of Filipinos. Its simple melody and poetic lyrics make it a classic example of traditional Filipino music.

29. how can the experience of the Filipino writers during the contemporary and present period help you understand the present traits, traditions, and culture of filipinos?​


Answer:

The diversity and richness of Philippine literature evolved side by side with the country's history. This can best be appreciated in the context of the country's pre-colonial cultural traditions and the socio-political histories of its colonial and contemporary traditions.          

      The average Filipino's unfamiliarity with his indigenous literature was largely due to what has been impressed upon him: that his country was "discovered" and, hence, Philippine "history" started only in 1521.

      So successful were the efforts of colonialists to blot out the memory of the country's largely oral past that present-day Filipino writers, artists and journalists are trying to correct this inequity by recognizing the country's wealth of ethnic traditions and disseminating them in schools and in the mass media.

      The rousings of nationalistic pride in the 1960s and 1970s also helped bring about this change of attitude among a new breed of Filipinos concerned about the "Filipino identity."
