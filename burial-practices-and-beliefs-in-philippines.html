1. what are the different indigenous burial practices in the Philippines?​


Answer:

During the Pre-Raphael period the early Filipinos believed in a concept of life after death.[1] This belief, which stemmed from indigenous ancestral veneration and was strengthened by strong family and community relations within tribes, prompted the Filipinos to create burial customs to honor the dead through prayers and rituals. Due to different cultures from various regions of the Philippines, many different burial practices have emerged. For example, the Manobos buried their dead in trees, the Ifugaos seated the corpse on a chari before it was brought to a cave and buried elsewhere.[2] The most common forms of traditional burials are supine pits, earthenware jars, and log coffins, and have been a topic of interest among Philippine archaeologists since the early 20th century.

Present-day Filipinos have retained the belief of life after death from their ancestors but generally practice Western religions such as Christianity. The most prominent contemporary practice of honoring the dead is by holding a wake and a following mourning period.[3] Modern traditions reflect indigenous values as well as influences of the Philippines' Spanish, American, and Chinese inhabitants.

9. Describe the burial practice of Filipinos in the ancient times.


Answer:

The burial practices of ancient Filipinos varied across different ethnic groups and regions, but there were some commonalities. Many ancient Filipinos believed in an afterlife, and thus, they believed that the deceased needed to be properly prepared for their journey to the next life.

One common practice was the use of burial jars, which were large earthenware vessels that were used to hold the remains of the deceased. These jars were often decorated with intricate designs and patterns and were sometimes buried in groups.

Another common practice was the use of burial mounds, which were large piles of earth and stones that were used to cover the burial site. These mounds were often shaped like animals or other objects and were sometimes adorned with jewelry, weapons, and other items that were believed to be necessary for the afterlife.

In some regions, the dead were buried in caves, which were believed to be sacred places that were close to the spirit world. The bodies were often placed in a fetal position and were accompanied by various objects, such as pottery and jewelry.

Some ethnic groups also practiced secondary burial, which involved exhuming the bones of the deceased after a certain period of time and placing them in a special container, such as a wooden box or a cloth bag. The bones were often kept in a special place, such as a family home or a communal area.

Overall, the burial practices of ancient Filipinos were diverse and reflected their beliefs in the afterlife and the need to properly prepare the deceased for their journey to the next world.

The Greeks believed that the soul was immortal and would transcend the physical body after death, so the body itself was not considered as important. Instead, the Greeks would place coins or other offerings in the mouth of the deceased to pay the ferryman Charon to take them across the River Styx to the afterlife. The body was then typically cremated, and the ashes were placed in an urn or buried in a grave.
