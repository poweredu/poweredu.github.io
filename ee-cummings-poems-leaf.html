3. What is the meaning behind e.e cummings’ absence of spacing?


This lovely E. E. Cummings probably took its cue from the Japanese haiku form. The timeless idea of loneliness and the ephemeral motion of a falling leaf are connected in E. Cummings' poem. He eschewed conventional grammar and punctuation in favor of a dynamic use of language, even coining new words by fusing existing ones to convey new ideas.

In order to further emphasize his points, Cummings also used enjambment, or sentences that cross line breaks. For instance, in the poem's opening line, Cummings interrupts the notion in the middle of the phrase, leaving the reader waiting for it to be finished. One reason why poets utilize enjambment is to quicken the poem's pace or to evoke growing emotions in the reader as they are drawn from one line to the next.

In a poetry, enjambment increases the drama. The opening sentence ends on a cliffhanger rather than the conclusion of an idea, making the reader want to keep reading to find out what occurs next. Depending on the length of the enjambment, it resolves either in the second or third line. The borders of words, headings and sub-headers, paragraphs, and sections are marked by spaces in texts. By directing the reader's eye and thoughts, they serve to both visually reinforce the conceptual organization of a text and to speed up the process of perception.

22. what is the meaning of leaf subsides to leaf​


Answer:

Then leaf subsides to leaf. This line shows us what happens after the early leaf is no longer figuratively a flower—it becomes a true leaf. But the speaker doesn't say "becomes," he says "subsides." This means that the first leaf sank down, or settled, to become another leaf.
