15. in programming, what is definition and examples of qualitative data, quantitative data, anecdotal data, contextual data​


In programming, data can be classified into several categories based on their characteristics and properties. Some of the most commonly used categories are qualitative, quantitative, anecdotal, and contextual data. Here are the definitions and examples of each of these categories:

1. Qualitative data: Qualitative data is descriptive in nature and is used to provide an understanding of a phenomenon. It is non-numerical and cannot be measured. Examples of qualitative data include opinions, feelings, perceptions, and attitudes. Qualitative data is often collected through interviews, focus groups, and open-ended surveys.

2. Quantitative data: Quantitative data is numerical in nature and can be measured. It is used to quantify a phenomenon or event. Examples of quantitative data include height, weight, age, income, and the number of items sold. Quantitative data is often collected through surveys, experiments, and observational studies.

3. Anecdotal data: Anecdotal data is based on personal accounts or experiences and is often used to illustrate a point. It is not necessarily representative of a larger population and cannot be used to make generalizations. Examples of anecdotal data include stories, testimonials, and personal observations.

4. Contextual data: Contextual data provides additional information about the circumstances surrounding an event or phenomenon. It is used to provide a deeper understanding of the situation. Examples of contextual data include location, time, weather conditions, and socio-economic factors.

In summary, these categories of data are important to consider in programming because they can help determine the most appropriate data collection and analysis methods to use for a given problem or research question.

28. Create your own context-rich problem relevant to fluid, density and pressure. You may further contextualize the problem by using Philippine contextualization or localization. Give an example of context-rich problem with detailed solution


Context-rich problem:

The Magat Dam in Isabela has a height of 61 meters and a width of 1,100 meters. If the dam is filled with water up to a depth of 50 meters, what is the total force exerted by the water against the dam? Assume that the density of water is 1,000 kg/m³ and that the gravitational acceleration is 9.8 m/s².

Solution:

The first step is to compute for the volume of water in the dam:

Volume of water = area of base x depth

Area of base = width x length

Area of base = 1,100 m x 61 m

Area of base = 67,100 m²

Volume of water = 67,100 m² x 50 m

Volume of water = 3,355,000 m³

Next, we can compute for the weight of water:

Weight of water = mass x gravity

Mass = density x volume

Mass = 1,000 kg/m³ x 3,355,000 m³

Mass = 3,355,000,000 kg

Weight of water = 3,355,000,000 kg x 9.8 m/s²

Weight of water = 32,923,000,000 N

Finally, we can compute for the force exerted by the water against the dam:

Force = pressure x area

Pressure = weight / area

Pressure = 32,923,000,000 N / 67,100 m²

Pressure = 490,785 Pa

Force = 490,785 Pa x 67,100 m²

Force = 32,902,338,500 N

Therefore, the total force exerted by the water against the Magat Dam is approximately 32.9 billion Newtons. This is equivalent to the weight of about 3.3 million tons. This problem is relevant to the Philippines as the Magat Dam is a major source of hydroelectric power and irrigation water for the provinces of Isabela and Cagayan.
