27. The complete combustion of butane, (g)


COMPLETE COMBUSTION OF BUTANE

Butane is a colourless, odourless, and highly flammable hydrocarbon gas that is mainly used in lighters to provide fuel for the flame.  Due to the nature in which it is used in lighters as a source of fuel, I will be discussing the chemical process of complete combustion in order to describe why such phenomenon occur.

The balanced equation for the complete combustion of butane is as observed below:

As observed from the balanced chemical equation above, butane reacts with oxygen gas and combusts, forming carbon dioxide and water.  The butane gas is stored in a both a liquid and gaseous state in iso-equilibrium form, and when gas is required to create the flame the butane is pressurized and thus vapourized, turning it into a gas where it reacts with oxygen and flame to combust.  This is one of the fuels that can be used in lighters, which have widespread application around the world, whether it’d be starting a fire or lighting candles on your birthday cake.  Naturally, in a combustion chemical equation, the limiting reactant is oxygen, and the excess reactant butane.  When there is not enough oxygen to create a complete combustion, carbon monoxide is thus formed and is released as soot.

Incomplete combustion has potentially hazardous results, as the gas created by incomplete combustion is carbon monoxide which is highly toxic.  Not only is it harmful to humans, but it also poses a threat to plant life as well,  as the exposure to carbon monoxide leads to the reduced oxygen delivery to the body’s organs.  Serious cases of carbon monoxide poisoning may lead to death if not treated properly.


28. Butane burns in oxygen. How many grams of water vapor are produced by the combustion of 580 grams of butane at standard conditions?


Answer:

C4H10 + (13/2)O2 → 4CO2 + 5H2O

2C4H10 + 13O2 → 8CO2 + 10H2O

The theoretical yield from burning 1 mol (=58 g) C4H10 is 5 mol (=90 g) H2O

Thus, the theoretical yield of water from the combustion of 580 g C4H10 is 900 g.

Because masses were specified, the temperature and pressure specifications are unnecessary.
