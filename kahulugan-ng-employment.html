10. ano ang kahulugan ng full employment?​


Answer:

Full employment is an economic situation in which all available labor resources are being used in the most efficient way possible. Full employment embodies the highest amount of skilled and unskilled labor that can be employed within an economy at any given time.

True full employment is an ideal—and probably unachievable—situation in which anyone who is willing and able to work can find a job, and unemployment is zero. It is a theoretical goal for economic policymakers to aim for rather than an actually observed state of the economy. In practical terms, economists can define various levels of full employment that are associated with low but non-zero rates of unemployment.

Full employment refers to a situation in which every able bodied person who is willing to work at the prevailing rate of wages is, infact, employed. Alternatively, it is a situation when there is no involuntary unemployment.

17. ano ang kahulugan ng an employment rate


Ano ang kahulugan ng an employment rate

The employment rate is expressed as a ratio of employment to population. This is a statistical ratio that estimates the percentage of the country's working-age population (age 15 to 64) that is worked. This involves those who have given up searching for jobs. According to the International Labour Organization, a worker is called working if they have worked at least one hour of "gainful" jobs in the previous week.

The national statistical agency calculates and reports the employment-to-population ratio for the economy on a daily basis. It is usually measured using survey data collection and the responses of specific citizens to questions posed by a country's national agency for the economy and statistics. Few nations often provide comparative statistics on the number of working persons who are enrolled as taxpayers who must make mandatory social security premiums to a country's national social insurance fund, which could be used to measure a better performance metric of employed workers relative to the overall labor force.  The employment-population ratio has not always been looked at for labor statistics and where individual places are economically, but since the recent recession, it has received increased interest globally, especially from economists. According to the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER), the Great Recession ended in June 2009. However, many countries were already suffering economically in 2009 and 2010, which is why the employment-population figure is still used by Americans and citizens all over the world.  The ratio is used to assess the economy's potential to produce jobs and is hence used in combination with the unemployment rate to provide a general assessment of the labor market's stance. A high ratio indicates that a significant proportion of the working-age population is employed, which would have a positive impact on GDP per capita. However, the ratio does not indicate working conditions, the amount of hours spent per worker, earnings, or the scale of the black market. As a result, labor market research must be performed in accordance with other data.
