11. What is cosmic inflation theory


Answer:

Cosmic inflation is the idea that the very early universe went through a period of accelerated, exponential expansion during the first 10-35 of a second before settling down to the more sedate rate of expansion.

Answer:

In physical cosmology, cosmic inflation, cosmological inflation, or just inflation, is a theory of exponential expansion of space in the early universe. The inflationary epoch lasted from 10−36 seconds after the conjectured Big Bang singularity to some time between 10−33 and 10−32 seconds after the singularity. Following the inflationary period, the universe continued to expand, but at a slower rate. The acceleration of this expansion due to dark energy began after the universe was already over 9 billion years old (~4 billion years ago).

17. cosmic inflamation theory​


Answer:

Explanation:

is a theory of exponential expansion of space in the early universe. The inflationary epoch lasted from 10−36 seconds after the conjectured Big Bang singularity to some time between 10−33 and 10−32 seconds after the singularity. Following the inflationary period, the universe continues to expand, but at a less rapid rate.

Inflation theory was developed in the late 70's and early 80's, with notable contributions by several theoretical physicists, including Alexei Starobinsky at Landau Institute for Theoretical Physics, Alan Guth at Cornell University, and Andrei Linde at Lebedev Physical Institute. Alexei Starobinsky, Alan Guth, and Andrei Linde won the 2014 Kavli Prize “for pioneering the theory of cosmic inflation". It was developed further in the early 1980s. It explains the origin of the large-scale structure of the cosmos. Quantum fluctuations in the microscopic inflationary region, magnified to cosmic size, become the seeds for the growth of structure in the Universe (see galaxy formation and evolution and structure formation). Many physicists also believe that inflation explains why the universe appears to be the same in all directions (isotropic), why the cosmic microwave background radiation is distributed evenly, why the universe is flat, and why no magnetic monopoles have been observed.

Formation of Elements Complete the following nuclear reactions and Identify the cosmic phase (nucleosynthesis, stellar formation and evolution, or stellar explosion) In which the reaction occurred. 1. p + ²H+OY 2. He + ²H+ p 3.22Mg +28Si+y +. 4.3 Ar +Ca+Y 5. 110Cd + n 6. 115Cd+B+0Y 7. ¹2C+2He - - + 08 8.¹2C+¹2C20Ne +_____ - Cosmic Phase​


Answer:

p + ²H + O -> ³He + γ - Nucleosynthesis

This reaction involves the fusion of a proton (p) with a deuterium nucleus (²H) and an oxygen nucleus (O) to form a helium-3 nucleus (³He) and a gamma ray (γ) photon. This reaction occurs during nucleosynthesis, which is the process by which elements are formed in the early universe.

He + ²H + p -> ⁴He + γ - Nucleosynthesis

This reaction involves the fusion of a helium-4 nucleus (He) with a deuterium nucleus (²H) and a proton (p) to form a helium-4 nucleus (⁴He) and a gamma ray (γ) photon. This reaction also occurs during nucleosynthesis.

22Mg + 28Si + y -> 50Cr + n - Stellar Formation and Evolution

This reaction involves the capture of a neutron (n) by a nucleus of 22Mg and a nucleus of 28Si, resulting in the formation of a nucleus of 50Cr and the release of a gamma ray (y) photon. This reaction occurs during the formation and evolution of stars.

3Ar + Ca + y -> 38K + n - Stellar Formation and Evolution

This reaction involves the capture of a neutron (n) by a nucleus of ³⁸Ar and a nucleus of Ca, resulting in the formation of a nucleus of ³⁸K and the release of a gamma ray (y) photon. This reaction also occurs during the formation and evolution of stars.

110Cd + n -> 111Cd + γ - Stellar Formation and Evolution

This reaction involves the capture of a neutron (n) by a nucleus of ¹¹⁰Cd, resulting in the formation of a nucleus of ¹¹¹Cd and the release of a gamma ray (γ) photon. This reaction also occurs during the formation and evolution of stars.

¹¹⁵Cd + B + O -> 116Cd + γ - Stellar Explosion

This reaction involves the capture of a positron (B) and an electron (O) by a nucleus of ¹¹⁵Cd, resulting in the formation of a nucleus of ¹¹⁶Cd and the release of a gamma ray (γ) photon. This reaction occurs during a stellar explosion, such as a supernova.

¹²C + ²He -> ¹⁶O + γ - Stellar Formation and Evolution

This reaction involves the fusion of a nucleus of ¹²C and a nucleus of helium-4 (²He) to form a nucleus of oxygen-16 (¹⁶O) and the release of a gamma ray (γ) photon. This reaction also occurs during the formation and evolution of stars.

¹²C + ¹²C -> ²⁰Ne + n - Stellar Formation and Evolution

This reaction involves the fusion of two nuclei of ¹²C to form a nucleus of neon-20 (²⁰Ne) and the emission of a neutron (n). This reaction occurs during the formation and evolution of stars.


24. Shiva, the Cosmic-dancer​


Answer:

Nataraja, (Sanskrit: “Lord of the Dance”) the Hindu god Shiva in his form as the cosmic dancer, represented in metal or stone in many Shaivite temples, particularly in South India

In the most common type of image, Shiva is shown with four arms and flying locks dancing on the figure of a dwarf, who is sometimes identified as Apasmara (a symbol of human ignorance; apasmara means “forgetfulness” or “heedlessness”). Shiva’s back right hand holds the damaru (hourglass-shaped drum); the front right hand is in the abhaya mudra (the “fear-not” gesture, made by holding the palm outward with fingers pointing up); the back left hand carries Agni (fire) in a vessel or in the palm of the hand; and the front left hand is held across his chest in the gajahasta (elephant-trunk) pose, with wrist limp and fingers pointed downward toward the uplifted left foot. The locks of Shiva’s hair stand out in several strands interspersed with flowers, a skull, a crescent moon, and the figure of Ganga (the Ganges River personified as a goddess). His figure is encircled by a ring of flames, the prabhamandala. In classic Sanskrit treatises on dance, this form, the most common representation of Nataraja, is called the bhujungatrasa (“trembling of the snake”)
