Characters Javert

Javert is so obsessed with enforcing society’s

laws and morals that he does not realize he is

living by mistaken assumptions—a tragic and

ironic flaw in a man who believes so strongly

in enforcing what he believes is right. Although

Javert is such a stern and inflexible character

that it is hard to sympathize with him, he

lives with the shame of knowing that his own

Gypsy upbringing is not so different from the

backgrounds of the men he pursues. He lives his life trying to erase this shame through his strict commitment to upholding the law.

Javert’s flaw, however, is that he never stops to question whether the laws themselves are just. In his mind, a man is guilty when the law declares him so. When Valjean finally gives Javert irrefutable proof that a man is not necessarily evil just because the law says he is, Javert is incapable of reconciling this new knowledge with his beliefs. He commits suicide, plagued by the thought that he may be living a dishonorable life. True to Javert’s nature, he makes this decision not with any emotional hysterics, but rather with a cool determination. Although he is a man of logic, he is impassioned about his work. To this end, Hugo frequently uses animal imagery to describe Javert, particularly when he likens him to a tiger. In the end, it is difficult to feel anything other than pity for Javert, who assumes his duty with such savagery that he seems more animal than man.

What do you think gives him the grace to do so? And what challenges does he have in letting go?​


Answer:

1. In Les Miserables, Inspector Javert represents a rigid adherence to the law without room for mercy or forgiveness. This kind of thinking can be prevalent among some religious people who may see the world in black and white terms, with little room for nuance or compassion. This mindset can stem from a fear of deviating from what is perceived as "right" and a desire for control. However, this can lead to a lack of empathy and understanding for others, which can come across as unwelcoming and harsh.

To change this, those committed to Christianity can work towards cultivating empathy and compassion for others, recognizing that everyone has their own struggles and challenges. This can involve actively seeking out and learning from diverse perspectives, practicing empathy and active listening, and prioritizing kindness and understanding in interactions with others. Additionally, a focus on forgiveness and grace, as exemplified by Valjean's journey in the film, can help shift the focus from strict adherence to rules towards a more compassionate and forgiving approach.

2. Valjean's decision to let go of Cosette is rooted in his deep love for her and desire for her happiness. He recognizes that his presence in her life may ultimately bring her harm, and so makes the difficult decision to allow her to move on without him. Valjean's ability to let go is a testament to his growth and selflessness throughout the film, as he learns to prioritize the needs and happiness of others over his own desires.

However, letting go is not without its challenges. Valjean must confront his own feelings of loss and loneliness, and grapple with the idea that he may not be able to protect those he loves. Additionally, letting go requires a certain level of trust in the world and in others, which can be difficult for individuals who have experienced trauma or loss. Ultimately, Valjean's ability to let go is a reflection of his strength and resilience, as well as his commitment to doing what is best for those he cares about.
