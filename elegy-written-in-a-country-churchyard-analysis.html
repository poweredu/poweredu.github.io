3. What evidence from "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard" supports Gray's classification as a pre-Romantic. ?.


Answer:

The evidence from "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard" that supports Gray's classification as a pre-Romantic is the way that Gray uses rural settings and subjects to explore questions about life, death, and the role of the individual in the universe. Gray's use of language is also suggestive of pre-Romanticism, with its emphasis on emotion and feeling over reason and intellect.

The poem is set in a rural graveyard, and Gray uses the setting to explore themes of mortality and the role of the individual in the grand scheme of things. He asks questions about who these people were in life, and whether their lives had any meaning or purpose. He also reflects on the inevitability of death, and how it is the great leveler that ultimately leads us all to the same end.

The poem is written in a pastoral style, and uses language that is emotive and suggestive. Gray paints a picture of the quiet, humble lives of the people who are buried in the churchyard, and how they are now at peace in death. He reflects on how their lives were probably filled with toil and hardship, but how they nonetheless lived simple and honest lives.

Gray's use of rural settings and subjects, as well as his emotive language, suggest that he is a pre-Romantic poet.
