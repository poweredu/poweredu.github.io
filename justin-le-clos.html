1. How do I balance CrO4 2- + ClO- → Cr(OH) 3 + ClO 4- in a basic solution?


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Separate the equation into two half-reactions.

Note that the oxidation numbers of Cr in CrO₄²⁻ and Cr(OH)₃ are +6 and +3, respectively. (Reduction)

Note that the oxidation numbers of Cr in CrO₄²⁻ and Cr(OH)₃ are +6 and +3, respectively. (Reduction)Note that the oxidation numbers of Cl in ClO⁻ and ClO₄⁻ are +1 and +7, respectively. (Oxidation)

Oxidation: ClO⁻ → ClO₄⁻

Reduction: CrO₄²⁻ → Cr(OH)₃

Step 2: Balance each half-reaction for number and type of atoms and charges. Add H₂O to balance the O atoms and H⁺ to balance the H atoms.

Oxidation half-reaction: We add three H₂O molecules to the left-hand side of the arrow to balance the O atoms:

ClO⁻ + 3H₂O → ClO₄⁻

To balance the H atoms, we add six H⁺ ions on the right side:

ClO⁻ + 3H₂O → ClO₄⁻ + 6H⁺

There are now one negative charge on the left side and five positive charges on the right side. Therefore, we add six electrons on the right:

ClO⁻ + 3H₂O → ClO₄⁻ + 6H⁺ + 6e⁻

Reduction half-reaction: We add one H₂O molecule to the right-hand side of the arrow to balance the O atoms:

CrO₄²⁻ → Cr(OH)₃ + H₂O

To balance the H atoms, we add five H⁺ ions on the left side:

CrO₄²⁻ + 5H⁺ → Cr(OH)₃ + H₂O

There are now three positive charges on the left side and no charge on the right side. Therefore, we add three electrons on the left:

CrO₄²⁻ + 5H⁺ + 3e⁻ → Cr(OH)₃ + H₂O

Step 3: Add the two half-equations together and balance the final equation by inspection. The electrons on both sides must cancel. If the oxidation and reduction half-reactions contain different numbers of electrons, we need to multiply one or both half-reactions to equalize the number of electrons.

Since both half-reactions contain different numbers of electrons, we multiply the oxidation half-reaction by 1 and the reduction half-reaction by 2

Oxidation: ClO⁻ + 3H₂O → ClO₄⁻ + 6H⁺ + 6e⁻

Reduction: 2CrO₄²⁻ + 10H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr(OH)₃ + 2H₂O

Adding the two half-reactions, we obtain

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccccc} & & & & \text{ClO}^- & + & 3\text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & \text{ClO}_4^- & + & 6\text{H}^+ & + & 6e^- \\ & & 2\text{CrO}_4^{2-} & + & 10\text{H}^+ & + & 6e^- & \rightarrow & 2\text{Cr(OH)}_3 & + & 2\text{H}_2\text{O} \\ \hline 2\text{CrO}_4^{2-} & + & \text{ClO}^- & + & 4\text{H}^+ & + & \text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & 2\text{Cr(OH)}_3 & + & \text{ClO}_4^- \end{array}[/tex]

Step 4: Add equal number of OH⁻ ions on both sides of the equation.

Since there are four H⁺ ions, we add four OH⁻ ions on both sides of the equation:

2CrO₄²⁻ + ClO⁻ + 4H⁺ + H₂O + 4OH⁻ → 2Cr(OH)₃ + ClO₄⁻ + 4OH⁻

Final Step: Combine the H⁺ and OH⁻ ions to form water.

Hence, the balanced redox equation in basic medium is

[tex]\boxed{2\text{CrO}_4^{2-} + \text{ClO}^- + 5\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{Cr(OH)}_3 + \text{ClO}_4^- + 4\text{OH}^-}[/tex]
