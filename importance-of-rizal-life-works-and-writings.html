An Overview of Philippine Literature Historically speaking, Philippine literature can be divided into three most important periods: pre-colonial, colonial, and post-colonial All literary works produced before the Spanish colonization such as chants, proverbs, songs, and folk narratives are considered under pre-colonial period which are mostly of oral traditions. This is not because early Filipinos did not have system of writing yet but due to the unavailability of printing materials. Life was simple and so is literature during this period. If we have phones and other forms of technology to entertain us, our ancestors had literature for recreation who were noted for being music lover as more than 10 types of songs in various Philippine languages were discovered. Our forefathers also valued words of wisdom for teaching values as evidenced by numerous proverbs or salawikain Story telling was used to explain phenomenon and to teach lessons as well. Having bugtong or riddles was also a favorite past time and listening to narratives of bravery, magic, and heroism in epics was a box office hit. The focus of literature suddenly changed upon the arrival of Spaniards. An emphasis on morality and religion was common in literary forms such as Corrido, Pasyon, and Cenaculo. The production of printed materials started but it was mostly of religious purposes and themes. It was made in an attempt to instill traditional Spanish ways and cultures among the native Filipinos. Despite this indoctrination, native tradition still survived and flourished especially for areas where the colonial power was less popular. Literacy paved the way to develop sense of nationalism among Filipinos during the latter part of Spanish colonization. Filipinos started to write about oppression, corruption, and other social ills such as in the works of Dr. Jose P. Rizal entitled Noli Me Tangere and its sequel El Filibusterismo. The literary production during the American Period was inspired by the developments in education and culture. Knowledge and information became accessible to all Filipinos because of the free public education. During the apprenticeship period, the Filipino writers imitated English and American models. All forms of literature were explored by Filipino writers. During this period, short stories were the most prevalent literary form. With the new knowledge of English language, many writers started to gain recognition both locally and internationally like Jose Garcia Villa who earned the International title, "Poet of the Century" as an example. Philippine Literature was interrupted in its development when we were conquered by Japan between 1941-1945 since freedom of expression was uncommon back then. Filipino writers in the Philippines were forbidden to write anything against the government and only those who were in exile had the privilege to do so. As a result of banning the English language, short stories written in the vernaculars flourished in the country. The three most influential foreign invaders to the types of literature produced during colonial periods are Spanish, American, and Japanese wherein the common themes and subject mostly tackle about oppression, religion, freedom, or anything that reflects the experiences of Filipino people under the colonizers. The influences brought to us by colonial periods are said to have been extended up to the postcolonial periods especially in social and cultural dimensions. Post-colonial is the period after the rebirth of freedom in the Philippines up to the present. One of the most unforgotten and controversial periods during this era is having Martial Law during the Marcos regime. As a language model, I can provide you with three reasons why taking a course on the life, works, and writings of Jose Rizal, a national hero in the Philippines, can play an important role in the lives of Filipino youth:

Cultural Identity and National Pride: Studying the life, works, and writings of Rizal allows Filipino youth to understand and appreciate their own cultural identity and national heritage. Rizal's writings, such as his novels "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," are considered cornerstones of Philippine literature and reflect the social, political, and cultural context of his time. By learning about Rizal's contributions to Philippine history and his advocacy for social reforms and national liberation, Filipino youth can develop a sense of national pride and a deeper connection to their country's rich cultural heritage.

Civic and Social Responsibility: Rizal was not only a writer and intellectual but also a social reformer and advocate for social justice. His works and actions inspired and fueled the Philippine revolution against Spanish colonial rule. Studying Rizal's life, works, and writings can instill in Filipino youth a sense of civic and social responsibility, encouraging them to become active and engaged citizens who contribute positively to their society. Rizal's values of patriotism, love for fellow Filipinos, and commitment to social change can serve as important lessons for the youth in shaping their understanding of their roles and responsibilities as citizens.

Critical Thinking and Analytical Skills: Rizal's works, particularly his novels, are complex and multifaceted, often tackling issues such as colonialism, nationalism, social inequality, and human rights. Studying Rizal's works in a college course can enhance the critical thinking and analytical skills of Filipino youth. They can learn to critically analyze and interpret Rizal's writings in the historical and social context of his time, and apply these skills to analyze and understand contemporary social issues in the Philippines. This can empower the youth with the ability to think critically, evaluate information, and form well-reasoned opinions, which are important skills in navigating the complexities of modern society.

In conclusion, taking a course on the life, works, and writings of Rizal can play a vital role in the lives of Filipino youth by fostering cultural identity and national pride, promoting civic and social responsibility, and enhancing critical thinking and analytical skills. It can provide them with a solid foundation for understanding their history, culture, and society, and inspire them to become active and engaged citizens who contribute positively to their country's development.

Jose P. Rizal

BECAUSE IT IS MANDATED BY LAW The teaching of Jose Rizal's life, works, and writings is mandated by Republic Act 1425, otherwise known as the Rizal Law. To gain an inspiring source of patriotism through the study of Rizal's life, works, and writings.

Jose P. Rizal or better known as Pepe was a Filipino reformist for a free society and not a revolutionary seeking independence. As head of the Movement for Change in the Philippines founded by Filipinos in Barcelona, ​​Spain, he contributed to La Solidaridad.

The goals:that the Philippines become a part-province of Spain;to have representatives in the Spanish Legislature (Parliament);to have Filipino priests in charge or to have secularization;freedom of assembly and press;equal rights before the law, whether Filipino or Spanish.

The powerful officials could not accept those changes, because it meant the loss of Spanish dominance. So when Rizal returned to Manila from Spain, he was accused of sowing trouble for the establishment of La Liga Filipina, tried and exiled to the City of Dapitan/Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte/Zamboanga in 1892. There, he built a school children's, and a hospital. In addition, he also implemented a municipal drainage system.
