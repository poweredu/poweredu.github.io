1. timeline of extinction of representative microorganisms, plants and animals


Here is a timeline of extinction of representative microorganisms, plants, and animals.

Presentation of a timeline of extinction of representative microorganisms plants and animals below includes those from year 2000 to 2016. You will notice that most of the items indicated are for timeline of extinct animals and also timeline extinction of plants, which humans are mostly to be blamed for.

On December 8, 2016, the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) gave a warning that the number of wild giraffes has went down by 40% in the last three decades, making it vulnerable to extinction.

On June 14, 2016, researchers from Australia said that the Bramble Cray melomys, a rodent that only lived in Bramble Cray in the Great Barrier Reef, has become the first documented mammal to go extinct due to man-made climate change.

On May 10, 2016, British researchers gave a warning that a fifth of our plant species is at risk to becoming extinct.

On April 25, 2016, Wildlife Conservation Society based in New York said that the Royal Turtle of Cambodia is nearly extinct, with less than ten left in the wild because of sand dredging and illegal logging.

On November 24, 2015, international experts said that more than half of the world’s primates, including apes, lemurs and monkeys, are facing extinction.

On November 10, 2011, the International Union for Conservation of Nature said that the Western Black Rhino of Africa has been declared as officially extinct.

On January 7, 2009, wildlife activists said that the box turtle is disappearing in Malaysia because of widespread illegal hunting for its meat and function as traditional Chinese medicine.

On June 16, 2008, a conservation group said that the Northern White Rhino in Central Africa is at the verge of being extinct. There were four surviving specimens, but they were not been seen since 2006.

On November 12, 2007, the International Union for Conservation of Nature said that the world’s smallest bear species faces extinction because of deforestation and poaching in Southeast Asia.

On September 12, 2007, the World Conservation Union’s Red List of Threatened Species report in 2007 said that more than 16,300 species of animals and plants are on the verge of disappearing from the planet, with nearly 200 more species that are near extinction within the previous year. Gorillas and orangutans were under Critically Endangered.

On August 8, 2007, an international team of researchers said that the Yangtze River dolphin in China is probably extinct. This is also the first whale or dolphin to be extinct due to human activity.

On May 23, 2007, 35 of Europe’s 231 mammal species fell under the threatened category.

In 2006, Terengganu, a northern state of Malaysia, was able to count five leatherback turtle nests only, compared to around 6,000 to 10,000 for 50 years earlier.

On December 13, 2006, an international expedition said that a rare, nearly-blind dolphin is already extinct after its search for its Yangtze River habitat.

On July 5, 2006, the World Wildlife Fund gave a warning that bluefin tuna is risked being extinct for being fished for illegal catches.

On October 6, 2005, lions in Kenya and Tanzania were rapidly disappearing in 36,000 square miles of national parks and faced extinction.

On September 12, 2005, an international group warned that only 887 hippopotamuses are left in Congo, and that they will be extinct in Africa.

On February 26, 2000, it was reported from United Arab Emirates that the last-known Arabian mountain goat, known as tahr, was killed.

The Philippines is a young archipelago with oldest soils dating back to Jurassic and archipelago forming during the Paleogene. Its faunal records of fossil bodies mostly are within the Cenozoic era with Pleistocene fauna began appearing in many islands attributable to modern geography. Paleontological and archeological findings yielded information regarding the extinct species found in the Philippines through the excavation of various sites in the country. The first scientifically described megafaunal fossil found was of a Stegodon (Stegodon mindanensis). It was collected from a Manobo medicine man in Mindanao, which was used as a talisman. Recent Holocene extinctions, however, are on the rise, being associated with human derived activities. Deforestation is one of the primary cause and is the reason of numerous extinctions and extirpations in the island of Cebu.
