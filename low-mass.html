If I were a star, the path that I want to choose is a low mass star. Because a low-mass star at the end of its life will swell into a red giant, eventually forming a ring known as a planetary nebula.

FURTHER EXPLANATION:

Main sequence stars like the sun are formed from clouds of dust and gas that are collapsed together by gravity. How stars evolve later depends on the mass of each. The most massive stars, with eight times the mass of the Sun or more, will never become white dwarfs. Instead, at the end of their lives, they will explode in a supernova, evolving into a neutron star or black hole.

Meanwhile, smaller stars like the Sun, will go through a slightly quieter evolutionary stage. These low-mass stars at the end of their lives will swell into red giants, which will eventually shed their outer layers to form rings known as planetary nebulae. Then the star's core will become a white dwarf, a star that does not fuse hydrogen.

When a star runs out of fuel, it collapses in on itself. White dwarf stars have a mass similar to that of the Sun but have a radius about the size of the Earth. This makes the white dwarf extremely dense; the gravity on the white dwarf's surface is 350,000 times that of Earth.

23. explain low mass star and massive star​


Answer:

Low mass stars (stars with masses less than half the mass of the Sun) are the smallest, coolest and dimmest Main Sequence stars and orange, red or brown in color. Low mass stars use up their hydrogen fuel very slowly and consequently have long lives. Low mass stars simply die by burning up their fuel to leave behind white dwarfs (contracted low mass stars about the size of the Earth) which themselves cool and contract further to black dwarfs.

A massive star is a star that is larger than eight solar masses during its regular main sequence lifetime. Massive stars are born, just like average stars, out of clouds of dust called nebulae.

28. describe the life cycle of a low mass star and a medium mass star


Low Mass Star:

A low mass star is defined as any star that has a mass lower than about eight times the mass of the sun. The life cycle of a low mass star begins with a cloud of gas and dust. This material collapses due to gravity and forms a protostar, which is a very bright star in the early stages of its life. Over time, the star continues to collapse and the temperature and pressure at the core increase significantly. This causes the star to enter the main sequence, where it fuses hydrogen into helium. The star remains in this state for billions of years before the hydrogen in the core is depleted. Eventually, the core will begin to contract, and the star will expand to become a red giant. As the core contracts, the star will begin to fuse helium into heavier elements. When the star has exhausted the helium in its core, it will become a white dwarf before fading away.

Medium Mass Star:

A medium mass star is defined as any star that has a mass between about 8 and 20 times the mass of the sun. The life cycle of a medium mass star begins with a cloud of gas and dust. This material collapses due to gravity and forms a protostar, which is a very bright star in the early stages of its life. Over time, the star continues to collapse and the temperature and pressure at the core increase significantly. This causes the star to enter the main sequence, where it fuses hydrogen into helium. Unlike a low mass star, a medium mass star will eventually exhaust the helium in its core and become a red supergiant. During this phase, the star will begin to fuse heavier elements until iron is formed. When the core can no longer sustain the fusion of heavier elements, the star will collapse and form either a neutron star or a black hole. The final stage of a medium mass star’s life cycle is the explosion of a supernova.
