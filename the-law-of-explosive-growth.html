1. what gas law best explains the explosion


Combined gas Law

Combined gas law is the combination of the application of Boyle's Law, Charle's Law, Gay-Lussac's Law. This all about the relationship between pressure, volume, and temperature of gas.

Boyle's Law  -  states that the relationship of volume and pressure is inversely proportional when the other variables are constant. When volume decreases pressure will increased.

Charle's Law -  states that the relationship between volume and temperature is directly proportional when the other variables are constant. When temperature increases, the volume also increases.

Gay-Lussac's Law -  states that the relationship of volume and pressure is directly proportional when the other variables are constant. When temperature increases the pressure also increases.

When the temperature of the gas inside the tire increases, the volume of the tire will naturally increased but since it is rigid it will build too much pressure. When the tire cannot hold the pressure inside the tire due to increasing temperature, the tire will explode. This is an example of combined gas law.

3. does the law of conservation of momentum hold true in collisions and explosions?


Answer:

Yes

Explanation:

The law of conservation of momentum states that. Momentum is conserved in ALL collisions or explosion in an isolated system where no external forces act. In other words the momentum before the collision or explosion is the same as that after it even if the kinetic energy is not conserved.

Answer:

Whether it is a collision or an explosion, if it occurs in an isolated system, then each object involved encounters the same impulse to cause the same momentum change. The impulse and momentum change on each object are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. Thus, the total system momentum is conserved.
