4. Calculate the vapor pressure of a solution made by dissolving 218 g glucose in 460 ml water at 30 ℃.what is the vapor pressure lowering.


Hello!

Calculate the vapor pressure of a solution made by dissolving 218 g glucose in 460 ml water at 30 ℃.what is the vapor pressure lowering.

* We have the following data:

P (pressure of a solution) = ?

V (water volume) = 460 mL

d (water density) = 1 g/mL

m1 (mass of the solute) = 218 g

m2 (mass of the solvent) = ?

MM H2O - molar mass of water or MMsolvent = ?

MM C6H12O6 - molar mass of glucose or MMsolute= ?

We apply the data to the density formula to find the mass of the solvent, see:

[tex]d = \dfrac{m_1}{V}[/tex]

[tex]m_2 = d*V[/tex]

[tex]m_2 = 460\:mL\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\dfrac{\hspace{0.5cm}}{~}*1\:\dfrac{g}{mL\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\dfrac{\hspace{0.5cm}}{~}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{m_2 = 460\:g}[/tex]

Let's find Molar Mass of water (solvent), let's see:

MM H2O - molar mass of water

H = 2*(1u) = 2u

O = 1*(16u) = 16 u

-------------------------

MM H2O - molar mass of water = 2 + 16 = 18 g/mol

MMsolvent = 18 g/mol

Let's find molar mass of glucose (solute), let's see:

MM C6H12O6 - molar mass of glucose

C = 6*(12u) = 72 u

H = 12*(1u) = 12u

O = 6*(16u) = 96 u

---------------------------

MM C6H12O6 - molar mass of glucose = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 g/mol

MMsolute = 180 g/mol

Let's find the number of moles (n1) of glucose (solute) and number of moles (n2) of water (solvent), let's see:

*n1 (solute)

[tex]n_1 = \dfrac{m_1}{MM_{solute}}[/tex]

[tex]n_1 = \dfrac{218\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g}{180\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g/mol}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{n_1 \approx 1.211\:mol}\Longleftarrow(mols\:of\:solute)[/tex]

* n2 (solvent)

[tex]n_2 = \dfrac{m_2}{MM_{solvent}}[/tex]

[tex]n_2 = \dfrac{460\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g}{18\:\diagup\!\!\!\!\!g/mol}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{n_2 \approx 25.55\:mol}\Longleftarrow(mols\:of\:solvent)[/tex]

Now, Let's calculate the fraction in mols of water (solvent), see:

[tex]X_{solvent} = \dfrac{mols\:of\:solvent}{mols\:of\:solvent + mols\:of\:solute}[/tex]

[tex]X_{solvent} = \dfrac{25.55}{25.55 + 1.211}[/tex]

[tex]X_{solvent} = \dfrac{25.55}{26.761}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{X_{solvent} \approx 0.9548}[/tex]

We know that when looking at a table of Vapor Pressure of liquids, we can see that the Vapor Pressure of water is equal to 31.82 mmHg, so what will be the vapor pressure of the solution at 30ºC ?

Let's go to Raoult's Law, let's see:

[tex]P_{solvent\:vapor} = P_{pure\:solvent} * X_{solvent}[/tex]

[tex]P_{solvent\:vapor} = 31.82\:mmHg * 0.9548[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\boxed{P_{solvent\:vapor} \approx 30.38\:mmHg}}\end{array}}\qquad\checkmark[/tex]

Answer:

30.38 mmHg (the vapor pressure lowering)

8. explaination of vapor density and vapor pressure ​


Answer:

Vapor density refers to the density of a gas or vapor in relation to the density of air or another reference gas at the same temperature and pressure. Vapor pressure, on the other hand, is the pressure exerted by a vapor in thermodynamic equilibrium with its condensed phases (solid or liquid) at a given temperature in a closed system. It is a measure of the tendency of molecules in a substance to escape into the gas phase.

Vapor Pressure

Vapor pressure or equilibrium vapor pressure is defined as the pressure exerted by a vapor in thermodynamic equilibrium with its condensed phases at a given temperature in a closed system. The equilibrium vapor pressure is an indication of a liquid's thermodynamic tendency to evaporate

Vapor Density

Vapour density is the density of a vapour in relation to that of hydrogen. It may be defined as mass of a certain volume of a substance divided by mass of same volume of hydrogen. vapour density = mass of n molecules of gas / mass of n molecules of hydrogen.

16. The vapor pressure of water at 80 C is 355 Torr. An aqueous solution is made by dissolving 50 grams of ethylene glycol (mm= 60 g/mol) in 50 grams of water (mm= 18 g/mol. What is the vapor pressure lowering of water in this solution? (NOTE: To calculate the vapor pressure lowering, we need to subtract the vapor pressure of solution from the vapor pressure of solvent. )


Answer:

∆P = 82 to rr

Explanation:

Calculating the number of moles of solute and solvent

n solute = 50 g × (1 mol / 60 g) = 0.83333 mol

n solvent = 50 g × (1 mol / 18 g) = 2.77778 mol

Calculating the total number of moles

n T = n solute + n solvent

n T = 0.83333 mol + 2.77778 mol

n T = 3.61111 mol

Calculating the mole fraction of the solvent

X solvent = n solvent / n T

X solvent = 2.77778 mol / 3.61111 mol

X solvent = 0.769232

Calculating the vapor pressure of the solution

P solution = (X solvent)(P solvent)

P solution = (0.769232)(355 to rr)

P solution = 273 to rr

Calculating the vapor pressure lowering

∆P = P solvent - P solution

∆P = 355 to rr - 273 to rr

∆P = 82 to rr

21. why the vapor pressure of a solution is lower? explain?​


Answer:

Why vapour pressure of solution is lower than solvent?

There are fewer solvent molecules on the surface of the solution than on the surface of the pure liquid. Fewer will vaporize and, as a consequence, the vapor pressure of the solution will be less than that of the pure liquid at the same temperature

What causes vapor pressure lowering?

Because some of the surface is now occupied by solute particles, there is less room for solvent molecules. This results in less solvent being able to evaporate. The addition of a nonvolatile solute results in a lowering of the vapor pressure of the solvent.

What is lowering of Vapour pressure of a solution?

Vapor pressure lowering is a colligative property of solutions. The vapor pressure of a pure solvent is greater than the vapor pressure of a solution containing a non volatile liquid. This lowered vapor pressure leads to boiling point elevation. When something is volatile is actually able to evaporate very quickly.


22. Calculate the vapor pressure lowering of water when 5.67 g of glucose, C6 H12 O6 , is dissolved in 5.2 grams of water at 250 C. The vapor pressure of water at 250 C is 23.8 mmHg. What is the vapor pressure of the solution ?​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

• For vapor pressure of the solution

Step 1: Calculate the number of moles of solute (glucose) and solvent (water).

Note that the molar masses of glucose and water are 180.16 g/mol and 18.02 g/mol, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} & n_{\text{solvent}} = 5.2 \: \cancel{\text{g}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol}}{18.02 \: \cancel{\text{g}}} = \text{0.28857 mol} \\ & n_{\text{solute}} = 5.67 \: \cancel{\text{g}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol}}{180.16 \: \cancel{\text{g}}} = \text{0.031472 mol} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mole fraction of solvent.

[tex]\begin{aligned} X_{\text{solvent}} & = \frac{n_{\text{solvent}}}{n_{\text{solvent}} + n_{\text{solute}}} \\ & = \frac{\text{0.28857 mol}}{\text{0.28857 mol + 0.031472 mol}} \\ & = 0.90166 \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the vapor pressure of the solution.

[tex]\begin{aligned} P_{\text{solution}} & = P_{\text{solution}}X_{\text{solvent}} \\ & = \text{(23.8 mmHg)(0.90166)} \\ & = \boxed{\text{21.5 mmHg}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For vapor pressure lowering

[tex]\begin{aligned} \Delta P & = P_{\text{solvent}} - P_{\text{solution}} \\ & = \text{23.8 mmHg} - \text{21.5 mmHg} \\ & = \boxed{\text{2.3 mmHg}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, the vapor pressure of the solution and the vapor pressure lowering are 21.5 mmHg and 2.3 mmHg, respectively.
