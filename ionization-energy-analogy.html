19. What is Ionization energy?​


Ionization energy is the least or smallest amount of energy needed to take away an atom's outermost valence electron. In the periodic table, as you progress towards the right side of it, the ionization energy increases since the nuclear charge induced by the protons (which pulls the electrons towards the nucleus) being added also increases. However, the ionization energy decreases as you progress downwards through the periods since more energy levels are being added and more energy levels means more electrons between the nucleus and valence electrons. All electrons possess a negative charge so essentially, the inner electrons are repelling the valence electrons being pulled inwards the nucleus. This phenomenon is known as electron shielding. So, basically, the element that's in the bottom left of the periodic table has the weakest ionization energy - other elements that are more electronegative can easily take away its valence electron. Meanwhile, the element that's in the top right corner of the periodic table has the highest ionization energy - no one can just simply take away its outer electrons.

27. Combining the electronegativity and ionization energy.​


Combining the electronegativity and ionization energy.Electronegativity is a measure of an atom's ability to attract electrons, while ionization energy is the amount of energy required to remove an electron from an atom or a positive ion. The two properties are related, but they are not the same.

Electronegativity tends to increase as you move from left to right across the periodic table, and it also tends to increase as you move down a group. This is because the atoms become larger and more polarizable as you move down a group, and the outermost electrons are farther from the positively charged nucleus. As a result, the atoms are better able to attract and hold onto electrons.

Ionization energy, on the other hand, tends to increase as you move from left to right across the periodic table and from top to bottom within a group. This is because the atoms become smaller and more tightly bound as you move across the periodic table, making it more difficult to remove an electron.

In general, there is a relationship between electronegativity and ionization energy, but it is not a simple one. Electronegativity and ionization energy can both be used to predict and explain the behavior of atoms and their interactions with other atoms, but they should not be considered interchangeable.


28. increasing ionization energy​


Answer:

The ionization energy of an atom is the amount of energy that is required to remove an electron from a mole of atoms in the gas phase:

M(g) ® M+(g) + e-

It is possible to remove more electrons from most elements, so this quantity is more precisely known as the first ionization energy, the energy to go from neutral atoms to cations with a 1+ charge. The second ionization energy is the energy that is required to remove a second electron, to form 2+ cations from 1+ cations:

M+(g) ® M2+(g) + e-

The third ionization energy is the energy required to form 3+ cations:

M2+(g) ® M3+(g) + e-

and so on. Ionization energies are always positive numbers, because energy must be supplied (an endothermic energy change) to separate electrons from atoms. The second ionization energy is always larger than the first ionization energy, because it requires even more energy to remove an electron from a cation than it is from a neutral atom.

The first ionization energy varies in a predictable way across the periodic table. The ionization energy decreases from top to bottom in groups, and increases from left to right across a period. Thus, helium has the largest first ionization energy, while francium has one of the lowest.

From top to bottom in a group, orbitals corresponding to higher values of the principal quantum number (n) are being added, which are on average further away from the nucleus. Since the outermost electrons are further away, they are less strongly attracted by the nucleus, and are easier to remove, corresponding to a lower value for the first ionization energy.

From left to right across a period, more protons are being added to the nucleus, but the number of electrons in the inner, lower-energy shells remains the same. The valence electrons feel a higher effective nuclear charge — the sum of the charges on the protons in the nucleus and the charges on the inner, core electrons. The valence electrons are therefore held more tightly, the atom decreases in size (see atomic radius), and it becomes increasingly difficult to remove them, corresponding to a higher value for the first ionization energy.
