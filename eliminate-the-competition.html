17. * Explain the difference of the followinga. Perfect Competitionb. Imperfect Competitionc. Monopolistic Competition​


Answer:Perfect vs. Imperfect Competition: An Overview

Perfect competition is a concept in microeconomics that describes a market structure controlled entirely by market forces. If and when these forces are not met, the market is said to have imperfect competition.

While no market has clearly defined perfect competition, all real-world markets are classified as imperfect. That being said, a perfect market is used as a standard by which the effectiveness and efficiency of real-world markets can be measured.

Perfect Competition

Perfect competition is an abstract concept that occurs in economics textbooks, but not in the real world. That's because it's impossible to attain in real life.

Theoretically, resources would be divided among companies equally and fairly in a market with perfect competition, and no monopoly would exist. Each company would have the same industry knowledge and they would all sell the same products. There would be plenty of buyers and sellers in this market, and demand would help set prices evenly across the board.

Answer:

20 children entered the competition.

Step-by-step explanation:

Word Problem

Let us analyze the given problem above.

To find the number of children originally entered the competition, we need to identify the number of eliminated students in every given situations.

"After three minutes, a fifth of the students had made mistakes and was excluded from the competition."

⅕ of the children were eliminated. Meaning, the remaining children is ⅘.

‌5⁄5‌ - ⅕ = ⅘

"In the next five minutes half of those remaining were eliminated."

Half of ⅘ were eliminated. Therefore, ⅖ of the children remained.

½ × ⅘ = ‌4⁄10‌ or ⅖

"Two minutes later four students were found cheating and were sent home."

4 of the remaining children were eliminated.

"Half of the remaining students had made mistakes and left the stage."

Half were eliminated. So, half remained.

"In the last few minutes one more participant made an unfortunate mistake and one contestant was left as the winner."

There were two children remained in the last part of the competition. Meaning, half is equal to 2.

If ⅖ of the children is equal to 8 (4 + 2 + 2), therefore ⅕ is equal to 4.

Since there are five parts, we need to multiply 4 by 5.

4 × 5 = 20

20 children entered the competition.
