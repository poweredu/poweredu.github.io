2. Characterization of casein and soy protein 1. Biuret Test: casein and soyprotein + Benedict’s reagent Expected result: 2. Hopkins-Cole Test:casein and soy protein + hopkin’s reagent Expected Result:3. Millons Test: casein and soyprotein + Millon’s reagentExpected result: 4. Xanthoproteic Test:casein and soyprotein + Xanthoproteic reagent Expected Result:​


Answer:

The tests you mentioned are used to detect the presence of proteins in a sample and are not specific to casein or soy protein. However, here are the expected results for each test with casein and soy protein:

Biuret Test:

This test is used to detect the presence of peptide bonds in proteins. When casein or soy protein is mixed with Benedict's reagent and heated, the solution should turn from blue to green, indicating the presence of protein.

Expected result: Green coloration.

Hopkins-Cole Test:

This test is used to detect the presence of tryptophan in proteins. When casein or soy protein is mixed with Hopkins reagent, a deep violet coloration should develop, indicating the presence of tryptophan.

Expected Result: Deep violet coloration.

Millons Test:

This test is used to detect the presence of tyrosine in proteins. When casein or soy protein is mixed with Millon's reagent and heated, a brick-red coloration should develop, indicating the presence of tyrosine.

Expected result: Brick-red coloration.

Xanthoproteic Test:

This test is used to detect the presence of aromatic amino acids (tryptophan, phenylalanine, and tyrosine) in proteins. When casein or soy protein is mixed with nitric acid, a yellow coloration should develop, indicating the presence of aromatic amino acids.

Expected result: Yellow coloration.


3. Which functional group is responsible for the visible positive result in the following tests? a. Biuret test b. Ninhydrin test c. Xantoproteic test d. Millon's test e. Hopkins-Cole test f. Sakaguchi test g. Nitroprusside test h. Fohl's test i. Test for Amide j. Pauly's test


Millon's Test is the functional group that is responsible for the visible positive result.

The development of a red or pink colored precipitate during the Millon's test serves as evidence of a favorable outcome. This suggests that tyrosine or a protein containing tyrosine is present.

Using Millon's test, an analytical technique, one may identify tyrosine, the only amino acid with the phenol group. Millon's test selectively identifies tyrosine, but proteins are not recognized since it also picks up the phenolic group, which can be found in other substances. As a result, additional tests such the Biuret and Ninhydrin tests must be performed in addition to the Millon's test. The test is useful in identifying proteins because tyrosine is a common component of them. The test was developed by French scientist Auguste Nicolas Eugene Millon, who received credit for its creation.

The premise of Millon's test is that complexes with heavy metals like mercury are formed as a result of nitrification of the phenol group in tyrosine. The test ingredient, known as Millon's reagent, is composed of mercuric and mercurous nitrates that have been thoroughly mixed with strong nitric acid. The phenol group on the tyrosine molecule is nitrated by the reagent's nitric acid during the test. The nitrated tyrosine is then mixed with the mercury ions in the solution to produce a red precipitate or solution. Some proteins that include tyrosine react initially with mercuric nitrate to form a white or yellow precipitate.
