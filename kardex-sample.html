18. Simple Ramdom Sampling ,Systematic Sampling,Stratified sampling,Cluster sampling, Multi-stage sampling examples


Answer:

Here are some examples of different sampling techniques:

Simple Random Sampling: A researcher wants to conduct a study on the academic performance of students in a school. They randomly select 50 students from the school's student list using a random number generator, ensuring that each student has an equal chance of being selected.

Systematic Sampling: A market researcher is conducting a survey on consumer preferences for a new product. They obtain a list of all registered users of a particular online shopping platform and decide to survey every 10th user on the list, starting from a randomly selected user.

Stratified Sampling: A health researcher is studying the prevalence of diabetes in a city with a diverse population of different age groups (e.g., children, adults, and elderly). They divide the population into three strata based on age (0-18 years, 19-65 years, and 65+ years) and randomly sample 50 individuals from each age group to ensure representation from each stratum.

Cluster Sampling: A sociologist is studying the living conditions of low-income households in a metropolitan area. They randomly select 10 neighborhoods (clusters) from a list of all low-income neighborhoods in the area and survey all households within each selected neighborhood.

Multi-stage Sampling: An ecologist is conducting a study on biodiversity in a large forest. They randomly select 5 quadrats (smaller areas) within each of 10 randomly selected blocks (larger areas) of the forest. Within each quadrat, they collect data on plant species diversity, population density, and other ecological variables

PVM = mRT

Answer:

To construct the sampling distribution of sample means, we need to find all possible samples of size 2 that can be taken from the population {2, 3, 4, 6, 10, 12}. There are 15 possible samples of size 2, and for each sample, we can compute the sample mean. The possible samples and their corresponding sample means are:

Sample 1: {2, 3}, Sample Mean = (2+3)/2 = 2.5

Sample 2: {2, 4}, Sample Mean = (2+4)/2 = 3

Sample 3: {2, 6}, Sample Mean = (2+6)/2 = 4

Sample 4: {2, 10}, Sample Mean = (2+10)/2 = 6

Sample 5: {2, 12}, Sample Mean = (2+12)/2 = 7

Sample 6: {3, 4}, Sample Mean = (3+4)/2 = 3.5

Sample 7: {3, 6}, Sample Mean = (3+6)/2 = 4.5

Sample 8: {3, 10}, Sample Mean = (3+10)/2 = 6.5

Sample 9: {3, 12}, Sample Mean = (3+12)/2 = 7.5

Sample 10: {4, 6}, Sample Mean = (4+6)/2 = 5

Sample 11: {4, 10}, Sample Mean = (4+10)/2 = 7

Sample 12: {4, 12}, Sample Mean = (4+12)/2 = 8

Sample 13: {6, 10}, Sample Mean = (6+10)/2 = 8

Sample 14: {6, 12}, Sample Mean = (6+12)/2 = 9

Sample 15: {10, 12}, Sample Mean = (10+12)/2 = 11

The sampling distribution of sample means is the distribution of these sample means. The mean of the sampling distribution is equal to the population mean, which is the average of all values in the population. In this case, the population mean is:

Population Mean = (2+3+4+6+10+12)/6 = 7

So, the mean of the sampling distribution is also 7.
