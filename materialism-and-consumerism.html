Answer:

Consumerism is the idea that increasing consumption of goods and services purchased in the market is always a desirable goal and that a person's wellbeing and happiness depends fundamentally on obtaining consumer goods and material possessions. In an economic sense, it is related to the predominantly Keynesian idea that consumer spending is the key driver of the economy and that encouraging consumers to spend is a major policy goal. From this point of view, consumerism is a positive phenomenon that fuels economic growth.

11. text on art and consumerism. Explain this?​


Art and consumerism are two intersecting yet complex concepts. Consumerism refers to the belief and practice of excessive consumption of goods and services as a means of achieving personal happiness and fulfillment. On the other hand, art is often seen as a form of expression and communication that transcends the material realm and has the potential to provoke deeper thoughts and emotions in the viewer.

In recent decades, the line between art and consumerism has become increasingly blurred, as the commercialization of art has become more widespread and widespread. Art has become a commodity that can be bought and sold, with prices often reflecting the market demand rather than the intrinsic value of the work itself. Artworks are frequently produced and sold as luxury goods, with the emphasis on their exclusivity and rarity rather than their aesthetic or cultural significance.

Critics argue that this trend has devalued the purpose and meaning of art, transforming it into a superficial and shallow expression of consumer culture. Consumerism reduces art to a means of fulfilling material desires and status symbols, rather than a means of exploring deeper truths and realities. This commercialization of art also contributes to the marginalization of certain forms of expression and the homogenization of artistic styles, as the market demands conformity to established tastes and trends.

However, others argue that art and consumerism are not mutually exclusive, and that the intersection between the two can offer opportunities for artistic expression and cultural critique. By examining and challenging the values and systems of consumer culture, artists can raise important questions about the impact of consumerism on society and the environment.

In conclusion, while consumerism and art may often appear to be in conflict, they are deeply intertwined and offer a complex and dynamic relationship. It is up to each individual to decide what role they want art to play in their lives, whether as a means of consumerist fulfillment or as a means of exploring deeper truths and realities.
