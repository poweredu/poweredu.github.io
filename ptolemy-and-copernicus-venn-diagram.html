The only difference between them, really, is that Copernicus shifted to heliocentrism and swapped the Earth (and Moon) with the Sun.

Both systems have the celestial bodies riding circular orbits inside a Sphere of Fixed Stars. Nor did Copernicus propose any changes to the Aristotelean physics of the old system. 25. Compare and contrast the theory of Ptolemy and Copernicus.


Ptolemy's theory, known as the geocentric theory, held that the Earth was at the center of the universe and that all other celestial bodies, including the sun and the planets, revolved around it. He believed that the planets moved in circular orbits, but to account for their retrograde motion (when they appear to move backward in the sky), he introduced the concept of epicycles, or small circles within the larger orbits. Ptolemy's theory was widely accepted for centuries and was the basis of astronomical knowledge in the Middle Ages.

Copernicus, on the other hand, proposed a heliocentric theory, which placed the sun at the center of the universe and had the planets, including Earth, revolving around it. He believed that the planets moved in circular orbits, but unlike Ptolemy, he did not need to introduce epicycles to account for their motion. Copernicus' theory challenged the prevailing belief in Ptolemy's geocentric theory, but it was not immediately accepted.

Ptolemy's theory dominated for centuries and was the basis for much of the astronomical knowledge in the Middle Ages. Copernicus' theory, paved the way for the development of modern astronomy and the scientific revolution. His theory was later refined by Kepler, who proposed that the planets move in elliptical orbits, and by Newton, who developed the laws of gravity that explained the motion of the planets.

SUMMARY:

Ptolemy's geocentric theory placed the Earth at the center of the universe, while Copernicus' heliocentric theory placed the sun at the center. Ptolemy used epicycles to explain the motion of the planets, while Copernicus did not need to introduce such a concept. Copernicus' theory challenged the prevailing belief in Ptolemy's theory and paved the way for modern astronomy.
