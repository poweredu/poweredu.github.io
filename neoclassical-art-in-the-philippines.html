4. what did you feed upon knowing that many Philippine artworks were influenced by concept and principles of western art,especially that of Neoclassical and Romantic art?​


Answer:

The influence of Western art on Philippine artworks can be seen in various forms, including the subjects depicted, styles, and techniques used by Filipino artists. During the Spanish colonial period, many Filipino artists were trained in Western styles of art, particularly the Neoclassical and Romantic movements, which were popular at the time.

The Neoclassical style, characterized by its emphasis on simplicity, clarity, and order, was often used by Filipino artists in their depictions of historical and religious themes. On the other hand, the Romantic style, which emphasized emotion, imagination, and individualism, influenced the creation of more expressive and passionate works of art.

Despite the influence of Western art, Philippine artworks also reflect the country's unique cultural identity and history, incorporating elements of indigenous art and local traditions.

Answer:

1. JUAN NOVICIO LUNA 1/4

The artist was best known for his dramatic and dynamic paintings, which he produced in the romanticism and realism art styles. On October 1, 1857, he was born in the northern Philippines. Not only as a painter, but also as a sculptor and a political activist, he became one of the first known Filipino artists. The National Museum in Manila houses one of his most famous works, "Spoliarium," for which he won first prize at the Madrid Exposition.

2. JUAN NOVICIO LUNA 4/4

His works have been characterized as "vigorous and dramatic." His style, which has elements of Romanticism, is influenced by Daumier and Rembrandt. He went to Japan in 1894 and returned after the Philippine Revolution in 1896. In December of 1899, he died in Hong Kong. His remains were buried in Hong Kong until 1920, when they were exhumed and sent to the San Agustin Church Crypt in the Philippines.

3. JUAN NOVICIO LUNA 2/4

So many of his works can now be found in prestigious Philippine museums such as the Lopez Museum and the National Museum. To become a sailor, he studied at the Escuela Nautica de Manila. 4. The Hay Wain (1821) - John Constable

This piece is important to this style of art because it depicts water, a horse and cart, and a building along the water's edge. Despite the fact that it appears to be a simple painting, the form, shape, and colors of the clouds and sky, set against the almost glowing yellowing of the trees, reveal the true romanticism of the period.

5. Memory of Mortefontaine (1864) - Jean-Baptiste Carot

Perhaps one Carot's key goals in this painting was to make the picture appear as though it were a distorted snapshot. He did a good job of it, and although it's still a lovely scene, the picture has been romanticized by the speckles of light that reflect around the image, giving it a fairytale-like quality. The water's reflection of light induces a pleasing scene. Although the painting is mostly realistic, other paint effects have been integrated into the picture, making this a romantic work. It's easy to see why this painting is one of the most famous works of romantic art in the world.

6. Gothic Cathedral by the Water (1813) - Karl Friedrich Schinkel

People are always taken aback by this painting. Schinkel has managed to perfectly portray how the sun can make even the dark side of a building look glamorous and fascinating by showing a backlit cathedral along the edge of the sea, with staff on the opposite side of the water going about their everyday business. With the ease and sense of movement with which the clouds are created and shaped in the sky, this piece truly stands out among some of the most popular romanticism pieces of all time.
