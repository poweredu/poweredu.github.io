Characteristics:

1. Historical research - Focuses on studying past events and their impact on the present. It involves the collection and analysis of primary and secondary sources.

2. Action research - Focuses on finding solutions to specific problems in a particular setting. It is typically conducted by practitioners or educators in their own classrooms or organizations.

3. Applied Research - Focuses on the practical application of knowledge to solve real-world problems. It involves using scientific methods to develop new technologies, products, or processes.

4. Experimental research - Focuses on testing cause-and-effect relationships between variables. It involves the manipulation of independent variables and the measurement of dependent variables.

5. Descriptive research - Focuses on describing phenomena or variables as they exist. It involves the collection of data through surveys, observations, or archival sources.

6. Correlational research - Focuses on examining the relationships between variables. It involves the measurement of variables and the calculation of correlation coefficients.

7. Survey research - Focuses on collecting data from a sample of individuals through questionnaires or interviews. It is used to gather information about attitudes, opinions, behaviors, or demographics.

8. Intervention research - Focuses on testing the effectiveness of interventions or programs. It involves the use of experimental or quasi-experimental designs to compare outcomes for groups receiving the intervention versus a control group.

The selection of respondent depends upon their characteristics and the will of the researchers.​


Answer:

The terms purposive sampling and convenience sampling are often used interchangeably, but they do not mean the same thing.

Convenience sampling is when researchers leverage individuals that can be identified and approached with as little effort as possible. These are often individuals that are geographically close to the researchers or those who have previously completed an online survey.

Purposive sampling is when researchers thoroughly think through how they will establish a sample population, even if it is not statistically representative of the greater population at hand. As the name suggests, researchers went to this community on purpose because they think that these individuals fit the profile of the people that they need to reach.

While the findings from purposive sampling do not always have to be statistically representative of the greater population of interest, they are qualitatively generalizable.

The more prior information that researchers have about their particular communities of interest, the better the sample that they’re going to select.
