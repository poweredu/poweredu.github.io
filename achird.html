14. bologna accord and washington accord


29 European countries signed a document called the Bologna Accord in June 1999, agreeing to reform higher education in order to meet the following objectives:

Establish a system of equivalent and comprehensible degrees across the European UnionEstablish a clear and consistent division between undergraduate and graduate studiesPromote mobility of students through various fields of study, institutions and nationsEstablish a quality assurance system and central authority to achieve uniform credentials and performance across the countries involvedpriority for higher education

There was little consistency in European higher education before the Accord. Universities in different countries were given various degrees, and it was not always clear what degrees were equal to each other. These differences made it hard for graduate program admissions offices and potential employers throughout regions to determine the level of education of an applicant without studying degree qualifications — a burdensome activity, given the wide range of diplomas even within a single European country.

The Bologna Accord clarifies the definition of degrees by creating a distinction between undergraduate and graduate studies and implementing the structure for "bachelor" and "master" training in European countries that sign the agreement. It also defines basic course requirements, a standard degree scale, a standardized document, and quality assurance measures to ensure common practices and standards of performance.
