5. paano nagsimula ang pintados festival ng tacloban​


Naging bansag ang Pintádos sa mga táong nakita ng mga Español sa Kabisayaan sa kanilang pagdating noong 1668. Sa wikang Español, ang pintados ay nangangahulugang mga may pinta dahil sa mga guhit sa katawan ng mga tao. Ang pagguhit ng makukulay at permanenteng disenyo sa katawan ay isang sinaunang sining sa Filipinas. Paglalagay ng tatô (mula sa Ingles na tattoo) ang karaniwang tawag ngayon. Sa Cordillera, tinatawag itong “fatek.” Gamit ang matatalas na bakal na pinainitan sa apoy, nagguguhit ng disenyo ang mga Pintados sa kanilang katawan para ipakita ang kanilang katayuan sa lipunan. Para sa mga lalaki, ang pinakamatapang na mandirigma ang may pinakamaraming disenyo mula ulo hanggang paa. Ang mga babae naman ay naglalagay din ng pintura sa kanilang mga kamay. Kaakibat sa prosesong ito ang iba pang ritwal ng pasasalamat para sa masaganang ani at tagumpay sa mga laban.

Panagbenga Festival (transl. Flower Festival) is a month-long annual flower occasion occurring in Baguio. The term is of Kankanaey origin, meaning "season of blooming".[1] The festival, held in February, was created as a tribute to the city's flowers and as a way to rise up from the devastation of the 1990 Luzon earthquake.[2] The festival includes floats that are covered mostly with flowers not unlike those used in Pasadena's Rose Parade. The festival also includes street dancing, presented by dancers clad in flower-inspired costumes, that is inspired by the Bendian, an Ibaloi dance of celebration that came from the Cordilleras.

Street parade at the Panagbenga Festival in 2009

A typical float at the Panagbenga Festival in 2009

The Bases Conversion Development Authority (BCDA), in collaboration with the John Hay Poro Point Development Corporation's (JPDC)[3] annual Camp John Hay Art Contest, gave its official logo from one of the entries: a spray of indigenous sunflowers from an artwork submitted by Trisha Tabangin, a student of the Baguio City National High School. The festival was set in February to boost tourism as it was considered as a time of inactivity between the busy days of Christmas season and the Holy Week and the summer season.[4]

On 1996, archivist and curator Ike Picpican suggested that the festival be renamed as Panagbenga, a Kankanaey term that means "a season of blossoming, a time for flowering".

On February 2020, the festival was initially postponed due to the threat of COVID-19, it was later then cancelled on March 2020.

How did pintados become the festival dance of Tacloban?

Tacloban City's Pintados festival is a Filipino celebration with its own distinct taste. This Pintados celebration commemorates the native Leytenos' pre-Spanish past via conflicts, epics, and traditional religions. The celebratory dancers, painted from head to toe with armor-like motifs to represent the tattooed warriors of old, are the most anticipated feature of the Pintados celebration. Dancers with bodies painted in a dazzling array of colors throng the streets of Tacloban City during the Pintados festival. At first glance, the sight of mature men dressed in vivid hues like luminous blue or neon green pouring onto the streets may appear absurd. However, when one becomes accustomed to this and observes the dances presented, one gains an understanding of the history of the people who originally resided on the islands of Leyte. The dancers' traditional dances depict the diverse customs that existed before the arrival of the Spaniards. These include idol worship, traditional music, and epic storytelling. As the Pintados celebration progresses, the mesmerizing rhythms of local instruments reverberate across the air, complementing the street dances. Aside from the cultural dances, the procession that crisscrosses Tacloban's streets is a sure bet. The procession usually starts at the Balayuan Towers and travels through the city of tacloban leyte.
