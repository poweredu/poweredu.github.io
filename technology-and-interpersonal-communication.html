Explanation:

Public communication is the exchange of messages with a number of people within an organization or outside the organization, face to face or through the media. However, in this section what will be discussed is only face-to-face between the organization and its external environment.

Public is also defined as impersonal, which includes many people, relating to or concerning a country, nation, or community that is not affiliated with the government of that nation. Communication is "a process by which a person or persons, groups, organizations, and societies create, and use information to connect with the environment and other people". Communication can take the form of verbal and nonverbal.

Forms of public communication include: public speaking, theater concerts and public debates. Forms of mass communication are: forms of communication involving mass communication media, newspaper & magazine articles, television and radio programmes, films and advertisements.

The scope of communication is very broad, public communication is also a combination of relations with the mass media (media relations), community outreach, crisis communication, customer relations, event planning and risk communication. Communication is something that cannot be avoided by every human being. As social beings, humans are predestined to live in society and interact with other humans by communicating. Studying communication means improving communication skills (writing, speaking, and so on). In addition, this also means learning to analyze communication events as social events.

Answer:

1. exchange of information between two or more people.

3.verbal, listening, written, and non-verbal communication.
4.
1.The Sender

Communication begins with a source, a person, group, or organization with meaning to share. Then, encoding occurs on the sender side. It’s an invisible process during which a message transitions from thought to language.

2. The Receiver

We all play this communication role — some better than others. If we do it well, we are skilled in the fine art of listening. To make that happen, there’s an invisible process going on known as decoding — the conversion of language into thought and meaning.

3. Encoding and Decoding

Here’s where the magic occurs. To transmit meaning, the sender must place meaning into a series of symbols (schemata) that represent concepts known to the receiver. While in the decoding process, symbols convert into concepts and ideas meaning something to the receiver.

4. The Medium

A sender chooses some type of communication medium. The three classic communication transmission channels are: written, verbal and electronic.

5. Receiver

A receiver is the individual, group, or organization decoding the message.

6. Interference (or Noise)

Sometimes referred to as static or itnterference, it affects both listening or hearing. A shortlist includes encoding problems on both the sender side, bad timing, failure to get feedback, etc. But there is a longer list of 17 communication barriers.

7. Feedback

Great communicators make use of the nonverbal channels the average person is not aware of. Feedback should flow back to the sender continuously; but this is rarely the case.

19. How can you best describe the irony of interpersonal communication in the technology age?A. The advances in technology actually increase the chances of miscommunication.B. The technology age actually provides fewer ways for people to communicate.C. The advances in technology have reduced the chances for miscommunication.D.The technology age has led to lesser competitive advantage in having strong communication skills.​


Answer:

A. The advances in technology actually increase the chances of miscommunication.

Explanation:

With the rise of social media and instant messaging apps, people are more likely to rely on text-based communication, which lacks the nuances of face-to-face interaction, such as tone of voice, facial expressions, and body language. This can lead to misunderstandings, misinterpretations, and even conflicts. Additionally, technology can also create a sense of distance and anonymity, which may lead to people communicating in ways they wouldn't in person, such as being more aggressive or confrontational.

Answer:

What ways can the different forms of communication (interpersonal, intrapersonal, small group, and mass) be enhanced with the aid of technology?

There surely are any ways technology ca *enhance* intrapersonal, interpersonal and other communication.

You’re off to a very good start if you are aiming to use technology to *enhance* real world old fashioned communication, not foolishly to *replace* it, like some high IQ but low wisdom and probably low experience persons who are enchanted (that means hypnotized) by technology think it would be “cool” to do.

How can a lover or harpsihord or operatic music not be overjoyed that we have recordings of Wanda Landowska playing? Or Bidu Sayao and Rosa Poncelle singing? But would they want to *replace* them with souped up moog synthesizers and Siri? Art historians can see things with high resolution photography (and why not electron microscopes?) in great paintings that were previously invisible to the naked eye. But would they want to to the paintings themselves in the documents shredder? Technology can save my beloved cat or dog or wife or daughter or even myself from diseases and injuries that would otherwise be show stoppers, but do we really want to replace them all with robots?

It used to be said that children should be seen and not heard. That was cruel because children have feelings. Computers do not have feelings and they should indeed be seen and not heard. I do not want to imagine the days of crowds marching in the streets (and spreading Covid-19 to real people who might not want it), demanding: Robots’ lives matter!
