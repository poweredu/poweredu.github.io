5. If 192 grams Ca mixed with 56 grams N2, which is the limiting reactant? Use this balance equation for the computation: 3Ca(s) + N2(g) → Ca3N2(s)​


Given:

mass of Ca = 192 g

mass of N₂ = 56 g

balanced chemical equation: 3Ca(s) + N₂(g) → Ca₃N₂(s)

Required:

limiting reactant

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of reactants.

For Ca

molar mass of Ca = 40.08 g/mol × 1

molar mass of Ca = 40.08 g/mol

For N₂

molar mass of N₂ = 14.01 g/mol × 2

molar mass of N₂ = 28.02 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of Ca₃N₂ produced by each reactant using dimensional analysis.

Using Ca

Since [tex]\text{3 mol Ca} \bumpeq \text{1 mol Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2}[/tex], the number of moles of Ca₃N₂ formed is

[tex]\text{moles of Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2} = \text{192 g Ca} × \frac{\text{1 mol Ca}}{\text{40.08 g Ca}} × \frac{\text{1 mol Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2}}{\text{3 mol Ca}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2} = \text{1.60 mol}[/tex]

Using N₂

Since [tex]\text{1 mol N}_{2} \bumpeq \text{1 mol Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2}[/tex], the number of moles of Ca₃N₂ formed is

[tex]\text{moles of Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2} = \text{56 g N}_{2} × \frac{\text{1 mol N}_{2}}{\text{28.02 g N}_{2}} × \frac{\text{1 mol Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2}}{\text{1 mol N}_{2}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of Ca}_{3}\text{N}_{2} = \text{2.00 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine the limiting reactant.

Since Ca produces less number of moles of Ca₃N₂

[tex]\boxed{\text{Ca is the limiting reactant.}}[/tex]
