17. Provide 3 examples of software projects that would be amenable to the waterfall model​


Answer:

Personally I wouldn’t really use waterfall anywhere, but I’m pretty comfortable with various Agile approaches and I can fake waterfall with Agile if I need to (for stakeholder purposes). There’s nothing in waterfall I couldn’t do as well or better in Agile (for example, I _can_ add up front planning, if it really makes sense).

But generally, if you know what needs to be done, and how to do it, waterfall works pretty well. Change is the bane of waterfall, so if I can trust there’s very little of it, I could use the approach. Even then, I’d use an incremental approach, since it’s the smartest way to do waterfall.

Anyway, waterfall is often associated with unchanging requirements. Well, I can run Agile with unchanging requirements, too. I just don’t allow change (without explicit agreement, a la formal change management). I can still use vertical slicing and iterations to deliver the system, and I get all benefits of Agile for transparency to progress, its effective risk management, working frequent software versions, faster delivery speed, and more effective retrospect practices, plus a much more motivating working environment for the team members. So really, there is just no value doing traditional development.

28. 1. What are the reasons why waterfall model is used in software development? 2. What do you think is the most important phase in the waterfall model? Why? 3. What makes the waterfall model a linear sequential model?Mark ko brainliest ​


1. The waterfall model is used in software development for several reasons, including:

-It provides a clear and well-defined process for software development, which makes it easy to understand and manage.

-It emphasizes the importance of planning and documentation, which can help reduce the risk of errors and misunderstandings later in the development process.

-It is well-suited for projects with well-defined requirements and a stable scope, where changes are less likely to occur.

-It provides a structured approach to software development that can be easier to follow and manage for inexperienced or junior developers.


2.The most important phase in the waterfall model may vary depending on the specific project and its requirements, but typically the requirements gathering and analysis phase is considered critical. This is because it sets the foundation for the entire project by identifying the customer's needs and expectations, which will inform all subsequent phases of development. If requirements are not gathered and analyzed properly, the project risks going in the wrong direction or failing to meet the customer's needs.

3. The waterfall model is a linear sequential model because it follows a strict, sequential order of phases. Each phase must be completed before moving on to the next, and there is no going back to previous phases once they are completed. This contrasts with iterative or agile development models, which allow for more flexibility and revisiting previous phases as needed. The waterfall model assumes that each phase can be completed independently of the others and that the output of one phase becomes the input for the next phase. This linear approach makes it easier to plan and manage the development process but can be less suitable for complex or evolving projects.
