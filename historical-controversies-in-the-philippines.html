12. Trace and narrate the historical background of the HOLOCAUST of the Jews as one of the most controversial issue of JUDAISM.​


The Holocaust, also known as the Shoah, was a genocide of the Jewish people during World War II. It is estimated that six million Jews were killed by the Nazi regime, along with five million other marginalized groups, including Romani people, disabled individuals, homosexuals, and political dissidents.

The origins of the Holocaust can be traced back to the rise of the Nazi Party in Germany in the 1930s. Adolf Hitler became the Chancellor of Germany in 1933, and immediately began implementing policies that targeted Jewish individuals and other groups. The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 stripped Jews of their citizenship and made it illegal for them to marry non-Jewish individuals.

In 1938, a Nazi-led program called Kristallnacht saw the destruction of Jewish homes, businesses, and synagogues across Germany, Austria, and parts of Czechoslovakia. This marked a significant escalation of anti-Semitic violence.

With the outbreak of World War II in 1939, the Nazi regime began implementing their "Final Solution," a plan to exterminate all Jews in Europe. Jews were rounded up and transported to ghettos and concentration camps, where they were subjected to forced labor, medical experimentation, and mass murder.

The Holocaust came to an end in 1945 with the defeat of Nazi Germany by Allied forces. The discovery of the death camps and the extent of the atrocities committed by the Nazis shocked the world.

The Holocaust is considered one of the most controversial issues in Judaism and in world history. It raised questions about the nature of evil, the role of humanity in preventing genocide, and the importance of remembrance and education in preventing such atrocities from happening again.

21. What are the advantages of learning the controversies in philippine history


Answer:

There are several advantages to learning about the controversies in Philippine history. Some of these advantages include:

1. Critical thinking skills: Studying controversies in Philippine history requires critical thinking skills to examine and analyze different perspectives, interpretations, and evidence. It encourages students to question and evaluate historical events, sources, and narratives, helping them develop their critical thinking skills, which are valuable in various aspects of life.

2. Historical awareness: Understanding controversies in Philippine history provides a more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of the country's past. It allows students to appreciate the complexity and diversity of historical events, characters, and contexts, going beyond simplistic or one-sided views of history. It promotes historical awareness and a deeper appreciation of the country's heritage and identity.

3. Empathy and perspective-taking: Learning about controversies in Philippine history exposes students to different viewpoints, ideologies, and interests. It helps them develop empathy and perspective-taking skills by understanding the motivations, beliefs, and experiences of different historical actors. This can foster tolerance, open-mindedness, and an appreciation for diversity in society.

4. Civic engagement: Studying controversies in Philippine history can inspire civic engagement and active citizenship. It encourages students to be critical and informed members of society who are aware of their rights and responsibilities. It can motivate them to participate in social, cultural, and political issues, and to make informed decisions as engaged citizens.

5. Historical lessons for the present: Controversies in Philippine history often have contemporary relevance. By examining historical controversies, students can draw connections to current issues and challenges in society, such as social inequality, human rights, governance, and national identity. It provides an opportunity to learn from history and apply those lessons to the present, fostering critical thinking and active engagement in current affairs.

23. why RA 10354 become controversial in the philippines?​

The Philippine Congress enacted Republic Act No. 10354 on Responsible Parenthood and Reproductive Health on December 18, 2012, after decades of what can only be described as “bitter public controversy and political wrangling.” Three days later, it was signed into law by the President of the Philippines. 1 Commonly known as the Reproductive Health (RH) Law, its Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR) were due to come into effect on Easter Sunday, March 31, 2013. However, just 10 days before that, the Supreme Court of the Philippines issued a status quo ante (or restraining) order against the RH Law for 120 days,2 during which period it would review the petitions challenging the new law itself; oral arguments before the Supreme Court had been set to begin on June 18, 2013, or six months since the enactment by Congress.

This paper gives an outline of the elements of the new RH Law, and closes with a revisit of the many arguments and counter-arguments made for and against the Bill then, and the Law now.

· Refusing to provide reproductive health care services to an abused minor and/or an abused pregnant minor, whose condition is certified to by an authorized DSWD official or personnel, even without parental consent particularly when the parent concerned is the perpetrator;

The Warsaw Treaty Organization (WTO), officially the Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation, and Mutual Assistance, and colloquially known as the Warsaw Pact (WP), was a Cold War-era collective defense treaty signed in Warsaw, Poland in May 1955 by the Soviet Union and seven other Eastern Bloc socialist republics of Central and Eastern Europe. The Warsaw Pact was the military counterpart to the Council for Mutual Economic Assistance (CoMEcon), the regional economic organization for Central and Eastern European socialist states. The Warsaw Pact was formed in response to West Germany's accession to NATO in 1955, as a result of the 1954 London and Paris Conferences.

Why is it critical to discuss contentious matters in class?

While it may be tempting for schools to avoid discussing such topics, doing so is neither desirable nor feasible.

Students from all backgrounds and lifestyles can learn to live and work together in a peaceful and polite manner by discussing contentious themes. It encourages them to pay attention to one another and sensitively discuss their differences. It also encourages people to reflect critically on their own views and ideas, as well as provide them with the confidence and abilities to do so publicly.

How to deal with contentious situations. Consider how you will present a balanced view of the problems when dealing with contentious matters to avoid prejudice.
