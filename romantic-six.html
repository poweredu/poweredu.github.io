17. compare and contrast the charactereristics of romantic music to romantic art​


1. Emphasis on emotion: Both romantic music and art placed a strong emphasis on emotions, but music was better suited for conveying intense emotional experiences. In contrast, romantic art often depicted more subtle emotions, using visual cues and symbolism to evoke complex feelings.

2. Individualism: Both romantic music and art celebrated individualism and the unique experiences of the artist. However, in music, this often manifested as a focus on the composer's personal life and experiences, while in art, it was more about expressing the individual perspective of the artist.

3. Nature and the past: Both forms of art and music glorified nature and the past, but in different ways. Romantic art often depicted the beauty of natural landscapes and the sublime power of nature, while romantic music often used nature as a metaphor for human emotion. Similarly, romantic art often looked to the past for inspiration, while romantic music often looked to the music of the past for inspiration.

4. Structure and form: Romantic music and art both experimented with structure and form, but in different ways. In music, romantic composers often pushed the boundaries of traditional musical structures, using unconventional forms and structures to express their ideas. In contrast, romantic art often used traditional techniques and forms, but with a new emphasis on individual expression.

5. Symbolism and metaphor: Both romantic music and art used symbolism and metaphor, but in different ways. In art, symbolism was often used to evoke complex emotions and ideas, while in music, metaphor was often used to express emotions and ideas that were difficult to put into words.

19. what is the difference between the romantic opera and our romantic today​


Answer:

When people talk about “Classical” music, they usually mean Western art music of any time period. But the Classical period was actually a very short era, basically the second half of the eighteenth century. Only two Classical-period composers are widely known: Mozart and Haydn.

The Romantic era produced many more composers whose names and music are still familiar and popular today: Brahms, Tchaikovsky, Schumann, Schubert, Chopin, and Wagner are perhaps the most well-known, but there are plenty of others who may also be familiar, including Strauss, Verdi, Liszt, Mendelssohn, Puccini, and Mahler. Ludwig van Beethoven, possibly the most famous composer of all, is harder to place. His early works are from the Classical period and are clearly Classical in style. But his later music, including the majority of his most famous music, is just as clearly Romantic.

The term Romantic covers most of the music (and art and literature) of Western civilization from the nineteenth century (the 1800’s). But there has been plenty of music written in the Romantic style in the twentieth century (including many popular movie scores), and music isn’t considered Romantic just because it was written in the nineteenth century. The beginning of that century found plenty of composers (Rossini, for example) who were still writing Classical-sounding music. And by the end of the century, composers were turning away from Romanticism and searching for new idioms, including post-Romanticism, Impressionism, and early experiments in Modern music.

Explanation:

Background, Development, and Influence

Classical Roots

Sometimes a new style of music happens when composers forcefully reject the old style. Early Classical composers, for example, were determined to get away from what they considered the excesses of the Baroque style. Modern composers also were consciously trying to invent something new and very different.

But the composers of the Romantic era did not reject Classical music. In fact, they were consciously emulating the composers they considered to be the great classicists: Haydn, Mozart, and particularly Beethoven. They continued to write symphonies, concertos, sonatas, and operas, forms that were all popular with classical composers. They also kept the basic rules for these forms, as well as keeping the rules of rhythm, melody, harmony, harmonic progression, tuning, and performance practice that were established in (or before) the Classical period.

The main difference between Classical and Romantic music came from attitudes towards these “rules”. In the eighteenth century, composers were primarily interested in forms, melodies, and harmonies that provided an easily-audible structure for the music. In the first movement of a sonata, for example, each prescribed section would likely be where it belonged, the appropriate length, and in the proper key. In the nineteenth century, the “rules” that provided this structure were more likely to be seen as boundaries and limits that needed to be explored, tested, and even defied. For example, the first movement of a Romantic sonata may contain all the expected sections as the music develops, but the composer might feel free to expand or contract some sections or to add unexpected interruptions between them. The harmonies in the movement might lead away from and back to the tonic just as expected, but they might wander much further afield than a Classical sonata would, before they make their final return.


20. what you learned about • Music of the Romantic Period • Violin and Strings Music of Romantic Period • Piano Music of Romantic Music • Program Music​


Answer:

Music of the Romantic Period:

The Romantic Period in music roughly spanned from the late 18th century to the early 20th century, and it was characterized by a focus on individual expression, emotion, and freedom. Romantic composers often sought to convey intense personal feelings through their music, and they often used rich harmonies, complex melodies, and varied textures to achieve this. Some notable Romantic composers include Ludwig van Beethoven, Franz Schubert, Frederic Chopin, Franz Liszt, Johannes Brahms, Richard Wagner, and Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky.

Violin and Strings Music of Romantic Period:

The Romantic Period saw a great deal of innovation in violin and string music. Composers such as Niccolò Paganini and Pablo de Sarasate pushed the boundaries of what was thought possible on the violin, with virtuosic displays of technique and expression. String quartets also became increasingly popular, with composers such as Felix Mendelssohn and Antonín Dvořák writing works that emphasized the interplay between the four instruments. In general, the music of the Romantic Period placed a greater emphasis on expression and emotion than on technical precision, which allowed for a greater degree of freedom and experimentation.

Piano Music of Romantic Music:

The Romantic Period was a golden age for piano music, with a wide range of composers writing music for the instrument. Some of the most famous composers of Romantic piano music include Chopin, Liszt, Robert Schumann, Johannes Brahms, and Franz Schubert. The piano music of this period often featured complex harmonies, virtuosic displays of technique, and an emphasis on personal expression and emotion. Many composers also explored new forms and structures, such as the character piece, which was a short, evocative work that captured a particular mood or emotion.

Program Music:

Program music is a type of music that tells a story or conveys a particular mood or emotion, often through the use of descriptive titles or programmatic elements. This type of music became particularly popular during the Romantic Period, with composers such as Hector Berlioz, Richard Strauss, and Bedřich Smetana writing works that had a narrative or programmatic element. For example, Berlioz's "Symphonie fantastique" tells the story of an artist who falls in love with a woman and then experiences a series of increasingly wild and vivid hallucinations, while Smetana's "The Moldau" is a musical depiction of the river that flows through the composer's native Bohemia. Program music was a way for composers to explore and express their own personal experiences and emotions in a more concrete and explicit way than was possible with purely instrumental music.

1. At its core, composers of the Romantic Era saw music as a means of individual and emotional expression. Indeed, they considered music the art form most capable of expressing the full range of human emotion. As a result, romantic composers broadened the scope of emotional content. Music was expected to communicate to the audience, often by using a narrative form that told distinct stories.

Romantic composers prioritized the emotional or narrative content of the music above its form, which is why they broke so many of the classical composers' rules. Romantic composers didn't reject or break with the musical language developed during the Classical Period. They used its forms as a foundation for their work but felt unconstrained by them.

Beethoven is the originator of this approach. He lived and worked during the transition from the Classical to the Romantic Period, and was an inspiration to the Romantic composers who came after him.

Beethoven's symphonies "shift[ed] the terrain" for what a symphony could be. He also demonstrated coming Romantic Era characteristics, such as composing auto-biographical works and naming movements, such as the third movement of his String Quartet No. 15 in A minor, Op. 132 (Song of Thanksgiving to the Deity from a convalescent in the Lydian mode).
