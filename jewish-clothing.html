12. In 1938, Kristallnacht was the night when Jewish people were freed from Nazi concentration camps. Jewish communities organized protests against the Nazi Party. Nazi mobs attacked Jewish businesses, homes, and schools. Nazis gathered Jewish people and forced them to move to ghettos.


Answer:

In 1938, Kristallnacht, also known as the "Night of Broken Glass," was a night when Nazi mobs in Germany and Austria attacked Jewish businesses, homes, and synagogues. This violent pogrom resulted in the destruction of Jewish property, the arrest and imprisonment of thousands of Jews, and the death of at least 91 Jews. The name "Kristallnacht" refers to the broken glass that littered the streets from the shattered windows of Jewish-owned businesses, homes, and synagogues. The event marked a significant turning point in Nazi persecution of Jews, as it signaled the escalation of violence towards them and the beginning of the Holocaust.

14. Which statement best summarizes the effects of the Nuremburg Laws? The laws created ways for Jewish people to attend school. The laws took away the rights of Jewish people in Germany. The laws allowed Jewish people to become German citizens. The laws declared the start of the Holocaust against Jewish people.


Answer:

The statement that best summarizes the effects of the Nuremberg Laws is: "The laws took away the rights of Jewish people in Germany." The Nuremberg Laws, enacted in 1935, were a series of anti-Semitic laws that stripped Jewish people of their civil and human rights, including their citizenship, the right to vote, the right to own property, and the right to marry non-Jewish Germans. These laws paved the way for the persecution and eventual genocide of Jewish people in Nazi Germany.

Jewish literature includes works written by Jews on Jewish themes, literary works written in Jewish languages on various themes, and literary works in any language written by Jewish writers.[1] Ancient Jewish literature includes Biblical literature and rabbinic literature. Medieval Jewish literature includes not only rabbinic literature but also ethical literature, philosophical literature, mystical literature, various other forms of prose including history and fiction, and various forms of poetry of both religious and secular varieties.[1] The production of Jewish literature has flowered with the modern emergence of secular Jewish culture. Judaism (Hebrew: יהדות‎, Yahadut; originally from Hebrew יהודה, Yehudah, "Judah", via Greek Ἰουδαϊσμός Ioudaismos[6][7][8]; the term itself is of Anglo-Latin origin c. 1400[9]) is an Abrahamic primarily ethnic religion comprising the collective religious, cultural, and legal tradition and civilization of the Jewish people.[10][1][11] Judaism is considered by religious Jews to be the expression of the covenant that God established with the Children of Israel.[12] It encompasses a wide body of texts, practices, theological positions, and forms of organization. The Torah is part of the larger text known as the Tanakh or the Hebrew Bible, and supplemental oral tradition represented by later texts such as the Midrash and the Talmud. With between 14.5 and 17.4 million adherents worldwide,[13] Judaism is the tenth largest religion in the world.
