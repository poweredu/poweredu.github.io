To illustrate that segment MIO is congruent to segment CIO, we will draw a diagram.

First, draw a straight line that intersects at points M, I, and C. Then, draw a line segment from point I that bisects the angle between segments MI and CI. Label the intersection point of this line segment and MC as point O.

[Insert image: https://i.imgur.com/9Jv6evW.png]

We know that segment MI is congruent to segment CI. This means that MICO is a kite, since MI and CI are two pairs of adjacent, congruent sides. Additionally, angle M and angle C are congruent, because they are vertical angles.

Since segment IO bisects angle MIC, we know that angle MIO and angle CIO are congruent. This is because the two angles are adjacent, and their shared side is bisected by segment IO.

So, we now have triangle MIO and triangle CIO, which share a common side (IO), and two congruent angles (MIO and CIO). This is enough information to conclude that the two triangles are congruent by the Angle-Side-Angle (ASA) postulate.

Therefore, we can say that segment MIO is congruent to segment CIO.

Answer:

The Song of my Lord

The commonly used title El Cantar de mio Cid means literally The Song of my Lord or The Poem of my Lord. As the original title of the poem is lost to history, this one was suggested by historian Ramón Menéndez Pidal. It is Old Spanish (old Castilian), adjusted to modern orthography.
