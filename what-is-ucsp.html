24. what is characteristics of Law mores Ucsp subject​


Answer:

Characteristics of Law:

Formal: Law is a formal system of rules that is officially recognized and enforced by a government or other authority.

Written: Most laws are written down in official documents, such as constitutions, statutes, or codes.

Binding: Laws are binding on individuals and groups within the jurisdiction of the authority that enacted them.

Enforceable: Laws are enforced by government agencies, such as the police and the courts, with punishments for non-compliance.

Universal: Laws apply to all individuals and groups within the jurisdiction of the authority that enacted them.

Consistent: Laws are applied consistently, without discrimination or bias.

Evolving: Laws are subject to change and revision over time, reflecting changes in society and its values.

Characteristics of Mores:

Informal: Mores are unwritten social norms that are enforced by informal social sanctions, such as social disapproval or exclusion.

Moral: Mores are based on shared ethical values and beliefs within a society, and they reflect what is considered right and wrong behavior.

Persistent: Mores are deeply ingrained in society and can persist for generations, even when laws or formal institutions do not officially recognize them.

Varied: Mores can vary between different cultures and social groups, reflecting the diversity of moral values and beliefs across societies.

Influential: Mores can have a powerful influence on behavior, shaping social interactions and relationships within a society.

UCSP Subject:

UCSP stands for Understanding the Culture, Society, and Politics, a subject that is designed to help students understand the cultural, social, and political forces that shape contemporary society. Some of the characteristics of UCSP as a subject may include:

Interdisciplinary: UCSP draws on insights and perspectives from a range of disciplines, including sociology, anthropology, political science, and history.

Critical: UCSP encourages students to adopt a critical perspective on the social and political world, examining issues and problems from multiple angles and considering alternative viewpoints.

Contextual: UCSP emphasizes the importance of understanding the historical, cultural, and social context in which contemporary issues and problems arise.

Practical: UCSP aims to equip students with the knowledge and skills needed to navigate and engage with the complex social and political world, and to become informed and active citizens.

26. What you have learned about Ucsp500 words​


Answer:

In the academic social sciences, students are taught to think of culture as representing the customs and mores of a society, including its language, art, laws, and religion. Such a definition has a nice neutral sound to it, but culture is anything but neutral. Much of what is thought to be our common culture is the selective transmission of class-dominated values. Antonio Gramsci understood this when he spoke of class hegemony, noting that the state is only the “outer ditch behind which there [stands] a powerful system of fortresses and earthworks,” a network of cultural values and institutions not normally thought of as political.1 What we call “our culture” is largely reflective of existing hegemonic arrangements within the social order, strongly favoring some interests over others.

A society built upon slave labor, for instance, swiftly develops a racist culture, replete with its own peculiar laws, science, and mythology, along with mechanisms of repression directed against both slaves and the critics of slavery. After slavery is abolished, racism continues to fortify the inequitable social relations—which is what Engels meant when he said that slavery leaves its “poisonous sting” long after it passes into history.

Culture, then, is not an abstract force that floats around in space and settles upon us—though given the seemingly subliminal ways it influences us, it can feel like a disembodied, ubiquitous entity. In fact, culture is mediated through a social structure. We get our culture from a network of social relations involving other people: primary groups such as family, peers, and other informal associations within the community or, as is increasingly the case, from more formally articulated and legally chartered institutions such as schools, media, churches, government agencies, corporations, and the military.

Linked by purchase and persuasion to dominant ruling-class interests, such social institutions are regularly misrepresented as politically neutral, especially by those who occupy command positions within them or are otherwise advantaged by them. What Gramsci said about the military might apply to most other institutions in capitalist society: their “so-called neutrality only means support for the reactionary side.”2
