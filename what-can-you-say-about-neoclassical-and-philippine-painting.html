12. What can you say about Neoclassical and romantic sculptures?


Answer:

Neoclassical and Romantic sculptures are two prominent styles in the history of Western art. Neoclassical sculpture emerged in the 18th century as a reaction to the overly decorative and ornate style of the Baroque era. It was characterized by a return to classical Greek and Roman art, with an emphasis on clean lines, symmetry, and idealized forms. Neoclassical sculptures often depicted heroic figures or historical events, and were intended to convey a sense of rationality and order.

In contrast, Romantic sculpture emerged in the late 18th century as a reaction to the Enlightenment and Neoclassicism. Romanticism emphasized emotion, passion, and individualism, and rejected the strict adherence to classical rules and forms. Romantic sculptures often depicted fantastical or supernatural subjects, and were characterized by their dramatic, dynamic compositions and attention to detail.

While Neoclassical and Romantic sculptures are different in their approach and aesthetic, they both have had a significant impact on the development of sculpture as an art form. Neoclassical sculptures, with their emphasis on idealized beauty and historical themes, have influenced many artists throughout the centuries, from the sculptors of the Renaissance to the present day. Romantic sculptures, on the other hand, with their focus on emotion and individual expression, paved the way for the more expressive and experimental styles of sculpture that emerged in the 20th century.

In summary, Neoclassical and Romantic sculptures are two distinct styles that have contributed to the rich history and diversity of sculpture.


13. what can you say about the arts of neoclassical and romantic period ​


Answer:

Neoclassical painting usually features a linear style (in which the outlines of objects are sharply defined, thanks to carefully controlled brushstrokes), whereas Romantic painters tended to favour a painterly style (in which freedom of colour takes precedence over sharply-defined forms; brushstrokes are less restrained, resulting in somewhat "messy" outlines). The painterly style often has visible brushstrokes, while the linear style features smooth areas of colour, in which no brushstrokes can be seen.
